Section 01

Company Profile

BIPROGY Inc. (Tokyo Stock Exchange: 8056) is a comprehensive technology company headquartered in Koto, Tokyo. Founded in 1958 as Nippon Remington Univac Kaisha, the company was known as Nihon Unisys for over three decades before rebranding to BIPROGY in April 2022. With roots extending to 1947, the company has operated at the center of Japan's enterprise technology ecosystem for nearly eight decades.

Historical Milestones

  • 1971: Listed on the Tokyo Stock Exchange, First Section
  • 1988: Nihon Unisys established through merger of Nippon Univac and Burroughs Corporation operations

BankVision core banking platform. SurFIN foreign remittance processing (ISO 20022 compliant, SWIFT certified). Branch systems, CRM, risk management, AML compliance solutions.

Digital Transformation

AI deployment services, IoT platform solutions, blockchain ecosystems, RPA and automation. ERP implementation (SAP, Oracle). Agile development and service design consulting.

Section 03

Market Analysis: Japanese Enterprise IT in the US

The US market for enterprise IT services exceeds $450 billion annually. Japanese technology companies have established meaningful footholds, led by NTT Data, Fujitsu, and Hitachi, but significant white space remains for companies with deep vertical expertise in financial services, utilities, and critical infrastructure.

Market Dynamics Favoring BIPROGY's Entry

Demand Side Tailwinds

  • US financial institutions accelerating ISO 20022 migration for cross-border payments (SWIFT deadline compliance)
  • Utility companies modernizing grid infrastructure for renewable energy integration
  • Critical infrastructure operators under regulatory pressure to upgrade legacy systems
  • Growing demand for "mission critical" IT partners with proven reliability track records

Supply Side Advantages

  • Japanese IT companies known for precision, reliability, and long-term partnership commitment
  • BIPROGY's 60+ year track record with Japan's largest banks and utilities is a differentiator
  • Price competitiveness vs. US consulting firms (Accenture, Deloitte, IBM) for comparable quality
  • Growing US appreciation for Japanese engineering discipline and operational excellence

Japanese Company US Market Entry: Patterns and Lessons

The most successful Japanese IT companies in the US have followed a consistent playbook. NTT Data achieved scale through aggressive acquisition (Sierra Systems, Cognosante, Flux7, Acorio). Fujitsu built sector-specific offerings under the "Uvance" brand. Hitachi Vantara carved out a niche in data infrastructure. The common thread: each found a wedge, a specific capability that US buyers could not easily source from domestic providers, and expanded from there.

BIPROGY's wedge is clear. Its financial services technology (BankVision, SurFIN) and critical infrastructure expertise represent capabilities refined over decades in one of the world's most demanding regulatory environments. These are not commoditized offerings. They are battle-tested systems that US financial institutions and utilities will pay a premium to evaluate.

Partnership and Channel Strategy

Direct sales alone will not generate the pipeline velocity BIPROGY needs in its first two years. A structured partnership strategy across four categories will accelerate market entry, provide credibility, and create deal flow that a standalone sales team cannot match.

System Integrators

Partner with established US system integrators who already serve BIPROGY's target verticals. These firms have existing buyer relationships and can position BIPROGY's solutions within larger transformation programs.

  • Tier 1 Targets: Infosys, Wipro, TCS (familiar with Japanese technology partnerships)
  • Tier 2 Targets: Cognizant, DXC Technology, Capgemini (strong financial services practices)
  • Specialized: Jack Henry & Associates, Temenos (banking technology ecosystem partners)
  • Partnership Model: Referral agreements progressing to co-sell and OEM arrangements

Consulting Firms

Advisory firms influence large technology decisions at BIPROGY's target accounts. Building relationships with practice leaders ensures BIPROGY is included in shortlists and RFP responses.

  • Big Four: EY, PwC, KPMG (all have Japan practice desks serving US-based Japanese companies)
  • Boutique: McKinsey, BCG, Bain (technology advisory for C-suite transformation decisions)
  • Japan Specialists: Makana Partners, Pacific Bridge Partners (specialize in Japan-US business facilitation)
  • Partnership Model: Knowledge sharing, joint thought leadership, analyst briefings

Technology Alliances

Align with the platforms that BIPROGY's solutions run on and integrate with. These alliances create marketplace visibility and co-marketing opportunities.

  • Cloud Platforms: Microsoft Azure (BankVision is Windows-based), AWS, Google Cloud
  • Enterprise Software: Salesforce, ServiceNow, SAP (integration partners)
  • Financial Services: SWIFT (BIPROGY already has Complementor Certification), Plaid, Stripe
  • Partnership Model: ISV partnerships, marketplace listings, joint solution development

Industry Associations and Trade Organizations

Membership and active participation in industry bodies provides visibility, networking with buyers, and credibility in verticals where trust is earned slowly.

  • Financial Services: American Bankers Association (ABA), NACHA, Payments Association
  • Utilities: Edison Electric Institute, American Gas Association, NARUC
  • Japan-US Business: JETRO, Japan Business Association of Southern California, US-Japan Business Council
  • Technology: CompTIA, AFCEA, ISSA (security-focused associations)

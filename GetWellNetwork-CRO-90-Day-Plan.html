1. The System I Run

Every plan I build for every client and every employer runs on one integrated method.

Deep Discovery, five layers. Surface Symptoms become Stated Problems become Operational Impact become Economic Impact become Root Constraint and Unconsidered Need. You do not earn the right to claim a Power Message until you reach Layer 4. Most sellers stop at Layer 2 and then wonder why their forecasts miss. In health-system patient engagement, Layer 4 is where the CFO lives: HCAHPS-driven VBP exposure, readmission penalty variance, nursing labor cost per patient day, length-of-stay delta, and the ambulatory leakage rate that a digital front door either contains or leaks.

Power Messaging. A Power Message links a unique capability to a buyer-specific problem that can be defined in clear economic terms. It emerges from discovery, never from marketing. "Get Well has a better TV than pCare" is not a Power Message. "Your system's HCAHPS Communication with Nurses domain is one standard deviation below the regional median. On your annualized DRG base that translates to approximately $6.8 million in at-risk Hospital VBP redistribution in FY 2027. Get Well Inpatient paired with GetWell Rounds+ and Eureka-driven service recovery has produced documented nurse-communication lift in peer systems that closes that gap on the timeline of the CY 2027 measurement year" is a Power Message.

Two gates before I sell. Gate 1 is Active Evaluation. The buyer is actually in motion, with a stakeholder, a timeline, and a procurement path. Gate 2 is Quantified Severity. They can name the dollar cost of doing nothing. If either gate fails, I exit with discipline. I keep the relationship warm and move on. Most deals die because sellers refuse to accept that a buyer is not actually buying.

Mutual Sequence of Events. Co-created with the buyer. Includes procurement, legal, information security, HIPAA and HITRUST artifacts, EHR integration scoping with Epic or Cerner-Oracle or Meditech, clinical review, implementation, and go-live. The sequence ends beyond signature, at live room deployment or at first monthly patient-touch milestone, because that is when the buyer actually sees the outcome they bought. That is how we never get caught selling.

The Plan Letter. Sent within 24 hours of every substantive conversation. It restates value in the buyer's economic language, delivers every commitment made in the meeting, lays out the complete timeline to go-live, and asks the buyer to confirm in writing. If they write back yes, the deal belongs in the forecast. If they rewrite the sequence, that is better data and the deal belongs in the forecast with updated milestones. If they go silent, they are not in the forecast.

The forecast rule. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in the forecast. I learned this at UKG, where the culture enforced it without exception. My manager's line was "great job on the forecast. Show me the plan letter." That rule made forecasts defensible by construction, not by hope. It is what kept my number real year after year and what produced top-5-percent-globally ranking for nearly six years.

The forcing function. If I cannot write a good Plan Letter after a call, I ran a bad meeting. Period. Run better meetings. That discipline is how I coach sellers without sitting in every one of their meetings.

Every initiative below exists to make Plan Letters writable and forecasts accurate. That is the whole point.

2.3 HCAHPS and Hospital VBP tailwind

Three federal signals converge in the next 12 to 18 months:

Interactive patient care platforms are the workflow surface where the 4Ms get documented and measured. Get Well's inpatient product is procurement-ready.

Implication for the sell. Health system CFOs and CNOs are actively procuring against HCAHPS and VBP pressure right now. A disciplined Get Well pitch maps directly to at least three of their financial scorecards. This is Initiative 1.

4. The Three Problems I Solve at Get Well

Problem 1: Make health system ROI defensible at procurement

Health system buyers buy in two modes: clinical outcomes and financial outcomes. Every Get Well deal wins or loses on whether the buyer can walk into their own CFO or CNO committee with defensible numbers. Marketing produces case studies that show outcomes. Sales must translate those outcomes into buyer-specific economic numbers in every deal.

The economic anchors for Get Well are known:

  • HCAHPS score improvement. Domain-specific lift (Communication with Nurses, Responsiveness, Discharge Information, Care Transition), quantified against the buyer's baseline and translated into Hospital VBP redistribution dollars.
  • Readmission penalty avoidance. 30-day readmission reduction, translated into HRRP penalty dollars avoided.
  • Length-of-stay reduction. Day-level LOS delta multiplied by average cost per patient day, translated into direct margin improvement.
  • Nursing labor efficiency. Minutes per nurse per shift returned by digital rounding and bedside concierge, translated into labor cost savings or capacity redeployment.
  • Service recovery cost avoidance. Real-time alert on HCAHPS risk before the patient is discharged. Lost HCAHPS score is HVBP loss. Service recovery during stay is HVBP protection.
  • Ambulatory leakage containment. Patients returned to the system's own outpatient network instead of leaking to out-of-network specialists. Revenue preserved per prevented leak.
  • AI-driven clinician time return. RhythmX-driven minutes returned per clinician per shift, translated into capacity or labor cost.

You have case studies that prove most of these. The challenge is not knowing that these anchors exist. It is making every seller translate every prospect's specific situation into those numbers in every deal. That is the discipline I install. A framework is what I build in the first 90 days, Initiative 3.

Problem 2: Close the AI precision care channel gap inside the installed base

GW RhythmX AI is explicitly named in the CRO job description as a distinct channel alongside inpatient and ambulatory. "AI precision care" is the tell. There is either no dedicated expansion motion, no codified playbook for selling AI to the existing 150 Get Well health system CIOs, or no repeatable pricing architecture. A CRO hired to turn AI capability into durable, scalable revenue must stand up the AI precision care motion as its own dedicated expansion engine inside the installed base, with NRR above 115 percent as the accountability metric.

The structural differences:

  • Buyer: CIO and CMIO, alongside the CNO and CMO who own the inpatient relationship.
  • Procurement cycle: faster in installed base (4 to 7 months) than net-new (9 to 14 months), but requires dedicated motion to execute on that speed.
  • Contract structure: per-clinician or per-encounter PMPM in AI precision care, distinct from the per-bed model of interactive patient care.
  • Security and integration: new TPRM review cycle in each system even where Get Well is already approved. The CRO's job is to accelerate the TPRM path through structural partnership with the health system's InfoSec organization.

Each requires dedicated muscle. Initiative 2 builds it.

Problem 3: Convert category consolidation pressure into Get Well's enterprise tailwind

Category consolidation pressure is the largest single commercial opportunity in Get Well's history. Every health system CIO is told by every advisor to consolidate vendors. A disciplined CRO positioning treats Get Well plus RhythmX as the consolidator of interactive patient care, digital rounding, ambulatory engagement, service recovery, and the AI precision care layer. One contract replaces three-to-five.

The packaging question is not "what does Get Well cost." It is "what does Get Well replace." Initiative 2 and Initiative 3 together operationalize the answer.

5. ICP Deep Dive

5.1 Who Get Well is a fit for by channel

Enterprise health system (large)

Company profile. 10-plus hospitals, 3,000-plus staffed beds, $5B-plus net patient revenue. Integrated delivery network with population health contracts. Epic or Cerner-Oracle EHR. CIO with 20-plus FTE IT organization. CNO and CMO with enterprise authority. Existing HCAHPS pressure signal and measurable Hospital VBP exposure. Renewal cycle on an existing interactive patient care vendor in the next 18 to 24 months.

Why this band. This is where enterprise ACV economics and platform adoption align. Below 10 hospitals the buyer usually buys feature-first and price-first. Above 30 hospitals the deal is a custom strategic account motion. 10 to 30 is the band where Get Well's enterprise packaging closes on a predictable cycle.

Academic medical center

Company profile. University-affiliated system. Research, teaching, and clinical tripartite mission. Often Epic-dominant. CMIO with significant AI mandate. Distinct procurement complexity; distinct innovation budget; distinct capacity for RhythmX pilot-to-enterprise motion.

Children's and specialty health system

Company profile. Pediatric or specialty (orthopedic, cardiac, oncology) focused. Strong parent and family engagement requirement. High HCAHPS sensitivity. Distinct clinical workflow requirements.

Community and regional health system

Company profile. 3 to 10 hospitals, regional density. CFO close to HCAHPS and HVBP economics. Less IT capacity; prefer proven platforms with minimal integration lift.

Ambulatory-only provider networks

Company profile. Large outpatient clinic networks, FQHCs, specialty ambulatory chains. Distinct buyer; distinct competitive set (Artera, Luma). This is a defer-in-first-90-days segment unless anchored to an inpatient relationship.

Federal and government

Company profile. VA, DoD health, IHS, state psychiatric and correctional health. Distinct procurement (FedRAMP, ATO), distinct contracting path. Get Well has historical government presence through DoD. Validate the current state in Section 14.

5.3 Who buys at Get Well

Economic buyer. Depends on the product and the year. For inpatient platform: CIO with CNO sponsorship and CFO approval. For digital rounding and service recovery: CNO with CFO co-signature. For ambulatory: CMO or VP Ambulatory Operations with CIO approval. For AI precision care: CIO and CMIO, with CFO signature at enterprise ACV.

Technical champion. VP Patient Experience, Director Patient Engagement, CNIO, and Clinical Informatics leaders.

User. Patients, families, nurses, physicians, case managers. Patient satisfaction data and clinician workflow feedback are purchasing criteria. Users are not the buyer but they are the evidence base.

Blocker. IT and InfoSec (HITRUST, HIPAA, SOC 2, TPRM), procurement (vendor consolidation mandate), and the EHR team (Epic integration approval).

5.4 What triggers active evaluation

These are the real signals every BDR, AE, and marketing team should hunt for daily.

  1. New CIO, CNO, CMO, CMIO, or VP Patient Experience hire in the last 90 days (fresh eyes, budget authority).
  2. Public HCAHPS score decline or Hospital VBP redistribution loss in the last reporting period.
  3. 30-day readmission penalty imposed or elevated under HRRP.
  4. Joint Commission survey finding related to patient experience or care transition.
  5. Existing interactive patient care contract expiration within 12 months (identified via Get Well's competitive intelligence).
  6. Health system M&A creating two patient engagement stacks that must consolidate.
  7. New hospital build or major bed expansion (integration window).
  8. Public innovation announcement on AI, patient experience, or digital front door.
  9. CFO-signed cost-containment initiative with measurable patient-engagement or labor-efficiency targets.
  10. State or regional payer VBP contract award that raises patient-experience stakes for participating systems.

Trigger events are the opening. The product pitch is never the opener.

The California Health Plan wildfire reframe: live proof of this framework

The discipline above is not theoretical. The following is how I ran it live at a large California health plan with 600,000-plus members. The industry surface (payer messaging) is adjacent to Get Well's (hospital patient engagement), but the procurement pattern is identical: commoditized RFP, price-first procurement committee, multiple vendors competing on feature check-box.

The plan had a commoditized RFP for member-facing messaging platform: $200K fixed-fee procurement, procurement-led buying committee, four or five vendors competing on price. An AE running a feature-led motion would have competed on platform features, lost on price, and moved on.

I ran the five-layer discovery:

  • Layer 1, surface symptom: inefficient member messaging.
  • Layer 2, stated problem: low engagement on routine touchpoints.
  • Layer 3, operational impact: rising member-service call volume, missed care-gap outreach deadlines.
  • Layer 4, economic impact: unmeasured but material impact on STAR-rating scorecards and on medical-claims drift in unmanaged cohorts. No one had connected the messaging platform to the claims economics.
  • Layer 5, root constraint and unconsidered need: during active wildfire season, the plan had no centralized mechanism to maintain continuity of care for displaced members. Messaging platform was being bought to solve a cost problem. The real unconsidered need was a continuity-of-care platform that could pivot the entire plan's communications in 24 hours when a wildfire displaced a ZIP code.

I reframed the RFP from messaging platform to continuity-of-care platform. The wildfire scenario made the case visible to the CMO and Chief Actuary. Messaging was a feature. Continuity of care was the system. The RFP committee had to go back to executive leadership to re-scope.

The result: $200K commodity RFP became $1.4M ARR strategic platform. 7x expansion. The plan used the deal internally as the template for how they scope vendor procurement going forward.

Why this directly applies to Get Well. Get Well competes in a category where the default procurement motion is commodity interactive patient care: price-per-bed, feature-check-box, multi-vendor bake-off. The California Health Plan reframe is the exact muscle required to convert a "replace our pCare TV system" RFP into an "architect our continuum-of-care patient relationship graph with AI precision care on top" strategic buy. The industry is adjacent. The motion is the same. The outcome is a repeatable 3x-to-7x expansion of initial procurement scope, which is the single highest-leverage revenue lever available to the CRO.

1. The System I Run

Every plan I build for every client and every employer runs on one integrated method.

Deep Discovery (5 layers). Surface Symptoms become Stated Problems become Operational Impact become Economic Impact become Root Constraint and Unconsidered Need. You do not earn the right to claim a Power Message until you reach Layer 4. Most sellers stop at Layer 2 and then wonder why their forecasts miss.

Power Messaging. A Power Message links a unique capability to a buyer-specific problem that can be defined in clear economic terms. It emerges from discovery, never from marketing. "Cosmo is faster than Apollo" is not a Power Message. "Your principal engineer is spending 30% of her week triaging API-layer incidents that Cosmo's observability eliminates, which is $300K of attention we can redirect at product velocity this fiscal year" is a Power Message.

Two gates before I sell. Gate 1 is Active Evaluation. The buyer is actually in motion. Gate 2 is Quantified Severity. They can name the dollar cost of doing nothing. If either gate fails, I exit with discipline. I keep the relationship warm and move on. Most deals die because sellers refuse to accept that a buyer is not actually buying.

Mutual Sequence of Events. Co-created with the buyer. Includes procurement, legal, security review, SOC 2 artifacts, implementation, and go-live. The sequence ends beyond signature, at live production, because that is when the buyer actually sees the outcome they bought, and it ensures we never get caught selling.

The Plan Letter. Sent within 24 hours of every substantive conversation. It restates value in the buyer's economic language, delivers every commitment made in the meeting, lays out the complete timeline to go-live, and asks the buyer to confirm in writing. If they write back yes, the deal belongs in the forecast. If they rewrite the sequence, that is better data and the deal belongs in the forecast with updated milestones. If they go silent, they are not in the forecast.

The forecast rule. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in the forecast. I learned this at UKG, where the culture enforced it without exception. My manager's line was "great job on the forecast. Show me the plan letter." That rule made forecasts defensible by construction, not by hope. It is what kept my number real year after year.

The forcing function. If I cannot write a good Plan Letter after a call, I ran a bad meeting. Period. So, run better meetings. That discipline is how I coach sellers without sitting in every one of their meetings.

Every initiative below exists to make Plan Letters writable and forecasts accurate. That is the whole point.

This is a company that raised at peak 2021 multiples, cut 15% of staff a year later, lost its entire commercial bench in 2024, and is now running pricing experiments at a time when its core product is facing an open-source-positioned challenger. The renewal quarters ahead are the most structurally vulnerable this company has faced since 2021. That is the displacement window.

Apollo enterprise ACV (estimated): Public indicators (Vendr, user-reported contracts) put real Apollo enterprise deals in the $100K-$400K ACV band, with large consumer-scale platforms exceeding $1M.

The ELv2 license controversy - the displacement narrative:

In November 2021, Apollo moved Federation v2 core, Apollo Gateway 2.x, and Apollo Router Core to the Elastic License v2. ELv2 is not OSI-approved as open source. It imposes three restrictions that procurement teams must review and approve: no managed-service redistribution, no bypassing license keys, no removing notices. Drew DeVault's public post ("Breaking down Apollo Federation's anti-FOSS corporate gaslighting") and the Hacker News thread around the announcement triggered an organized push for a community-maintained fork.

The named public defection: Soundtrack Your Brand, an early Apollo GraphOS adopter, explicitly cited the license change as the trigger to migrate to Cosmo. They stood up Cosmo in one day. Fredrik Warnsberg (VP Eng) is on record.

The controversy is still contentious. Every open-source-committed procurement team continues to surface ELv2. Every serious CTO with an open-source thesis has to evaluate whether the legal restrictions are acceptable. That is why Cosmo's Apache 2.0 positioning converts.

Apollo renewal reevaluation windows. Apollo's Series D was August 2021. Assuming typical three-year enterprise contracts, the 2024-2025 cohort was the initial post-Series D wave at renewal. 2026-2027 is the second renewal cycle. These are the decision moments. Combined with the three departed commercial leaders, the 15% layoff, the new pricing model forcing re-papering, and the Federation v1 to v2 migration tax many customers are still carrying, the next four to six quarters are the most structurally vulnerable period Apollo has faced.

4. The Two Problems I Solve at WunderGraph

Problem 1: Making the problem economic for buyers who live in a technical world

Your buyers are VPs of Engineering, CTOs, and Heads of Platform Engineering. Many of them default to "we can build this ourselves." Most people who sell infrastructure lead with technical depth and lose on budget defense. Technical elegance is not a budget argument. A CFO does not approve elegance.

The economic anchors for Cosmo are known:

  • Engineering velocity. Weeks-to-ship per product team multiplied by cost per week of delayed revenue.
  • On-call burn. Incidents per quarter at the API layer multiplied by senior engineer hours multiplied by fully-loaded cost.
  • Apollo GraphOS displacement. Direct spend delta at renewal, documented with the public pricing anchors above.
  • DIY engineering cost avoided. Number of engineers multiplied by quarters multiplied by fully-loaded cost of an internal federation project that stalls. Baseline: $750K-$1.25M/year loaded.
  • Principal engineer retention. Replacement cost at 1.5x salary when API architecture is frustrating enough to cause attrition.
  • Time-to-market unlocked. Net new revenue from features shipping 30-60 days sooner.

You have case studies that prove most of these. Acoustic's $178K. SoundCloud's 86% infra reduction. Monkey Factory's 3x development velocity. Those are proof points. They are not yet a framework. The challenge is not knowing that these anchors exist. It is making every seller translate every prospect's specific situation into those numbers in every deal. That is the discipline I install. A framework is what I build in the first 90 days (Initiative 3).

Problem 2: Stop asking closers to hunt

The common error I see in growth-stage SaaS is that closers are told to generate their own pipeline. That is like asking a sharpshooter to dig in the mud for a stone to craft into an arrowhead. The closer should be pulling the bow and making the shot, over and over. We hire different people for different jobs.

Hunters land intros. Closers run full-cycle after the intro is on the calendar. Sales Engineers earn technical trust. Channel leaders multiply the human capacity without adding headcount cost. That is the specialization model I would install from day one. The lean phase team in Section 11 is built around it.

5.3 Who buys

Economic buyer. VP of Engineering, CTO, or Head of Platform Engineering. Not a business function. The buyer owns an engineering budget and has authority over architectural decisions that span multiple product teams.

Technical champion. Principal or staff engineer who owns API architecture. Usually the person who researched Cosmo on GitHub, ran the open-source version, and is internally advocating for the managed SaaS. I arm this person with materials they can use to win the internal budget fight.

User. Frontend and backend teams currently fighting over schema ownership. They are the ones who feel the pain every day. Their frustration is the initial demand signal.

Blocker. Security and compliance review (data exposure, SOC 2 artifacts, vendor risk). Procurement (open-source liability, vendor continuity, pricing structure).

5.4 What triggers active evaluation

This is the list every BDR, every AE, and marketing hunts for daily. These are real signals, not vanity metrics.

  1. New VP of Engineering or CTO hire in the last 90 days. Fresh eyes, budget authority, willingness to change.
  2. Recent API outage or security incident (public or on LinkedIn).
  3. Monolith-to-microservices migration in progress (job posts for platform engineer, staff engineer on API layer).
  4. Apollo GraphOS renewal window approaching (identified via contract length and public customer data).
  5. DIY federation project running 12-18 months with no shipped outcome (signal: key engineer leaves, LinkedIn).
  6. Acqui-merger where two engineering cultures must reconcile an API layer.
  7. Series B-D funding announcement with scale-of-engineering language in the press release.
  8. Public engineering blog post describing API pain.

Trigger events are the opening. The product pitch is never the opener.

Why most VP Sales candidates miss this. You have case study dollars already. Acoustic saved $178K. SoundCloud cut infra 86%. Monkey Factory reports 3x development velocity.   2. Quantify the value more precisely than current case studies capture. What is the true dollar impact? What business outcomes do they credit Cosmo with that are not in marketing copy?

Weeks 5-8: Framework design. Take the raw EBR data and structure it. Standard discovery questions every seller asks:

  • How many engineers do you have?
  • How many product teams consume the API layer?
  • What is your average time to ship a feature that requires schema changes?
  • How many API-layer incidents did you have last quarter? How many hours did they consume?
  • Are you currently on Apollo GraphOS? What is the contract value and renewal date?
  • Do you have a DIY federation project in flight? How many engineers? How long?

Standard economic translations:

  • Engineering velocity. Weeks saved per feature × revenue per week impact × features per quarter
  • On-call reduction. Incident hours × senior engineer fully-loaded cost
  • Apollo savings. Direct annual spend delta (documented with public pricing table)
  • DIY avoidance. Engineer count × quarters × fully-loaded cost (baseline $250K loaded)
  • Retention. Replacement cost for principal engineers at 1.5x salary
  • Time-to-market. Net new revenue from features shipping 30-60 days sooner

Standard outputs:

  • One-page business case PDF, customer-branded
  • Executive summary slide
  • ROI calculator tailored to their numbers

The OCR deal - a live proof of the framework

The discipline above is not theoretical. The following is how I ran it live at Ushur.

A national radiology company processing 5.5M doctor faxes per year came to us wanting OCR. At half a penny per page, that was a $27,500 / year commodity transaction. An AE running a feature-led motion would have closed it at that number and moved on.

I ran the five-layer discovery:

  • Layer 1 (surface symptom): manual fax processing.
  • Layer 2 (stated problem): 15 minutes per fax, 5.5M faxes per year, staff time.
  • Layer 3 (operational impact): delays in downstream scheduling. Manual error rate.
  • Layer 4 (economic impact): idle radiology machines when patients cancel or miss appointments. Idle technicians. Missed revenue from no-shows.
  • Layer 5 (root constraint, unconsidered need): revenue leakage no one was measuring, because no one was connecting the document-processing delay to patient engagement to machine utilization.

I mapped Ushur's unique capability: the automation could solve the OCR and feed the patient engagement layer downstream. Reduce fax-to-schedule time from 15 minutes to seconds. Drive automated outreach that reduced no-shows. Translate the operational improvement into ROI on the machines plus staff utilization plus no-show recovery.

The result: $1.6M ARR / $4.8M TCV. The $27K commodity became a strategic deal because the real problem was economic revenue leakage, not document processing. Ushur used this deal at Sales Kickoff to teach every rep in every vertical the reframe discipline.

That is what the Cosmo Economic Impact Framework will do at every qualified deal. The industry changes. The muscle does not.

Weeks 9-12: Deploy and enable. Train every AE. Use the framework in every new deal starting week 9. Measure win rate and ACV before and after. Iterate based on close-won and close-lost data.

You cannot do one well without the others.

  2. Cosmo Enterprise Growth should anchor $250K-$400K. Parity with Apollo Enterprise mid-band. Adds Streams, advanced observability, 24x7.

The NIH counter-frame. The most expensive Cosmo deal is always cheaper than the cheapest DIY. A 3-engineer build team loaded at $750K/year costs more than a $500K Cosmo Enterprise Platform deal before we count migration risk, compliance burden, and opportunity cost. The pricing conversation is not "Cosmo vs Apollo." It is "Cosmo vs your own engineering payroll."

Packaging principle. Every tier is rationalized by a specific buyer pain, not by feature-count gating. Starter serves the 250-750 engineer federation-forming org. Growth serves the 750-2000 engineer federation-committed org with expanding integration needs. Platform serves the 2000+ engineer Fortune 500 multi-BU environment. A buyer should be able to hear the tier name and know which one is theirs.

12. Forecast Discipline - Plan Letter as the Forecast Gate

I learned at UKG that a forecast is only as good as the evidence behind it. The culture was strict. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in the forecast. Short forecasts simply weren't tolerated. That produced forecasts that were defensible by construction, not by hope.

I would install this discipline at WunderGraph from week 1.

The rule. Every deal in the forecast must be backed by a Plan Letter sent to the buyer and confirmed in writing. The confirmation is captured in Salesforce as an attached email thread or Plan Letter artifact.

The weekly review. Thursday forecast calls start with the same question I heard at UKG. "Great job on the forecast. Show me the plan letter." If the seller cannot produce the letter and the buyer's written confirmation, the deal comes out of the commit. Full stop.

The coaching effect. When reps know the letter is the test, they run better meetings. They push discovery to Layer 4. They co-create the Sequence of Events. They do not accept "sounds great, we will let you know" as a meeting outcome. They ask for the confirmation in writing.

The cultural effect. Within one quarter, forecast accuracy tightens dramatically. I have seen this work at UKG across 200+ sellers. It will work at WunderGraph across 3-5.

The magic sentence I use to describe this to any executive audience: I run a system where every rep can write a good plan letter after every call. If they cannot, it means they didn't run a good meeting, and we know what to fix. If they can, the forecast is defensible because the buyer wrote back confirming the next steps. That is how you get forecast accuracy, not by yelling at the forecast.

13. Demo and Discovery Framework

13.1 Before the demo

The demo is not the first conversation. Discovery comes first. If we run a demo before Layer 4 of discovery is complete, we are selling to no one in particular. The demo becomes a product tour, and product tours do not close deals.

I would install a rule: no demo until the AE has quantified at least one economic pain in the prospect's own words. Without that, the demo is postponed.

13.2 The demo itself

  • Who attends. AE, SE, prospect's technical champion plus economic buyer.
  • What gets shown. We focus on features that address the specific pain quantified in discovery. Not a tour. If on-call burn: observability, tracing, breaking change detection. If Apollo migration: migration tooling and router performance comparison. If DIY avoidance: managed router, schema registry, enterprise support.
  • What the AE controls. The economic translation. SE handles technical depth. AE ties every feature back to the buyer's quantified pain in the buyer's language.
  • What happens after. Plan Letter within 24 hours. Always.

13.3 Post-demo Plan Letter structure

Every Plan Letter has five elements:

  1. Gratitude and excitement. Genuine. Names specific people. References the conversation.
  2. Re-messaged value in the buyer's language. Three Power Messages minimum.
  3. Every commitment delivered or assigned. Videos, documents, answered questions, outstanding items with owners named.
  4. Timeline to go-live, not just contract signature. Evaluation milestones, procurement, security review, implementation, go-live.
  5. Buyer confirmation ask. "Please confirm I have captured this correctly" or "Please let me know if I have missed anything."

When the buyer writes back yes, the deal belongs in the forecast. When the buyer rewrites the sequence, we have better data and the deal belongs in the forecast with updated milestones. When there is no response, we follow up. If still no response, the deal is not in the forecast.

# CANONICAL CASE: CALOPTIMA MEDICAID REDETERMINATION → ENTERPRISE EXPANSION

**Classification:** Canonical pattern. Land-and-expand converted into enterprise scale through multi-partner orchestration. Likely the same deal as the Ushur public-record "largest deal in company history" (~$10M TCV).

**Status:** v1.0 from Jeff's verbatim anonymized facts, 2026-04-22.

## Facts on record

- Client: large regional Medicaid health plan, approximately 600,000 to 700,000 members.
- Initial ask: Medicaid redetermination outreach support.
- Initial expected deal size under surface framing: approximately $360,000.
- Buyer's non-RFP procurement threshold: $250,000. Any engagement above triggers formal RFP, multi-month.
- Timing: end of COVID public health emergency. Members needed requalification for Medicaid or risked coverage loss.
- Consequences of delay: member coverage loss, plan enrollment decline, operational/financial impact.
- Initial land price: $249,000 (deliberate, just below RFP threshold).
- Enterprise expansion: buyer issued RFI, then enterprise RFP. Budget ~$3.8M/year.
- Partner orchestration: Predictive Analytics firm (originally a competing bidder) recruited as subcontractor. Rex Wallace Consulting added as strategic execution partner.
- Internal resistance: CRO, CEO, leadership pushed to narrow scope to messaging only (~$1M cap). Jeff rejected that direction.
- Procurement discipline: during RFP silent period, leadership pressured seller to send side-channel outreach. Jeff refused.
- Final outcome: ~$3M ARR, just under $10M total contract value, additional expansion, largest deal in company history.

## 1. PROBLEM DECOMPOSITION

**Stated initial problem:** Medicaid redetermination outreach at the end of the public health emergency. "Help us reach members to requalify."

**Urgent timing constraint:** end of PHE created a hard deadline for redetermination. Every unreached member at risk of coverage loss. Every lost member = lost premium revenue to the plan and lost care continuity for the member.

**Actual enterprise problem:** the redetermination use case was the visible tip. Underneath:
- Significant and ongoing decline in Star Ratings.
- Star Ratings directly tied to CMS reimbursement levels, with penalty structure exposure in the hundreds of millions of dollars.
- Ineffective member engagement across multiple touchpoints.
- No predictive analytics layer for identifying at-risk members.
- Underperformance on CAHPS and related quality surveys that feed Star Ratings.
- Organizational fragmentation across Clinical, Quality, Member Experience, IT, and Government Affairs departments, none of which were solving the problem as an integrated system.

**Financial consequences of inaction:**
- Immediate: lost members and premium revenue at the redetermination window.
- Ongoing: continued Star Rating decline compounding CMS reimbursement penalties.
- Systemic: enterprise-level financial exposure in the hundreds of millions.

**Separation of surface from systemic risk:**

| Surface framing | Systemic risk |
|---|---|
| Redetermination outreach | Enterprise Star Ratings decline + reimbursement exposure |
| ~$360K project | Hundreds of millions in cumulative CMS penalty exposure |
| Single department (Member Experience) | Cross-functional failure: Clinical, Quality, MemEx, IT, Gov Affairs |
| Messaging deliverable | Predictive analytics + engagement + execution system |
| Procurement-led purchase | C-suite and board-visible enterprise initiative |

## 2. DISCOVERY ANALYSIS

**What was originally requested:** messaging support for the redetermination push. A narrow, time-bound, transactional scope.

**What was uncovered through structured discovery:**

The seller walked the Five-Level Deep Discovery chain against the redetermination ask:

- Level 1 (Surface Symptom): "we need to reach members for redetermination."
- Level 2 (Stated Problem): "our member engagement tools are insufficient for the volume and speed required."
- Level 3 (Operational Impact): "our cross-department processes for identifying, reaching, and requalifying at-risk members are fragmented. This delays outreach and creates gaps."
- Level 4 (Economic Impact): "failed redetermination = direct premium revenue loss per uncontacted member, plus downstream Star Rating impact from member experience degradation."
- Level 5 (Root Constraint + Unconsidered Need): "the plan has no integrated member-intelligence + engagement + execution system. Redetermination is one symptom. Star Ratings decline is another. CAHPS underperformance is another. The root constraint is absence of an integrated enterprise engagement operating system tied to CMS reimbursement mechanics."

**Why this changed deal size and scope:**

At Level 5, the economic consequence is measured in hundreds of millions of CMS reimbursement exposure, not in cost per outreach. Budget authority shifts from a Member Experience director to the CFO, CMO, and board. The appropriate solution shifts from a messaging tool to an enterprise engagement operating system. The deal size shifts from ~$360K to multi-million ARR.

**Why shallow discovery would have produced a small deal:**

A seller who stopped at Level 2 would have responded to the stated messaging need at the $249K threshold, delivered successfully, and exited. The enterprise problem would have remained invisible to the buyer (because the buyer had not seen it as an enterprise problem), and the expansion conversation would not have been possible. Most sellers do exactly this. A deal size cap is what you inherit when discovery stops early.

## 3. VALUE WEDGE IDENTIFICATION

**What a commodity seller would have done:**

Quote the redetermination use case at the $249K threshold (or slightly below, competitively). Win the narrow engagement. Deliver. Renew at similar scope. Optimize unit economics on messaging throughput. Compete on price against comparable messaging vendors at next procurement.

**Why that leads to a capped deal size:**

- The conversation stays with Member Experience or procurement.
- The buyer's view of the seller is "messaging vendor."
- No path to Star Ratings conversation because it is a different stakeholder's budget.
- Competitors can undercut at next renewal.
- Every new use case requires new procurement cycles.

**The seller's differentiated position:**

Not a messaging vendor. Not a predictive analytics vendor. Not a consulting vendor. Instead: **enterprise engagement operating system integrator**. The position combines:
- Core platform (member messaging and engagement at scale).
- Predictive analytics (via subcontractor partner).
- Execution consulting (via Rex Wallace Consulting partner).
- Prime contractor status orchestrating the three.

**Why combining analytics, engagement, and execution created a new category:**

No competing vendor could deliver all three as an integrated offering. Pure messaging vendors lacked predictive analytics. Pure analytics firms lacked engagement execution. Pure consulting firms lacked platform capability. The combination, delivered through one prime contractor, addressed the Root Constraint (integrated enterprise engagement tied to CMS mechanics). Everyone else could only address a slice.

**Why this could not be compared on price:**

Because no competitor bid an equivalent scope. Comparing the unified prime-contractor bid to any single-vendor bid is not a price comparison. It is a scope comparison where the unified bid uniquely addresses the Root Constraint. Procurement cannot legitimately compare a complete enterprise operating system bid to a messaging-only bid at lower unit economics.

## 4. LAND-AND-EXPAND MECHANICS

**Why pricing at $249K mattered:**

The buyer had a formal $250K threshold. Any engagement above required a multi-month RFP. The PHE deadline did not permit multi-month waiting. Pricing at $249K meant:
- Fit under the buyer's own procurement rule.
- Immediate action authorized without RFP friction.
- Solve the urgent redetermination need.
- Establish operational presence inside the account.

**How this enabled immediate action:**

By staying below the threshold, the buyer's authorized signer could approve without procurement involvement. Time-to-contract compressed from months to days. The buyer's clock (end of PHE) and the seller's clock (land) aligned.

**How this created strategic positioning for the larger deal:**

- The seller was inside the account executing, not pitching.
- Performance on the redetermination scope became live proof for the enterprise bid.
- Relationships formed with stakeholders outside Member Experience (Quality, Clinical, Gov Affairs).
- The seller observed the plan's operational reality firsthand, informing the subsequent enterprise proposal's specificity.
- When the RFI came, the seller was the incumbent with operational evidence, not a cold bidder.

**Why this is not discounting but sequencing:**

Discounting is lowering price to win the same deal. Sequencing is using a smaller initial deal as the mechanism to win a much larger subsequent deal. The $249K was full-price for its scope. It was chosen to fit a procurement constraint, not to beat a price competitor.

## 5. TIMING AND URGENCY MODEL

**Why the end of the public health emergency created urgency:**

Under the PHE, automatic Medicaid continuous enrollment was in effect. When the PHE ended, every Medicaid member needed to be formally requalified within a bounded period or lose coverage. For a plan with 600-700K members, missing the window meant tens of thousands of lost members, direct premium revenue loss, and downstream Star Rating degradation from the experience.

**Why waiting for RFP would have caused failure:**

A multi-month RFP cycle (typical for government/Medicaid contracting) would have run past the redetermination window. By the time the RFP was awarded, the members would already be deactivated. The procurement rules did not permit the plan to wait, which is why the $249K threshold entry was not merely convenient but strategically necessary.

**How urgency was linked to financial and operational risk:**

- Every week of delay = measurable member coverage loss.
- Each lost member = premium revenue loss + Star Ratings member-experience impact.
- Star Ratings impact = CMS reimbursement exposure in the next rating cycle.
- The seller quantified this in the buyer's own numbers. The buyer could not tolerate the delay, which created the economic case for the threshold-entry structure.

**Urgency as a control mechanism:**

Urgency, when properly quantified, is the seller's ally. It compresses deliberation cycles and narrows the universe of viable competitors (those who can deliver inside the window). The seller who correctly maps urgency to financial exposure earns the speed premium.

## 6. PARTNER ORCHESTRATION MODEL

### How the seller identified missing capabilities

After walking discovery to Level 5 and seeing the Root Constraint, the seller inventoried:
- What the core platform could do well (messaging, engagement).
- What it could not do (predictive analytics at Stars/CAHPS depth; cross-functional operational execution).

The gap between capability inventory and Root Constraint revealed the capabilities that had to be brought in from elsewhere.

### Why the analytics firm was critical

Stars and CAHPS predictive analytics require specialized Medicaid/Medicare domain expertise, data science talent, and track record. The core platform did not have it. Without predictive analytics, the solution could not identify at-risk members before they churned, before they scored low on CAHPS, or before operational intervention was feasible. Predictive analytics was a non-negotiable system component for the enterprise problem.

### Why the consulting partner was necessary

The enterprise problem required coordination across Clinical, Quality, Member Experience, IT, and Government Affairs. This is organizational change work, not software deployment. Rex Wallace Consulting provided the domain expertise and execution capacity to align these departments into a single operating rhythm. Without this, the software would deploy but the operational improvement would not land.

### How a competitor was converted into a subcontractor

The predictive analytics firm had submitted its own independent bid into the RFP. The seller recognized that:
- The analytics firm could not win the full scope alone (missing engagement execution + consulting).
- The seller could not win the full scope alone (missing predictive analytics).
- Combined, they could win the full scope.

The seller approached the analytics firm with a partnering proposal: join this bid as a subcontractor, access the larger deal size and longer engagement, concede prime contractor status to the seller. The analytics firm accepted because winning a larger piece of a winning bid was better than losing an independent bid.

### How assembling partners expanded deal size

The combined bid:
- Addressed the full enterprise scope (Root Constraint).
- Could not be compared on price to any single-vendor bid.
- Commanded the budget appropriate to the scope (~$3.8M/year vs. $1M or less for narrow scope).
- Positioned the seller as prime contractor with control over the entire engagement.

Partner orchestration is a force multiplier. It is the mechanism that turns a single-vendor ceiling into a multi-vendor enterprise contract while keeping the selling seller as prime.

## 7. INTERNAL RESISTANCE MODEL

### Why leadership pushed for a smaller deal

The seller's own CRO, CEO, and leadership recommended narrowing the bid to the messaging component only (~$1M ceiling). Their reasoning was likely:
- Smaller deals are easier to win.
- Smaller scope has less execution risk.
- The core platform is proven; the integration is unproven.
- Partner coordination adds complexity.
- Losing a smaller bid is less visible than losing a larger one.

### Why that instinct is common

Executives are paid to manage risk. A smaller deal is a lower-risk commitment. Enterprise bids with partner orchestration have multiple execution dependencies and can fail visibly. The natural organizational instinct is to shrink scope to the sure win.

### Why it is strategically incorrect in this case

Because the surface problem (redetermination, messaging) was bounded at ~$1M ceiling by the buyer's initial scope. Competitors could match that scope and compete on price. The seller who wins that deal earns a low-margin, non-expansion contract.

But the Root Constraint was measured in hundreds of millions of CMS exposure. The enterprise bid was the only bid that addressed the Root Constraint. No competitor had assembled equivalent scope. Winning the enterprise bid was therefore MORE LIKELY than winning a narrow bid, because narrow bids had many viable competitors and the enterprise bid had none.

Additionally, winning a narrow bid would have closed the expansion door. The buyer would have had its messaging need solved and would not have seen the Root Constraint as a solvable problem.

### How a seller should act when internal pressure conflicts with deal reality

1. **Refuse respectfully but firmly.** The seller has direct evidence from discovery that leadership does not have. The seller must stand on that evidence.
2. **Expose the asymmetric risk.** Narrow scope competes against many vendors at low margin. Enterprise scope competes alone at high margin. The probability-weighted expected value of the enterprise bid is higher.
3. **Take ownership.** The seller becomes the architect of the full solution and owns the outcome. Internal skeptics have less standing to pre-empt when the seller owns the architecture.
4. **Orchestrate the partners.** Partner commitments make the enterprise bid real to internal leadership. A theoretical expansion is easy to veto. A committed multi-partner bid with subcontracts in motion is not.
5. **Be willing to be wrong at scale.** If the enterprise bid fails, the seller absorbs the visibility. This is the cost of betting on the reframe. Sellers who are unwilling to own that cost stay in narrow scope forever.

## 8. PROCUREMENT DISCIPLINE MODEL

### Why formal procurement must be respected

Government and Medicaid contracting has legal and regulatory constraints on buyer-vendor communication during an active procurement. Violation can result in:
- Disqualification of the seller's bid.
- Audit findings against the buyer.
- Protest by competing vendors.
- Reputational damage that closes future opportunities.

### Why silence is normal

During a formal procurement, buyers legally cannot respond to side-channel outreach. Extended silence is not disengagement. It is compliance. A seller who interprets silence as disengagement and reacts with informal outreach is signaling both desperation and non-compliance.

### Why side-channel communication is dangerous

Side-channel communication (texting stakeholders, calling champions, extracting informal updates):
- Violates procurement rules.
- Puts the champion in legal jeopardy.
- Gives competitors grounds to protest.
- Signals to the buyer that the seller does not understand government contracting.
- Can result in outright disqualification of an otherwise winning bid.

### How discipline becomes a competitive advantage

Most sales organizations do not have the discipline to hold silence during a long RFP period. They pressure their sellers to "just check in," "send a note," "see if they need anything." This is how enterprise bids are lost.

A seller who holds discipline:
- Demonstrates government-contracting literacy to the buyer.
- Differentiates from competitors who do not.
- Earns the buyer's trust as a compliant partner for future expansions.
- Wins on the merits of the bid, not on relationship hustle.

### The principle (Jozu doctrine)

> Silence during formal procurement is normal and must not trigger undisciplined behavior.

## 9. DEAL CONTROL MECHANICS (ACROSS THE FULL ARC)

**Initial entry ($249K land):**
- Control: price-structured under the buyer's own procurement threshold. Avoids RFP. Wins on speed.
- Artifact: Plan Letter documenting the redetermination scope, success criteria, and the acknowledged-but-not-scoped larger enterprise problem as a future conversation.

**Discovery period (during the land engagement):**
- Control: live operational presence gives the seller firsthand observation of the plan's broader gaps. Seller surfaces insights back to the buyer, positioning as a strategic advisor rather than a tactical vendor.
- Artifact: Plan Letter updates after each cross-functional meeting. Sequence of Events being drafted informally with the buyer.

**RFI phase:**
- Control: the seller is no longer cold bidding. The seller is the incumbent with delivery evidence.
- Artifact: RFI response explicitly references the land engagement outcomes. Competitors do not have this proof.

**RFP phase:**
- Control: the seller has assembled the full-scope prime-contractor bid. Competitors bidding narrow scope are structurally disadvantaged.
- Artifact: RFP response showing integrated analytics + engagement + execution as a single deliverable system. Rex Wallace Consulting engagement scope. Analytics subcontract scope.

**Silent period:**
- Control: procurement discipline. No side-channel outreach. Signals compliance and patience.
- Artifact: zero. Controlled silence.

**Final negotiation:**
- Control: the seller's bid is the only bid that addresses the Root Constraint at full scope. Negotiation is on terms, not scope reduction, because no competitor can match the scope.
- Artifact: Mutual Sequence of Events for implementation phase (steps 6-9 of the Jozu 9-step Sequence).

## 10. FAILURE MODE COMPARISON

**If the seller had stayed in the initial use case:**
- Deal size: capped at ~$1M ceiling for messaging scope.
- Competition: multiple messaging vendors.
- Likely win probability: 20-40% on price.
- Relationship: procurement-to-vendor.
- Future expansion: unlikely. Door closes after initial engagement.

**If the seller had waited for full RFP before acting:**
- Deal outcome: redetermination window missed.
- Member loss: tens of thousands of members.
- Plan financial damage: direct premium revenue loss + Star Ratings impact.
- Relationship damage: plan views the seller as unable to move at the pace of the business.
- Deal outcome: highly likely to lose the subsequent RFP to a competitor who landed via another mechanism.

**If the seller had violated procurement rules:**
- Outcome: bid disqualification risk, protest risk, relationship destruction.
- Even if not disqualified, the buyer's confidence in the seller's judgment is damaged.
- Future enterprise work with this plan becomes unlikely.

**If the seller had not assembled partners:**
- Outcome: narrow scope bid only possible. Competitive against many messaging-only bidders.
- No structural differentiation. Back to price competition.
- Deal size: under $1M ceiling.

**Contrast with actual outcome:**

| Path | Deal size | Competitive pressure | Relationship level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Narrow scope (what leadership wanted) | ≤$1M | High, many vendors | Procurement |
| Waited for RFP | $0 (missed window) | N/A | Damaged |

## 11. SYSTEM EXTRACTION (Reusable Decision Framework)

### Trigger conditions (seller recognizes when to apply this pattern)

1. Buyer presents a narrow, urgent use case.
2. A procurement threshold creates friction between the buyer's urgency and formal process.
3. Discovery reveals a larger hidden enterprise problem with economic exposure far exceeding the narrow use case.
4. Internal leadership is likely to push for scope reduction.
5. A formal RFP cycle is probable.

### When all five are present, apply:

**Step A: Threshold-Constrained Entry.** Structure the initial engagement at or below the buyer's non-RFP procurement threshold. Not a discount. A sequencing decision that exchanges deal size now for strategic positioning later.

**Step B: Active Discovery During Land.** Use the live engagement period to observe cross-functional gaps, meet stakeholders outside the initial champion, and validate the Root Constraint against operational reality. Every cross-functional meeting produces a Plan Letter.

**Step C: Capability Gap Inventory.** Compare the full-scope problem against the core platform's capabilities. Identify what must come from partners.

**Step D: Partner Recruitment.** Identify the partners (including competitors who have submitted or will submit their own bids) and structure them as subcontractors under the seller's prime contractor status. Win by unifying, not by competing alone.

**Step E: Enterprise Bid Construction.** Build the bid at the scope of the Root Constraint, not the stated problem. The bid price follows the scope of the Root Constraint, not the buyer's initial budget expectation.

**Step F: Internal Pressure Resistance.** Expect leadership to push for scope reduction. Respond with: (a) the asymmetric risk argument, (b) the committed partner scope, (c) seller ownership of the architecture.

**Step G: Procurement Discipline.** During formal RFP silent periods, no side-channel outreach. Silence is compliance, not disengagement.

**Step H: Plan Letter Everywhere.** Every meeting across the full arc produces a Plan Letter. Every Plan Letter contains a validation question. Every forecasted deal has a buyer-validated Sequence of Events.

### Decision framework (condensed)

> When you encounter a narrow-but-urgent use case with a procurement threshold and a larger hidden enterprise problem: enter at threshold, use the land period for active discovery and stakeholder expansion, inventory capability gaps, recruit partners (including competitors) as subcontractors, build the enterprise bid at Root Constraint scope, resist internal pressure to narrow scope, maintain strict procurement discipline, and let the Plan Letters carry control across every phase.

## 12. INTEGRATION INTO JOZU ENTERPRISE REVENUE SYSTEM

**Value Wedge:** the combined prime-contractor scope (platform + analytics + consulting) was the Wedge. No competitor had assembled it. The Wedge produced the scope no competing bid could match.

**Deep Discovery (Five Levels):** the seller walked the 5-level framework to reach Root Constraint + Unconsidered Need. At Level 5, the economic consequence (CMS reimbursement exposure in hundreds of millions) revealed the enterprise deal size.

**Power Messaging (Why This / Why Now):**
- **Why This Solution:** constraint match (addresses integrated enterprise engagement operating system), differentiated capability (only prime contractor who assembled all three), supporting evidence (live delivery from the $249K land).
- **Why Act Now:** visible cost of inaction (redetermination window closing, Stars declining), active financial loss (premium revenue + CMS penalty), delay increasing risk (further Stars degradation).

**Buying Cycle Alignment (Two Gates):**
- **Gate One:** active evaluation or change cycle? YES, the PHE end forced it.
- **Gate Two:** problem severity quantified? YES, at Level 5 with CMS exposure numbers.

**Plan Letter:** used across initial engagement, RFI, and RFP phases to carry the reframe, the validation, and the Sequence.

**Mutual Sequence of Events (Nine Steps):**
- Evaluation Phase (1-5) executed during the land engagement and RFI: problem severity validated, success criteria agreed, technical demo aligned, stakeholder expansion (Clinical, Quality, Gov Affairs), security/compliance review.
- Execution Phase (6-9) executed during RFP and contract: commercial structuring, executive approval, contract execution, implementation plan.

**Infrastructure Pillars:** meeting objectives set per meeting (e.g., "secure Quality department alignment on Stars mechanism"). CRM discovery fields populated per stakeholder. Plan Letters after every meeting. Mutual Action Plan mandatory before RFP response.

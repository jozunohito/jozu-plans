**Wrong (flat enumeration):**
- "ICP definition, Power Messaging, discovery architecture, Plan Letter discipline, and Mutual Sequence of Events"

- "ICP definition, Power Messaging, structured Discovery, and Plan Letter discipline"

## 2. THE VALUE WEDGE

### Definition (from value-wedge-system repo)

The Value Wedge is the unique intersection where:
- Your organization's distinctive capabilities meet
- The prospect's **unconsidered need** and **economic pain**
- Your competition cannot effectively compete

This differentiated position emerges only through deep discovery that uncovers **root constraints** and **quantified economic impact**.

### Why most sellers fail to reach it

1. They lead with their product's most impressive feature, not the buyer's most expensive problem.
2. They accept surface pain without probing to economic consequence.
3. They do not earn the trust required for the buyer to disclose the critical problem.
4. They sell horizontally (many features) instead of vertically (one wedge, proven deep).

### Canonical quote (Jeff, verbatim from Value Wedge presentation)

> "Where most discovery stops, value wedge entry begins."

Purest teaching case. When the product is identical to the competitor's (same vendor, same SKUs, same capability), the Wedge cannot be the product. The Wedge is the seller's discipline. Jeff's narrative to fill.

## 3. DEEP DISCOVERY (FIVE LEVELS)

### Framework (verbatim levels from value-wedge-system repo)

Discovery operates on five escalating levels. A seller who stops before Level 5 loses the deal to commodity pricing.

1. **Surface Symptoms.** Stated complaints and feature gaps. What the buyer initially says on an intro call.
2. **Stated Problems.** Acknowledged issues and known gaps. The buyer's own framing of the pain.
3. **Operational Impact.** Process breakdown, cycle time effects, workarounds.
4. **Economic Impact.** Quantified financial loss, margin erosion, penalty exposure, opportunity cost.
5. **Root Constraint + Unconsidered Need.** The underlying structural constraint that, if resolved, produces outcomes the buyer had not yet articulated as a need. This is where the Value Wedge lives.

### Central discovery question

**"And Then What Happens?"**

Used repeatedly to walk the buyer from Level 1 through Level 5. Each answer triggers the next question. The progression ends at the Revenue Floor, where the economic buyer lives.

### Doctrinal anchor (Jeff, verbatim)

> "Discovery is not a phase of the sales process. It is the mechanism that determines the size of the deal."

### Why discovery gets skipped (Jeff's published list)

1. Mistaking activity for progress.
2. Fearing loss of buyer attention.
3. Lacking CRM enforcement that requires discovery completion before deal progression.

### Trust as a prerequisite

Buyers do not disclose Level 4 or 5 material to people they do not trust. Trust is earned through operator-level knowledge, honest disclosure of what the seller does and does not know, willingness to disqualify, and process discipline that protects the buyer from wasting political capital.

The Plan Letter is the strongest trust-earning signal a seller can give. It demonstrates the seller actually listened.

### Failure modes (detectable in real time)

- Deal competes on features or price → Discovery stopped before Level 5.
- Nobody has said "I would approve this budget" → Economic buyer not identified.
- Buyer cannot articulate why this must be done by a specific date → Reason for action not time-bound.
- Seller cannot write out procurement + legal + security review path → Decision process assumed, not verified.

## 4. POWER MESSAGING (Why This Solution / Why Now)

### Framework (verbatim from value-wedge-system repo)

Two conclusions derived from evidence, not positioning.

**Why This Solution** requires all three:
1. **Constraint match.** The solution addresses the mapped Root Constraint surfaced in Level 5 discovery. Not a feature match. A constraint match.
2. **Differentiated capability.** The capability is unique (Zone B in the Value Wedge construction) and cannot be replicated by competitors.
3. **Supporting evidence and proof.** Case studies, telemetry, quantified before/after. Not assertion.

**Why Act Now** requires all three:
1. **Visible cost of inaction.** The buyer can see what continuing without the solution costs, in their own numbers.
2. **Active financial loss occurring.** The loss is not theoretical or future. It is happening this quarter.
3. **Delay increasing risk or cost.** Every period of delay produces measurable additional exposure.

### Why this construction matters

Power Messaging is the artifact of evidence-based selling. It survives procurement because it is not a marketing assertion. It is a logical conclusion. Competing vendors lead with positioning. A Jozu seller leads with the buyer's own evidence organized into two unavoidable conclusions.

### Integration point

Power Messaging lives inside the Plan Letter (Section 6) and frames the buyer's business case. Power Messages also live in the system components on jozuconsultinggroup.com/founders as named system elements.

## 5. BUYING CYCLE ALIGNMENT (Two Gates)

### Framework (verbatim from value-wedge-system repo)

Professional sales requires validating two gates before forward motion.

**Gate One: Is the buyer in an active evaluation or change cycle?**

If no, the buyer has no mechanism to buy, regardless of how compelling the problem is. Best case outcome is a future-quarter opportunity, not a current-quarter deal.

**Gate Two: Is problem severity quantified to justify change?**

If no, the buyer has no economic case to present internally for budget allocation. Even with a change cycle, the deal will stall at finance or executive approval.

### Rule (v0.3, corrected 2026-04-22)

Without both gates met:

> "The correct action is not acceleration. It is widening."

If either gate fails, do not walk. Widen. Surface a larger use case, follow the economic pain downstream, and tie the solution to an existing forcing function inside the account. Contract renewals, regulatory deadlines, budget cycles, and board-level numbers are the most common forcing functions. The forcing function, not the original ask, is what creates Active Evaluation and Quantified Severity at a scope large enough to carry the deal.

**Canonical teaching case.** The large national radiology provider deal is the reference pattern. The $30K OCR ask by itself did not clear either gate. Jeff anchored the deal to the incumbent patient-engagement vendor's contract renewal date. The renewal was the forcing function. It created both gates simultaneously. The integrated revenue-optimization platform replaced the incumbent's majority-revenue contract. Outcome: $1.6M ARR, 53x the original ask. See `doctrine_canonical_case_us_radiology.md` Section 7a.

### Exit versus widen

Exit only when the account has no surfaceable forcing function, no adjacent use case, and no stakeholder willing to engage at the economic layer. In practice that condition is rare. Most "failed" deals are deals the seller did not widen. Widening is the default; exit is the exception.

### Why this is a control mechanism

Most sellers try to accelerate deals that have not passed both gates at the original scope. This is how pipelines get clogged with deals that never close. Jozu-disciplined sellers widen to the forcing function, which is why Jozu pipelines carry fewer but larger deals with dramatically higher committed conversion.

This is also why Jeff's individual-contributor performance at Ushur produced ~10× peer output: where peers stopped at a narrow ask, Jeff surfaced the forcing function and widened the scope to match.

## 6. PLAN LETTER SYSTEM

### Definition (Jeff, verbatim from Sales Process vs. Training essay)

> "Plan Letters as documentation. One-page mutual understanding documents summarizing the buyer's problem, economic impact, proposed solution, and expected outcomes create accountability artifacts for pipeline reviews."

### Why it matters

If a seller cannot write a Plan Letter after a meeting, the seller did not have a real meeting. Inability to produce a Plan Letter means:
- The critical business problem was not identified.
- The economic consequence was not quantified.
- The decision process was not clarified.
- The next step was not mutually agreed.

A failed Plan Letter is a red flag for the entire deal. It is also the only reliable leading indicator of forecast integrity at early pipeline stages.

### Required structure

1. **Gratitude.** Brief, sincere, specific.
2. **Summary of what was heard.** Buyer's situation, stated pain, reason for action, critical business problem. Buyer's language.
3. **Problems mapped to solutions.** Each problem paired with the Value Wedge capability that addresses it, tied to economic consequence.
4. **Validation question.** "Have I captured this correctly? Is anything missing or mischaracterized?"
5. **Sequence of Events (Section 7).**
6. **Confirmation of next steps.**

### Enforcement mechanics

- Control: the seller proposes the sequence. Buyers who engage are buying. Buyers who ignore are disqualifying themselves.
- Alignment: Plan Letter is the shared source of truth for every subsequent conversation. Drift is visible.
- Forecast integrity: a deal with a validated Plan Letter and confirmed Sequence is forecastable. A deal without one is not.

## 7. MUTUAL SEQUENCE OF EVENTS (Nine Steps)

### Framework (verbatim from value-wedge-system repo)

A co-created roadmap from discovery to implementation. Two phases, nine steps.

**Evaluation Phase:**
1. **Validation of Problem Severity.** Buyer confirms, in writing, the severity quantified in discovery.
2. **Agreement on Success Criteria.** Buyer and seller jointly define what success looks like post-implementation.
3. **Technical Demo Aligned to Use Case.** Demo is scoped to the buyer's validated use case, not a standard deck.
4. **Stakeholder Expansion.** Champion introduces additional stakeholders relevant to the decision.
5. **Security / Compliance Review.** Initiated early enough to not become a gate at the end.

**Execution Phase:**
6. **Commercial Structuring.** Pricing, terms, contract structure aligned to the capability model, not the unit-price model.
7. **Executive Approval.** Economic buyer confirms commitment.
8. **Contract Execution.** Legal and procurement close.
9. **Implementation Plan.** Already agreed and scheduled before contract signature.

### Doctrinal rule (Jeff, verbatim)

> "Structure follows severity. Never before."

Meaning: the Sequence only works if problem severity was quantified in discovery (Level 4 Economic Impact minimum) and validated by the buyer. Building the Sequence against an unquantified problem produces a wish list, not a commitment.

### Why mutual validation matters

A Sequence validated only by the seller is a wish list. A Sequence validated by the buyer is a commitment. The difference is the difference between pipeline and forecast.

### Why this beats traditional sales motion

Most sellers try to negotiate commercial terms before stakeholder expansion and before security review. They discover the blockers in the final 30 days, which is when deals slip a quarter. The Jozu Sequence frontloads procurement, legal, security, and stakeholder work into the Evaluation Phase, so the Execution Phase runs on rails.

## 8. SYSTEM SYNTHESIS (End-to-End Flow)

### Causal chain (verbatim framing from value-wedge-system repo)

Deep Discovery → Quantified Pain → Power Messaging → Buying Cycle Alignment → Mutual Sequence of Events → Predictable Win Probability

This is not a checklist. It is an integrated system where skipping steps destroys the logical foundation.

### Full execution flow

1. **Pre-call.** Situational discovery from public sources. Informed hypothesis about the buyer's likely critical business problem.
2. **First call.** Surface Symptoms (Level 1), Stated Problems (Level 2), probe into Operational Impact (Level 3). No demo. No pricing.
3. **Plan Letter 1.** Sent within 24 hours. Summary, problems-mapped-to-solutions, validation question, proposed Sequence.
4. **Second call.** Value Wedge validation. Buyer confirms or corrects Plan Letter. Discovery advances to Economic Impact (Level 4).
5. **Plan Letter 2.** Updated with corrections. Sequence of Events mutually validated in writing.
6. **Economic buyer meeting.** Root Constraint + Unconsidered Need (Level 5) presented. Economic buyer commits to decision date or reveals the real blocker.
7. **Buying Cycle Alignment check.** Both gates validated.
8. **Sequence execution.** Evaluation Phase steps 1-5 run in parallel per the Sequence. Plan Letter refreshed at each milestone.
9. **Execution Phase.** Steps 6-9 close the deal on or ahead of the committed date.

### Control points that enforce discipline

- Every meeting produces a Plan Letter. No exceptions. If it cannot be written, the meeting did not happen.
- Every Plan Letter contains a Validation Question.
- Every committed forecast deal has a buyer-validated Sequence of Events in writing.
- Every deal passes both Buying Cycle Gates before entering forecast.
- Every seller has weekly deal inspection against these artifacts.

### Feedback loop

Every closed deal produces new Value Wedge examples, new discovery patterns, and new Sequence templates that feed the next deal. The system compounds. This is why "Training fades. Process compounds" is mechanically true.

## 9. INFRASTRUCTURE PILLARS (Process vs. Training)

Jeff's published four pillars from the "Sales Process vs. Sales Training" essay:

1. **Meeting Objectives in Invitations.** Calendar invites specify concrete goals ("identify the economic sponsor"), not generic ("intro call").
2. **Required CRM Discovery Fields.** Mandatory fields gate deal progression: business problem, economic impact, organizational stakeholders, inaction consequences.
3. **Plan Letters as Documentation.** Artifact of every substantive meeting.
4. **Mutual Action Plans (Sequence of Events).** Strongest predictor of deal closure. Survives personnel transitions.

Diagnostic test: each missing element is revenue leakage where training is substituting for sustainable process.

> "In Japan, the relationship earns the conversation. In the U.S., the conversation earns the relationship."

1. **Nemawashi vs. Speed.** Japanese consensus-building conflicts with American buyers' expectations for rapid value articulation.
2. **Trust-building mechanisms.** American buyers prioritize demonstrated competence and problem understanding over long-term presence.

### Four service pillars (from services/japan-market-entry.html)

1. **U.S. Messaging Adaptation.** Translating value propositions from Japanese business context into language emphasizing specificity, ROI, and urgency.
2. **Pipeline Generation.** Building American sales pipelines from scratch through target account identification, outbound strategy, and meeting generation.
3. **Sales Process Design.** Creating enterprise sales processes aligned with American market requirements while maintaining operational discipline.
4. **Team Training.** Preparing sales teams for American enterprise dynamics including competitive procurement, multi-stakeholder selling, and negotiation practices.

### Success framework

- Reposition around economic outcomes.
- Accelerate sales cycles.
- Sequence relationship investment AFTER value validation.
- Use technical capabilities as proof, not primary messaging.

1. The System I Run

Every plan I build for every client and every employer runs on one integrated method. I call it the Jozu Enterprise Revenue System. It is my own synthesis built across 25 years of US enterprise sales. CustomerCentric Selling, where I have served as a coach since 2014, is a separate methodology that sharpened how I teach sales process. The Jozu system is mine.

Deep Discovery (5 levels). Surface Symptoms become Stated Problems become Operational Impact become Economic Impact become Root Constraint and Unconsidered Need. You do not earn the right to claim a Power Message until you reach Level 4. Most sellers stop at Level 2 and then wonder why their forecasts miss. In specialized-model AI infrastructure, Level 4 is where the CFO and the VP of Platform Engineering live: per-token cost at production scale, GPU utilization economics, model serving infrastructure spend, latency budget for the user-facing application, and the dollar cost of an inference outage on a customer-facing workflow.

Power Messaging. A Power Message links a unique capability to a buyer-specific problem defined in clear economic terms. It emerges from discovery, never from marketing. "Fastino is faster and cheaper" is not a Power Message. "Your customer support classification workflow runs 40 million inference calls per month at $0.0030 per call on GPT-4o-mini, which costs you $1.44M per year. Your p95 latency is 1.4 seconds, which adds 8 percentage points of agent abandonment in the IVR fallback path. Fastino's specialized classification model holds the same accuracy at $0.04 per million tokens and a p95 of 80 milliseconds. That removes $1.3M of annual inference spend and recovers approximately 6 points of agent containment, which on your contact volume is worth roughly $4.8M in deflected agent labor" is a Power Message.

Two gates before I sell. Gate 1 is Active Evaluation. The buyer is in motion, with a stakeholder, a timeline, and a procurement path. Gate 2 is Quantified Severity. They can name the dollar cost of doing nothing. If either gate fails, I do not walk. I widen. I surface a larger use case, follow the economic pain downstream, and tie the solution to an existing forcing function inside the account, a contract renewal, a regulatory deadline, a board-level number, a quarterly OKR. That is how a large national radiology provider became $1.6M ARR from a $30K OCR request in my book: the OCR engagement alone had no active evaluation, so I anchored the deal to the incumbent's contract renewal. The renewal was the forcing function. My solution replaced the incumbent's majority-revenue contract.

Mutual Sequence of Events (nine steps). Co-created with the buyer. Evaluation phase: problem severity validation, success criteria, demo aligned to use case, stakeholder expansion, security and compliance review. Execution phase: commercial structuring, executive approval, contract execution, implementation plan. Every step is validated in writing before the next one runs. This is the sequence that closes procurement, legal, security review, and AI governance committee deals at modern enterprise.

The Plan Letter. Sent within 24 hours of every substantive conversation. It restates value in the buyer's economic language, delivers every commitment made in the meeting, lays out the complete timeline to go-live, and asks the buyer to confirm in writing. If they write back yes, the deal belongs in the forecast. If they rewrite the sequence, that is better data and the deal belongs in the forecast with updated milestones. If they go silent, they are not in the forecast.

The forecast rule. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in the forecast. I learned this at UKG, where the culture enforced it without exception for 5.5 years. That rule made forecasts defensible by construction, not by hope. It is what produced top 5 percent global ranking at UKG and 10x peer outperformance during a senior IC run at Series A Ushur.

The forcing function. If I cannot write a good Plan Letter after a call, I ran a bad meeting. That discipline is how I coach sellers without sitting in every one of their meetings, and it is the discipline I will install at Fastino on day one, even when I am the only seller.

Every initiative below exists to make Plan Letters writable and forecasts accurate. That is the whole point.

2.3 Forcing functions (the timing is right now)

Five vectors converge in the next 12 to 24 months. Each is a discrete procurement-motion trigger that a disciplined founding seller can convert.

By Q3 2026, most CFOs are asking finance and platform engineering for an explicit per-workflow inference cost report.   • Data-residency and AI governance regulation. The EU AI Act high-risk system deadline (August 2026), state-level AI laws (Colorado SB 205, California AB 2013), and sector regulators (NYDFS, OCC, FFIEC, HHS OCR) are pushing model deployment in-region or in-VPC. Specialized models with on-prem or VPC footprints become the only compliant option for many workloads.
  • Hyperscaler marketplace commit-burn-down. Most enterprises now hold multi-year Azure, AWS, or GCP commitment dollars that they need to burn. A model vendor transactable through Azure Marketplace can be procured against existing commit, bypassing two quarters of legal negotiation. Fastino's Microsoft investor relationship makes Azure Marketplace a near-term reality.
  • Agentic-system production deployment. 2026 is the year the hype-stage agentic systems hit production. Production agentic systems run hundreds of inference calls per user task. Per-token economics matter at that volume. A specialized-model wedge inside an agentic stack is a high-leverage opening.

Implication for the sell. Every one of these is a "Why Now" trigger that a disciplined founding seller converts into a Plan Letter within one meeting. The Power Message is not "specialized models." 4. The Three Problems I Solve at Fastino

Problem 1: Convert specialized-model architecture into a Power Message every economic buyer can repeat

Fastino's product is technically differentiated. Most founding-stage AI infrastructure companies fail not on product but on translating the technical wedge into a Power Message that the CFO and the head of platform engineering both sign off on. The first job of the Founding Head of Sales is to build that translation layer.

The economic anchors for specialized-model adoption are known:

  • Per-task inference cost reduction. Frontier API spend per workflow versus Fastino specialized-model cost per workflow. Most narrow tasks cost 50x to 200x less on a specialized model.
  • Latency budget. Sub-200-millisecond p95 versus 800 to 2,000 millisecond p95 on frontier APIs. Translates to user-experience metrics: containment rate, conversion rate, abandonment rate.
  • Deployment-footprint TCO. On-prem or VPC versus API egress. Translates to GPU spend, MLOps team load, and data-residency compliance.
  • Time-to-production. Days versus months. Translates to opportunity cost of delayed feature launch.
  • Accuracy-per-dollar. Specialized-model accuracy on the narrow task versus frontier-model accuracy at 50x the cost.
  • Vendor concentration risk. Single-frontier-API dependency versus multi-vendor model stack with Fastino as the specialized layer.

You already have these numbers in your design partner conversations. The challenge is not knowing the anchors exist. It is making every founding seller, the founders included, translate every prospect's situation into those numbers in every conversation. That is the discipline I install in Initiative 1.

Problem 2: Build the founding pipeline engine as a two-gear motion (PLG plus enterprise top-down)

The posted JD calls for "pipeline-from-scratch" and "PLG sales experience." The structural reality at Fastino is that PLG and enterprise top-down are not alternatives. They are two gears of one motion.

  • Bottom-up gear: developers and AI engineers find Fastino through GitHub, Hacker News, AI engineer events, and self-serve API trial. They build a prototype in a week. The product proves the wedge inside the account before any commercial conversation.
  • Top-down gear: the Founding Head of Sales targets named enterprise accounts where the bottom-up signal is already present (developers using the API, the company's GitHub footprint indicating AI investment, public job postings for AI engineers). The top-down motion expands from a working prototype to a security review, a privacy review, a procurement review, and a multi-year contract.

Each gear feeds the other. The bottom-up gear generates the pipeline signal that the top-down gear acts on. The top-down gear converts the signal into committed dollars that fund the next round and the team build. Each requires distinct muscle. Initiative 2 builds both gears and the connection between them.

Problem 3: Turn the cap table and hyperscaler relationships into the founding-stage distribution layer

The JD specifies "channel strategy with cloud providers, hyperscalers, partnerships, and events." At a $25M seed company with Microsoft direct on the cap table and the GitHub and Docker CEOs as personal investors, the channel motion is not a Year Two priority. It is a Year One priority.

Canonical proof. At Ushur in 2023-2024, I led CalOptima Health (Orange County Health Authority), California's largest Medicaid managed-care plan, through a Medicaid redetermination procurement against a posted public-payer RFP. The initial land was a $249K transactional engagement. Through a disciplined application of threshold-entry (SOC 2 and HIPAA as door-openers, not the deal), procurement orchestration, partner integration (the systems integrator was already inside the account), and explicit navigation of internal resistance from procurement, legal, and the infosec committee, I walked the account from a $249K transactional buy to approximately $10M in total contract value over the program lifecycle. The pattern was: lead with the regulatory forcing function (Medicaid continuous-coverage unwinding), validate economic severity at Level 4 (member churn cost, STAR rating impact, call-center surge cost), co-author a Mutual Sequence of Events that scheduled procurement, legal, and infosec review in the evaluation phase, and arrive at commercial structuring with three stakeholders already aligned in writing. Procurement, legal, and infosec resistance was neutralized inside the sequence, not at the end. That is the exact dynamic Fastino enterprise sellers will face every day on regulated-vertical AI procurement. This initiative codifies the pattern for Fastino.

8. Pricing and Packaging Thesis

Fastino's pricing must reward production-volume adoption, reward workflow-attach across the same account, protect the bottom-up self-serve motion from feature commoditization, and align the enterprise package to value realization rather than seat or instance count.

8.1 Self-serve and PLG pricing

Self-serve pricing should anchor on per-million-token economics with a generous free tier (10M tokens per month) that converts a developer to a champion in the first week. Above the free tier, per-million-token pricing 30 to 50 percent below frontier-API pricing on equivalent narrow workflows. No per-seat charge inside self-serve. Per-seat is a friction tax on a developer-led motion.

8.2 Workflow-attach pricing

Most AI infrastructure pricing is per-call or per-token. That model breaks at enterprise because the buyer wants to deploy multiple specialized models across multiple workflows inside the same parent contract. The enterprise package should lead with a workflow-bundle price (e.g., classification + extraction + voice + agentic tool selection) and price additional workflows at declining marginal cost. This matches how the buyer actually constructs the production stack.

8.3 Deployment-footprint pricing

VPC and on-prem deployment must be priced as a footprint commitment, not a per-call multiplier. A flat platform commitment plus per-million-token usage above a baseline aligns Fastino's revenue to the customer's growing usage without penalizing the deployment posture that makes the deal possible in the first place.

8.4 Enterprise commercial construct

  • Multi-year term default. Two-year baseline with year-two uplift (5 to 7 percent). Multi-year accelerator for the AE.
  • Workflow-attach accelerator. Each additional production workflow inside the parent contract triggers a documented attach kicker.
  • Strategic-account custom construct. Global 2000 accounts get a custom enterprise license agreement with multi-entity, multi-workflow, and footprint-inclusive pricing under a negotiated ceiling.
  • Default discount ceilings. 10 percent single-year, 15 percent multi-year. Anything above requires CEO sign-off.

8.5 What this is not

  • Not a seat-based model. Seats commoditize the model layer and invite frontier-API undercut.
  • Not a free trial for enterprise. Free trials are a self-serve motion. Enterprise trials run through Proof-of-Value engagements with mutual success criteria in writing.
  • Not a benchmark price. Pricing must anchor on the buyer's economic anchor (per-call cost, latency budget, footprint TCO), not on competitor list price.

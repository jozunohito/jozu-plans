1. The System I Run

Every plan I build for every client and every employer runs on one integrated method. It is the Jozu Enterprise Revenue System. It is what converts a founder-led motion into a predictable engine without losing the founder energy that produced early traction.

Five-Level Discovery. Surface Symptoms become Stated Problems become Operational Impact become Economic Impact become Root Constraint and Unconsidered Need. You do not earn the right to claim a Power Message until you reach Level 4. Most sellers stop at Level 2 and then wonder why their forecasts miss. In the payer environment, Level 4 is where the chief actuary, the chief medical officer, and the chief financial officer live: Medical Loss Ratio rebate exposure, CMS star rating revenue impact, risk-adjustment documentation gaps tied to Medicare Advantage risk scores, Medicaid redetermination churn cost, prior-authorization leakage, avoidable-admission dollars per thousand members, HEDIS performance tied to rate-setting, and the quarterly board report that drives the CEO's own accountability.

Power Messaging. A Power Message links a unique capability to a buyer-specific problem defined in clear economic terms. It emerges from discovery, never from marketing. "Siftwell uses AI to identify at-risk members" is not a Power Message. "Your plan runs 480,000 Medicaid lives with a 3.4 percent annual avoidable-admission rate at a $14,200 average admission cost, which is roughly $232M of avoidable spend. Siftwell's intervention engine, validated against your own claims data in a 60-day proof, identifies the 8,000 members who drive 60 percent of that spend with sufficient lead time for your care managers to engage before the admission, targeting a Year One avoidable-admission reduction that translates to $25M to $45M of medical expense" is a Power Message. The difference is economics, attribution, and buyer-specific math.

Two Gates before I sell. Widen, not walk. Gate 1 is Active Evaluation: the buyer is in motion, with a stakeholder, a timeline, and a procurement path. Gate 2 is Quantified Severity: they can name the dollar cost of doing nothing. If either gate fails, I do not walk. I widen. I surface a larger use case, follow the economic pain downstream, and tie the solution to an existing forcing function inside the account. A contract renewal, a regulatory deadline, a state rate-setting cycle, a star-ratings threshold, a board-level number.

The canonical teaching example is a large national radiology provider who requested a $30,000 OCR proof-of-concept. The OCR engagement alone had no active evaluation and no quantified severity. I did not walk. I widened. I anchored the conversation to the incumbent revenue-cycle vendor's contract renewal, surfaced the economic impact of continued revenue leakage, and reframed the technical symptom into a revenue-infrastructure play. The engagement became $1.6M ARR with incumbent displacement. That is widen-not-walk. At Siftwell, the same discipline applies when a plan says "we are only looking at readmissions." Widen to total medical expense, risk adjustment, and star ratings. Follow the economics.

Mutual Sequence of Events. Co-created with the buyer. Evaluation phase: problem severity validation, success criteria, demo aligned to use case, stakeholder expansion, clinical and actuarial review, security and compliance review. Execution phase: commercial structuring, executive approval, contract execution, implementation plan. Every step is validated in writing before the next one runs. This is the sequence that closes procurement, legal, compliance, and clinical-committee-governed deals. It is how I closed the CalOptima Health (Orange County Health Authority) deal at $10M-plus TCV. It is how I closed the California health plan wildfire deal at $1.4M ARR against a commoditized RFP.

The Plan Letter. Sent within 24 hours of every substantive conversation. It restates value in the buyer's economic language, documents every commitment made in the meeting, lays out the complete timeline to go-live, and asks the buyer to confirm in writing. If they write back yes, the deal belongs in the forecast. If they rewrite the sequence, that is better data and the deal belongs in the forecast with updated milestones. If they go silent, they are not in the forecast.

The forecast rule. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in the forecast. I learned this at UKG, where the culture enforced it without exception. That rule made forecasts defensible by construction, not by hope. It is what produced my top 5 percent globally ranking for 5.5 years and the senior IC run at Series A Ushur delivering $24M TCV in under two years.

2.4 Regulatory and operational forcing functions (the timing is right now)

Every named account has a forcing function. The disciplined enterprise seller finds it and anchors the Plan Letter to it.

  • CMS Star Ratings 2026 and 2027. Star rating methodology changes have compressed MA plan margins materially. Every half-star movement is worth tens to hundreds of millions of dollars in quality bonus revenue at scale. Plans are actively investing in quality-gap closure and member-level intervention.
  • Medicaid redetermination. The end of the continuous eligibility provision drove roughly 25M disenrollments between 2023 and 2025. States and Medicaid MCOs now face churn-management, re-enrollment, and risk-adjustment resets. Every state Medicaid MCO has an active workstream.
  • CMS Medicare Advantage Risk Adjustment Model v28. Phased in across 2024, 2025, and 2026. Plans are actively recalibrating risk-score strategies and investing in documentation and intervention analytics.
  • Value-based care contract renewals. Most provider-plan shared-savings and capitation contracts reset on 3-year cycles. Analytics investments follow those renewals.
  • State rate-setting cycles. Medicaid capitation rates reset annually by state. Plans with underperforming MLR or HEDIS need analytics improvements before the next rate cycle.
  • MA plan-year cycle (AEP) and Marketplace open enrollment. Annual procurement windows aligned to bid cycles and operational readiness.
  • CMS Interoperability and Prior Authorization Final Rule. Payer-to-payer data exchange by January 2027 for MA, Medicaid, CHIP, and Marketplace plans. Forces data-pipeline investments that Siftwell can anchor against.
  • Health Equity Index. Replacing CMS reward factor starting 2027 star ratings. Plans with diverse Medicaid and dual-eligible populations need member-level stratification and intervention.

Implication for the sell. Each of these is a "Why Now" trigger that a disciplined seller converts into a Plan Letter within one or two meetings. The Power Message is not "we do AI for health plans." It is "your 2026 star ratings dropped a half-star, which at 1.1M MA lives is approximately $125M in annualized quality bonus revenue at risk. 2.5 The AI tailwind inside the payer category

Siftwell's AI positioning is not a novelty. It is in line with a structural shift in how payers think about analytics. Three specific shifts to underline:

  • From retrospective to prospective. The legacy analytics category (Cotiviti, HealthEdge, SAS internal builds) was optimized for what already happened: claims scrubbing, retrospective quality HEDIS pulls, DRG validation. The AI-native category is optimized for what is about to happen: which members are at risk, which providers are at risk, which contracts are at risk. The Power Message moves from accuracy to lead-time.
  • From report to action. Payers have been drowning in reports for a decade. Care managers and quality teams still work from spreadsheets. AI-native analytics close the report-to-action gap by surfacing the specific 50 to 500 members who need outreach this week. The operational lift is measurable. The economic lift is material.
  • From point tool to embedded agent. Early payer AI was point-solution (risk-adjustment coding, prior-authorization triage). The category is evolving toward embedded intervention-orchestration agents that coordinate care managers, outreach, and clinical workflow. Siftwell's positioning sits inside this shift.

Implication for the sell. Siftwell's sellers must carry the category-shift narrative, not just the product. Every first conversation establishes which side of the shift the plan is on: still buying reports or buying action. That framing becomes the Two Gates qualifier.

4. The Three Problems I Solve at Siftwell

Problem 1: Transition the sales function from founder-led to structured, predictable, and scalable

Founder-led motions at pre-Series A companies close the first 10 to 25 logos because the founders are the product, the pitch, and the Plan Letter rolled into one. That pattern breaks at 50 simultaneous opportunities across 30 different payer buying environments. The transition failure mode is well known: the founder stays in every deal, the new hires cannot close without the founder, the pipeline grows but conversion does not, and the forecast becomes unreliable just when the board starts underwriting the next round.

The fix is not to remove the founder from sales. The fix is to bottle the motion that the founder and the SVP of Growth are already running, formalize it as the operating system every new seller runs, and free the founder to appear in deals as the strategic sponsor rather than the de facto account executive. 5.3 Who buys at a health plan

Economic buyer. Chief medical officer or chief financial officer, depending on the primary use case. For star-ratings and quality, typically CMO. For MLR and total cost of care, typically CFO. For risk adjustment, often chief actuary with CFO alignment.

Technical champion. VP or director of care management, VP or director of population health, VP or director of quality, VP or director of risk adjustment. This is where the Power Message lands and where the Plan Letter originates.

User. Care managers, population-health analysts, quality analysts, risk-adjustment coders, medical-management nurses.

Blocker. Chief information officer and chief data officer on data-pipeline concerns. Chief compliance officer on PHI and HIPAA posture. Procurement on vendor consolidation and standard terms. Legal on BAA and DPA terms. Internal analytics team on build-versus-buy sentiment.

5.4 What triggers active evaluation

These are the signals every Siftwell BDR, AE, and marketing motion should hunt for daily.

  1. New chief medical officer, chief financial officer, chief actuary, chief operating officer, or VP of care management hired in the last 90 days at a Tier 1 or Tier 2 account.
  2. CMS star ratings slip of half a star or more in the most recent release, with MA enrollment above 200,000 lives.
  3. Public disclosure of MLR rebate exposure in the most recent 10-K, state insurance filing, or not-for-profit audited financial.
  4. State Medicaid redetermination churn rate above the state average, disclosed in the state Medicaid agency's quarterly report.
  5. State rate-setting cycle opening in the next 12 months for a Medicaid MCO operating at or above actuarial breakeven.
  6. Incumbent analytics vendor (Cotiviti, Clarify Health, Innovaccer, or legacy SAS/Teradata investment) on contract renewal or re-RFP.
  7. CMS Medicare Advantage Risk Adjustment Model v28 transition workstream named publicly in earnings or industry press.
  8. Health Equity Index readiness workstream disclosed in 2027-plan-year preparation.
  9. New provider-plan value-based-care contract executed or renewed, creating shared-savings-analytics investment.
  10. Public layoffs or restructuring impacting care-management, quality, or analytics FTE headcount (AI-assisted intervention is the replacement narrative).
  11. Consulting engagement referenced publicly (Chartis, Milliman, Deloitte, Oliver Wyman) on population health, star ratings, or risk-adjustment strategy.
  12. Board-level comments in recent investor calls, annual report, or public filings referencing care-management effectiveness, total cost of care, or analytics investment.

Trigger events are the opening. The product pitch is never the opener.

8. Pricing and Packaging Thesis

I would not change pricing in the first 90 days. I would instrument and listen. By day 90, I would have a defensible thesis. Early hypothesis below.

8.1 Likely current state

Pre-Series A payer-analytics companies typically sell a platform subscription tiered by lives-under-management, with professional services for implementation and data onboarding. ACV ranges from $150K to $1M depending on segment and lives covered. Terms are typically 12 to 36 months with annual escalators. Premium tiers include advanced intervention products, quality and star-ratings analytics, risk-adjustment modules, and SDOH.

8.2 Observations that inform the thesis

  • Lives-based pricing under-captures value in intervention analytics. A 500K-life plan that avoids 1,200 admissions at $14,000 each captures $16.8M in avoidable spend. Charging $400K on lives under management leaves value on the table. The path is outcome-linked or cost-avoidance-linked pricing for a portion of the deal.
  • Multi-year discipline improves forecast predictability. 3-year term deals with annual escalators and defined expansion triggers produce Series A and Series B-ready ARR metrics. Single-year deals hurt LTV and renewal economics.
  • Value-based pricing experiments are worth piloting with the right account. A handful of sophisticated plans are open to gain-share or at-risk pricing tied to specific outcome metrics. One or two lighthouse deals in Year One is the right pace. More would be premature.
  • Premium tier attach is an ACV lever. Moving customers from base tier to premium tier through documented ROI is a durable expansion motion. Target 25 to 40 percent ACV uplift at renewal on the expansion motion.

8.3 Packaging thesis (by day 90)

  • Maintain lives-based base tier as the default.
  • Introduce a premium intervention tier with documented outcome benchmarks tied to avoidable admissions, star ratings, or risk-adjustment capture.
  • Pilot one or two outcome-linked pricing constructs with lighthouse accounts in Year One.
  • Default to 3-year terms with 5 to 7 percent annual escalators.
  • Define a named expansion trigger set (additional line of business, additional region, additional use case) to make the expansion motion a repeatable commercial play.

9. Win/Loss Hypotheses

9.1 Likely win patterns

  • Anchored on a named forcing function. Star-ratings slip, MLR pressure, redetermination churn, rate-cycle timing, incumbent renewal. The buyer can articulate why now in economic terms.
  • Executive sponsor is the chief medical officer or chief actuary. Both carry the P&L lens and the clinical credibility to move the committee.
  • 60-day parallel proof against the plan's own data. Direct operator-level evidence replaces vendor claims.
  • Multi-stakeholder mapped inside the first three weeks. The deal never surprises procurement or compliance.
  • Siftwell's payer-operator DNA shows in discovery. Sellers speak CMO and actuary language, not vendor language.

9.2 Likely loss patterns

  • Founder-only relationship. Deal sits on founder air time, new hires cannot carry, procurement chokes the deal.
  • Procurement surprise. BAA, DPA, security review, or vendor consolidation mandate arrives late and kills velocity.
  • CIO or analytics team build-it-ourselves mandate. Plan's internal team positions as the cheaper option. Loss pattern unless the CMO or CFO overrides on speed-to-value.
  • Incumbent loyalty under contract. Cotiviti, Clarify, or homegrown system with sunk investment and political ownership.
  • Consultant-led evaluation that favors a breadth platform. Chartis or Deloitte recommends a CDP-style broad-platform vendor.

9.2.1 The internal-resistance pattern

A recurring loss pattern worth calling out explicitly: internal resistance inside the plan. The pattern looks like this. Economic buyer (CMO, CFO, actuary) sees the value and wants to move. A mid-level stakeholder (often analytics director, IT director, or an entrenched vendor-management owner) blocks, delays, or rewrites the requirements to favor a competitor or the incumbent. The deal appears to stall but is actually being slow-rolled by the blocker.

Playbook response.

  • Surface the blocker early in the Stakeholder Map. Name the internal dynamic openly with the executive sponsor.
  • Widen the stakeholder base so the blocker is not the only gate. Add a board member, a CFO's CFO, or an external consultant who validates the Power Message.
  • Tighten the Mutual Sequence of Events with dates the blocker has committed to. Missed dates become executive escalation triggers.
  • If the blocker is unwinnable, anchor on the next forcing function and revisit in 6 months with a larger economic case.

The CalOptima Health (Orange County Health Authority) deal surfaced two different blocker patterns during its 14-month cycle. Both were managed through stakeholder widening and executive sponsor re-engagement. The deal closed. The same discipline applies at Siftwell.

9.3 Playbook responses to loss patterns

  • Against founder-only risk: install the Stakeholder Map artifact as a required gate.
  • Against procurement surprise: frontload procurement and security in the Mutual Sequence of Events.
  • Against build-it-ourselves: lead with 60-day parallel proof and operator benchmarks.
  • Against incumbent loyalty: anchor on contract renewal and widen the economic aperture beyond the incumbent's scope.
  • Against consultant-led breadth: bring the actuary or CMO economic case directly, not through the consultant.

12.5 Forecast integrity discipline

Forecast integrity is the single most important artifact the CRO delivers in the first 90 days. Pre-Series A boards and lead investors price the next round against forecast accuracy. Sandbagging loses credibility. Overcommitting is worse.

The discipline runs on four rules.

  • Plan Letter confirmed, or not in the forecast. No exceptions. No "strong verbal." No "champion says it is done."
  • Mutual Sequence of Events signed before commit. The buyer has confirmed the procurement, legal, and executive-approval steps with named dates. If those dates shift, the deal shifts.
  • Coverage ratio target: 3x to 4x for committed quarter, 4x to 6x for upside quarter. Below that, the commit is unsupported.
  • Weekly forecast call. Every seller. No exceptions. Forecast is a team discipline, not an individual exercise.

This is the culture I ran at UKG for 5.5 years. It produced top 5 percent of global sellers. It is what I ran during the senior IC year at Series A Ushur. It is the architecture I install at Siftwell in Weeks 1 through 8.

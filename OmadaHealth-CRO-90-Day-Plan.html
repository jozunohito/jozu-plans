1. The System I Run

Every plan I build for every client and every employer runs on one integrated method.

Deep Discovery (5 layers). Surface Symptoms become Stated Problems become Operational Impact become Economic Impact become Root Constraint and Unconsidered Need. You do not earn the right to claim a Power Message until you reach Layer 4. Most sellers stop at Layer 2 and then wonder why their forecasts miss. In digital therapeutics, Layer 4 is where the buyer's CFO lives: total medical spend per member per month, attributable savings, productivity-adjusted absenteeism reduction, realized claims ratio shift by cohort.

Power Messaging. A Power Message links a unique capability to a buyer-specific problem that can be defined in clear economic terms. It emerges from discovery, never from marketing. "Omada has better outcomes than Livongo" is not a Power Message. "Your Tier-1 hypertension cohort is driving a $4.2 million annual claims variance against benchmark. Omada Insights for Hypertension, paired with clinically-validated behavior change protocols, has produced a documented A1C and systolic BP reduction in peer plans that we can translate into a claims-ratio delta specific to your book" is a Power Message.

Two gates before I sell. Gate 1 is Active Evaluation. The buyer is actually in motion, with a stakeholder, a timeline, and a procurement path. Gate 2 is Quantified Severity. They can name the dollar cost of doing nothing. If either gate fails, I do not walk. I widen. I surface a larger use case, follow the economic pain downstream, and tie the solution to an existing forcing function inside the account, a contract renewal, a regulatory deadline, a board-level number. That is how the large national radiology provider became $1.6M ARR from a $30K OCR request: the OCR engagement alone had no active evaluation, so I anchored the deal to the incumbent's contract renewal. The renewal was the forcing function. My solution replaced the incumbent's majority-revenue contract.

Mutual Sequence of Events. Co-created with the buyer. Includes procurement, legal, security review, HIPAA and HITRUST artifacts, clinical review where applicable, implementation, and go-live. The sequence ends beyond signature, at live enrollment and first monthly members-active milestone, because that is when the buyer actually sees the outcome they bought. That is how we never get caught selling.

The Plan Letter. Sent within 24 hours of every substantive conversation. It restates value in the buyer's economic language, delivers every commitment made in the meeting, lays out the complete timeline to go-live, and asks the buyer to confirm in writing. If they write back yes, the deal belongs in the forecast. If they rewrite the sequence, that is better data and the deal belongs in the forecast with updated milestones. If they go silent, they are not in the forecast.

The forecast rule. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in the forecast. I learned this at UKG, where the culture enforced it without exception. My manager's line was "great job on the forecast. Show me the plan letter." That rule made forecasts defensible by construction, not by hope. It is what kept my number real year after year and what produced top-5-percent-globally ranking for nearly six years.

The forcing function. If I cannot write a good Plan Letter after a call, I ran a bad meeting. Period. So, run better meetings. That discipline is how I coach sellers without sitting in every one of their meetings.

Every initiative below exists to make Plan Letters writable and forecasts accurate. That is the whole point.

2.2 The chronic disease slice (where Omada actually competes)

Digital therapeutics is an umbrella. Omada's addressable slice is specifically chronic disease behavioral change and adjacent weight and MSK care, delivered through employer, payer, health system, PBM, and Medicare channels.

  • US adults living with prediabetes: 97.6 million per CDC 2024 National Diabetes Statistics Report. Fewer than 15 percent are currently enrolled in any form of structured DPP. The unaddressed member pool is 80+ million.
  • US adults with hypertension: 119 million, per CDC. Fewer than half are controlled to guideline target.
  • US adults with type 2 diabetes: 37 million diagnosed, 8 million additional undiagnosed.
  • US adults eligible for GLP-1s for obesity indication: roughly 40 to 50 million by current BMI thresholds. The 2025-2026 wave of supply-constraint relief plus broadening payer coverage is producing double-digit monthly growth in addressable enrollment.
  • Musculoskeletal (MSK) annual US spend: $380 billion total direct cost per BMUS 2023 estimates. Digital-first MSK players (Hinge, Sword) have proven employer willingness to pay for programmatic alternatives.

Omada's addressable membership (my math):

  • Commercial covered lives in US employer-sponsored insurance: approximately 180 million.
  • Prevalence of at-risk chronic conditions across Omada's pillars (prediabetes, T2D, hypertension, obesity, MSK, behavioral health): conservatively 35 percent of covered lives carry at least one target condition.
  • That is 63 million addressable commercial covered lives before any Medicare, Medicaid, or DTC layer.
  • Omada current reach: 886,000 members at year-end 2025. That is 1.4 percent of the commercial addressable base alone.
  • A 5 percent ceiling produces 3.1 million members. At current average per-member economics, that is a multi-billion-dollar ARR envelope before Medicare, government, and international channels are added.

Implication for the sell. Omada is the category leader by customer count and revenue, and it has captured approximately one percent of its own addressable commercial market. This is not a mature category. It is an early-inning category with a proven P&L, newly-demonstrated operating leverage, and public-company discipline. The CRO's job is to build the machinery that captures the next 3 to 5 percent of addressable lives in a defensible, profitable, repeatable way.

2.3 Medicare and government tailwind

Three federal signals converge in the next 12 to 18 months:

  • CMS Contract Year 2026 final rule (published Federal Register April 15, 2025): policy and technical changes to Medicare Advantage Program, Medicare Part D, and PACE. Star ratings recalibration drove billions of additional dollars to MA plans in 2025. MA plans with pressure on chronic-condition quality measures are actively procurement-motion for vendors who can move their STARs.
  • CMS Contract Year 2027 and certain CY 2026 proposed changes (published Federal Register April 6, 2026): additional MA and Part D technical changes. This extends the procurement runway for MA vendor selection into late 2026.
  • Medicare DPP reimbursement: CMS reimburses CDC-recognized DPP providers for eligible beneficiaries. Omada is the nation's largest CDC-recognized DPP provider. Every MA plan that bears chronic-condition risk has an economic case to layer Omada on top of their existing medical management.

Implication for the sell. The MA channel is in active procurement motion right now. MA plans face simultaneous pressure on chronic-condition outcomes (STAR measures), on capitated margin (benchmark adjustments), and on member retention (plan shopping acceleration). A disciplined Omada pitch maps directly to at least three of their scoring metrics. This is Initiative 1.

2.4 GLP-1 as platform expansion, not as a fad

  • Omada members on GLP-1s: 50,000 (Dec 2024) to 150,000 (Dec 2025). 3x in one year.
  • Weight loss program revenue: +50%+ in 2025.
  • GLP-1 Flex Care launched late 2025 / early 2026. This is the employer-risk-sharing product that constrains pharmacy spend exposure while preserving access.
  • Industry context: Novo Nordisk and Eli Lilly 2025 reports show 10-15 million US adults on a GLP-1 for weight or diabetes; CMS under active review for Part D obesity coverage; employer direct contracting on GLP-1 is the fastest-moving pricing experiment in benefits in a decade.

Implication for the sell. GLP-1 Flex is a trojan horse for enterprise expansion in existing accounts and for net-new employer landings that would have been out of reach 18 months ago. Every employer HR benefits leader has a GLP-1 conversation pending. The CRO's job is to make sure Omada is the first meeting on their calendar. Initiative 2 operationalizes this.

2.5 Gaps and flags (to validate in the first 30 days)

  • No analyst firm has published a discrete TAM for "payer-contracted chronic behavior change" as a standalone category. I would construct the sizing as a percentage of total claims spend on target conditions, validated against Omada's own contracted payer ARR.
  • Medicare FFS beneficiary reimbursement rates for DPP have historically been lower than MA or commercial rates. The P&L impact of a government-channel push depends on the mix between FFS and MA. I would not commit a growth number without seeing the current MA contract economics in detail.
  • Omada's international opportunity (UK NHS, EU payers, Canada provinces, Japan Koseisho/insurers) is not in the current job description's channel list. I would not propose international expansion in the first 90 days. I would set up the long-run validation work in parallel, because Japan-to-US and US-to-Japan is a specialty of mine and Omada's methodology maps to at least three international payer systems.

3. Competitive Landscape

The competitive map determines where Omada wins without a fight, where Omada must win against a named competitor, and where Omada should walk away.

3.1 Teladoc / Livongo - the primary displaceable competitor

Financial and organizational profile:

  • Teladoc acquired Livongo for $18.5 billion in August 2020 at peak virtual-care multiples.
  • Teladoc stock has declined approximately 90 percent from its 2021 peak. Market cap has compressed from ~$40B to ~$2B range.
  • 2022-2024 multiple rounds of layoffs. C-suite turnover including CEO transition (Jason Gorevic departure announced April 2024).
  • Livongo-branded chronic care programs subsumed into Chronic Care Complete umbrella (Feb 2022). Member growth has trailed original Livongo trajectory.
  • 2025 Q4 results showed continued revenue pressure and margin compression. Pivoting investment toward BetterHelp and primary care, away from chronic care.

Competitive read. Teladoc is the largest current incumbent in Omada's accounts and also the most vulnerable. Every Livongo deal signed 2020-2022 is now 3 to 6 years into contract. Large payer and employer renewals are in active evaluation. Teladoc's own investment reprioritization toward BetterHelp and primary care signals weaker roadmap commitment to pure chronic. This is the displacement window. Every Omada seller should be able to name three specific Livongo pain points in every competitive deal: outcomes erosion post-integration, support degradation, and roadmap uncertainty.

The displacement narrative Omada already has: "Livongo was a great product. Livongo inside Teladoc is not the same product. Your members notice. Omada is the clinically-validated alternative with a single focused roadmap." That framing, used consistently across the sales org, produces compounding wins. The messaging needs codification into a seller-usable competitive battle card. Initiative 3 builds it.

3.2 Virta Health

  • Total funding ~$359M (PitchBook 2024). Last round Series E. Valuation ~$2B at peak.
  • Revenue estimated at $40-60M range (Latka, Sacra directional).
  • Distinctive clinical model: low-carbohydrate nutritional ketosis for type 2 diabetes reversal. Clinically differentiated from Omada's behavior-change approach.
  • Smaller employer footprint. Strong case study depth on diabetes reversal specifically.

Competitive read. Virta is narrow and clinically-differentiated. Omada wins where the buyer wants a platform that covers diabetes plus prediabetes plus hypertension plus behavioral health plus weight plus MSK. Virta wins where the buyer has been convinced by a clinical team that reversal-via-keto is the right approach. Handle Virta as a channel conflict in diabetes-only deals, not as a platform competitor. When Virta shows up, re-anchor the conversation to category breadth and cross-condition cohort economics, and the CFO will bring the buying committee back to Omada.

3.3 Noom

  • Private; raised ~$657M through Series F (2021, Silver Lake).
  • Shifted toward DTC weight plus GLP-1 access model after 2022 retrenchment. Employer enterprise motion is real but smaller than Omada's.
  • Public pricing $60/month DTC, enterprise custom. Weight and behavior only; no hypertension or chronic disease integration.

Competitive read. Noom is now primarily a DTC GLP-1 adjacent player. In enterprise, they compete only on weight. When Omada is selling a platform, Noom loses on condition breadth. When the buyer has a narrow weight mandate (HR benefits leader wants "something simple for weight"), Noom can win on price. Omada's defense is to move the conversation to GLP-1 Flex plus prediabetes prevention plus claims economics, where platform breadth compounds.

3.4 Lark

  • Private; raised ~$200M through Series D (2023).
  • AI-first model; lighter-touch than Omada's human-coach-plus-AI design.
  • Price-competitive in the mid-market employer band.

Competitive read. Lark competes most effectively on price in self-insured employers with under 2,000 lives. Omada's win framing is clinical outcomes depth, case study volume, and peer-validated payer contracts. In enterprise and payer deals, Lark is rarely the finalist. In mid-market, the Omada seller has to be ready with a "why not both, but Omada first" framework for employers genuinely split on approach.

3.5 Hinge Health (MSK)

  • Went public in 2025. Trading on NYSE as HNGE.
  • MSK-only focus. Proven employer adoption. Large case study library.
  • Public-market discipline has reset their pricing rigor.

Competitive read. Hinge is an MSK-specialist competitor with a stronger MSK-specific narrative than Omada. Omada's MSK offering is a pillar but not the primary marketing lead. In MSK-only deals, Hinge wins on specialization. In platform deals, Omada wins on category breadth. The CRO decision is whether to invest in deepening MSK parity with Hinge or to concede MSK-lead deals and anchor MSK as the fifth pillar inside platform wins. My working thesis is the latter: Hinge gets MSK-lead deals, Omada gets every deal where MSK is not the lead. Validate in Section 14.

3.6 Sword Health (MSK)

  • Valuation $3B (latest round 2024).
  • MSK plus pelvic health plus expansion into mental health.
  • Direct competition with Hinge.

Competitive read. Sword is structurally similar to Hinge. Same strategic call applies. Sword's pelvic health expansion is worth monitoring as a potential women's-health adjacency Omada could consider.

3.7 DTC GLP-1 players (Ro, Hers, WeightWatchers)

  • Ro, Hers, and WeightWatchers have built direct-to-consumer GLP-1 businesses, often at prices below employer-channel pricing.
  • They compete for the same end-consumer but rarely in the same procurement process.

Competitive read. DTC GLP-1 players are an indirect threat. They set consumer price expectations ("why would my employer pay X when I can get it myself for Y"). Omada's counter-frame is the clinical program wrapper: medication access alone is not care. The employer case is that wrapping medication in Omada's clinical program produces better adherence, better outcomes, and a better risk profile than raw medication dispensing. This is the narrative that converts HR benefits leaders who have been told by their CFO to "just let the DTC players handle it."

3.8 Employer point-solution fatigue (the real competitive headwind)

Every large employer HR benefits leader has a folder of underperforming point solutions from 2020-2023. Benefit consultants (Mercer, Aon, WTW, Lockton, Gallagher) have all counseled consolidation. Internal CFO pressure to simplify vendor management and realize ROI on existing contracts is at an all-time high.

Strategic read. This is simultaneously threat and opportunity. The threat: HR buyers are less willing to add a new vendor. The opportunity: HR buyers are actively looking to consolidate onto fewer, broader platforms. Omada's pitch in every procurement conversation must start with consolidation math: "You currently pay X point solutions for chronic, weight, behavioral, and MSK. Here is what a single Omada contract replaces." That framing, delivered by every seller in every deal, is the single highest-leverage narrative upgrade available. Initiative 3 codifies it.

4. The Three Problems I Solve at Omada

Problem 1: Make payer and employer ROI defensible at procurement

Payer and employer buyers buy in two modes: clinical outcomes and financial outcomes. Every Omada deal wins or loses on whether the buyer can walk into their own CFO or actuary with defensible numbers. Marketing produces case studies that show outcomes. Sales must translate those outcomes into buyer-specific economic numbers in every deal.

The economic anchors for Omada are known:

  • Medical cost trend reduction. PMPM reduction in the target condition cohort, documented against the buyer's own claims data.
  • Claims-ratio shift. Percentage points of improvement in MLR for the contracted population.
  • Productivity-adjusted absenteeism. Dollar-quantified reduction in lost productivity, using BMUS or NIOSH benchmarks as the starting point.
  • STAR-rating impact for MA plans. Scoring delta on HEDIS measures (HbA1c, blood pressure control, weight) that translate to CMS quality bonus.
  • Point-solution consolidation savings. Dollarized reduction in total vendor spend achieved by replacing multiple chronic, weight, or MSK vendors with Omada.
  • GLP-1 cost containment. PMPM GLP-1 spend reduction achieved through Flex Care's gated access and clinical wrapper.

You have case studies that prove most of these. The challenge is not knowing that these anchors exist. It is making every seller translate every prospect's specific situation into those numbers in every deal. That is the discipline I install. A framework is what I build in the first 90 days (Initiative 3).

Problem 2: Close the Medicare and government channel gap

Medicare Advantage and government channels are explicitly listed in the CRO job description as emerging channels. "Emerging" is the tell. There is either no dedicated team, no codified playbook, or no repeatable contracting path. A CRO hired to build a market architecture must stand up the Medicare and government motion as its own dedicated go-to-market within the larger commercial org, not as an add-on to the payer team.

The structural differences:

  • Procurement cycle: 9 to 18 months in MA, versus 4 to 9 months in commercial.
  • Buyer: Chief Medical Officer and Chief Actuary in MA plans, versus HR and CFO in employer, versus CMO and VP Medical Management in commercial health plans.
  • Contract structure: per-member-per-month (PMPM) or value-based arrangement in MA, versus fixed-fee or PMPM in commercial.
  • Regulatory surface: CMS reporting obligations, STAR impact, risk adjustment integration; distinct from commercial.

Each of these requires dedicated muscle. Initiative 1 builds it.

Problem 3: Convert GLP-1 Flex from pricing novelty into enterprise anchor

GLP-1 Flex Care is the fastest-growing product at Omada by a factor of three. It is also the product most vulnerable to being bought as a point buy rather than as the entry wedge for a broader Omada relationship. A disciplined CRO packaging strategy treats Flex as the anchor that brings a new employer or payer in, then expands in the next renewal to the full chronic and MSK platform.

The packaging question is not "what does Flex cost." It is "what does Flex unlock." Initiative 2 operationalizes the answer.

5. ICP Deep Dive

5.1 Who Omada is a fit for (by channel)

Employer (self-insured)

Company profile. 3,000 to 100,000 covered lives. Self-insured with stop-loss. HR benefits team with 3+ FTEs. At least one existing chronic or weight point solution under performance scrutiny. Employer (fully insured)

Company profile. 100 to 3,000 employees. Buying through their carrier (UnitedHealth, Aetna/CVS, Cigna, Anthem/Elevance, Blue plans). The sell is through the carrier, not direct to the employer.

Commercial health plan (payer)

Company profile. Regional and national health plans with at least 500,000 members. Commercial product lines (individual, small group, ASO). Chronic condition management team with at least a VP-level owner. Typically under pressure from employer-group customers on cost trend management.

Medicare Advantage plan

Company profile. MA plans with at least 100,000 MA lives. Chief Medical Officer and Chief Actuary own chronic condition economics. Under STAR ratings pressure. Contract year alignment to CMS fiscal cycle.

PBM

Company profile. CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx, Navitus, Prime Therapeutics, and mid-tier PBMs. GLP-1 utilization management is the current procurement-motion topic. Omada's Flex product is the procurement-ready answer.

Health system

Company profile. Integrated delivery networks with population-health contracts. Chronic condition management at enterprise scale. Typically operating under value-based arrangements with payers.

5.2 Channel prioritization in the first 90 days

Invest heavily: Medicare Advantage, PBM, self-insured employer (existing expansion).

Invest moderately: Commercial health plan (continue current motion), health system (targeted relationships).

Defer for 90 days, validate after: DTC (growth vs cost unclear without seeing CAC/LTV data), fully insured employer (downstream of payer motion), international.

5.3 Who buys at Omada

Economic buyer. Depends on channel. For employer self-insured: CHRO, CFO, or VP Benefits with CFO approval. For payer: CMO, Chief Actuary, or SVP Clinical Operations. For MA: CMO and Chief Actuary. For PBM: SVP Clinical and SVP Formulary.

Technical champion. VP Benefits (employer), VP Chronic Disease Management (payer), Medical Director DPP (MA), VP GLP-1 Category (PBM).

User. Members themselves are the end user. Member experience and engagement data are purchasing criteria, but members are not the buyer.

Blocker. Benefits consultants (if not aligned, they kill the deal), procurement (vendor consolidation mandate), IT and security (HIPAA, HITRUST, SOC 2).

5.4 What triggers active evaluation

These are the real signals every BDR, AE, and marketing team should hunt for daily.

  1. New CHRO, CMO, Chief Actuary, or VP Benefits hire in the last 90 days (fresh eyes, budget authority).
  2. Public earnings call reference to chronic condition cost pressure or MLR pressure.
  3. CMS star rating decline or MA plan contract award cycle.
  4. Benefit consultant RFP activity (visible through consultant-side disclosures or LinkedIn).
  5. Existing point-solution contract expiration within 6 months (identified through Omada's own competitive intelligence).
  6. Employer public announcement of cost-containment initiative or vendor consolidation.
  7. GLP-1 coverage announcement in a payer or employer population.
  8. Acquisition or merger creating two benefits programs that must consolidate.
  9. Workforce expansion past 3,000 or 10,000 covered lives (new procurement band).
  10. State-level Medicaid managed-care RFP activity.

Trigger events are the opening. The product pitch is never the opener.

7. First 90 Days: The Three Initiatives

Initiative 1: Medicare Advantage and Government Channel Penetration

Why this first. This is the single channel explicitly flagged in the CRO job description as "emerging." Standing up the Medicare motion is the most specific mandate in the posting, and the timing is favorable: CY 2026 and CY 2027 CMS rules are being finalized in real time, STAR rating pressure on MA plans is at a multi-year high, and Omada is already the largest CDC-recognized DPP provider. The infrastructure exists. The commercial architecture does not yet.

Initiative 2: GLP-1 Flex Care Enterprise Capture and Pricing Discipline

Why this matters. GLP-1 Flex Care is the fastest-growing product at Omada by a factor of three. It is also the most pricing-vulnerable because every employer and every PBM is anchored to GLP-1 medication costs rather than Omada program value. Without disciplined packaging, Flex becomes a commodity wrapper on top of a commodity medication. With disciplined packaging, Flex becomes the anchor for full-platform enterprise conversion.

Weeks 1-4: Audit. Interview every enterprise account owner, the CCO, and the CMO. Pull every Flex Care contract signed in the last 12 months. Map what tier each buyer signed, discount history, expansion pillars attached, current member adoption, renewal dates, auto-renewal language, claims and outcomes data captured.

Weeks 5-8: Design. Build the Flex Care Enterprise Packaging Framework with three tiers:

  • Flex Care Starter. Weight-only pillar. GLP-1 Flex gating, clinical wrapper, member engagement, outcomes reporting. Default landing product for employers with narrow weight mandate.
  • Flex Care Growth. Adds prediabetes prevention and diabetes management. Cross-condition cohort reporting. Claims-integration for payer clients. Mid-market payer standard.
  • Flex Care Platform. Full condition platform (prediabetes, diabetes, hypertension, weight, MSK, behavioral health) with enterprise claims integration, dedicated account management, custom outcomes reporting. Enterprise standard for 25K+ lives.

Each tier is rationalized by buyer pain, not by feature-count gating. Default discount ceilings: 10% single-year, 15% multi-year. Anything above requires CCO or CEO sign-off.

Weeks 9-12: Deploy and expand. Apply the framework to 20 to 30 existing enterprise customers as expansion conversations. Framing: "You landed on Flex Starter. Here is what we have built since you signed, and here is what it does for your specific claims and member profile. Worth 30 minutes?"

Initiative 3: Payer and Employer Economic Impact Framework (Through In-Person EBRs)

Why this matters. Omada has case study dollars already. Documented MLR reductions. HbA1c and blood pressure improvements. STAR-rating contributions. These are marketing assets today. They need to become a repeatable economic translation framework every seller uses in every deal.

Weeks 1-4: EBRs with marquee payer and employer customers. Schedule in-person EBRs with the 12 to 15 largest contracted customers, weighted toward payers. Two goals: deepen the relationship and quantify the value more precisely than current case studies capture. The EBR is discovery in disguise.

Weeks 5-8: Framework design. Standard discovery questions every seller asks:

  • What is your PMPM claims spend on diabetes, hypertension, weight, and MSK in the target cohort?
  • What is your current STAR-rating scorecard? What is the dollar impact of a 0.1-star delta?
  • How many point-solution chronic or weight vendors do you currently fund? What is your consolidated vendor spend?
  • What is your current GLP-1 PMPM exposure? Projected if coverage broadens?
  • What is your cost trend target for the contract period? What is your actual cost trend running?
  • What are your top-three retention-risk drivers?

Standard economic translations: MLR improvement, vendor consolidation savings, STAR quality bonus, GLP-1 cost containment, productivity-adjusted absenteeism, retention.

Standard outputs: one-page business case PDF (customer-branded), executive summary slide, ROI calculator tailored to their numbers, claims-integration data request list.

The California Health Plan wildfire reframe: live proof of this framework

The discipline above is not theoretical. The following is how I ran it live at a large California health plan.

The plan had a commoditized RFP for member-facing messaging platform: $200K fixed-fee procurement, procurement-led buying committee, four or five vendors competing on price. An AE running a feature-led motion would have competed on platform features, lost on price, and moved on.

I ran the five-layer discovery:

  • Layer 1 (surface symptom): inefficient member messaging.
  • Layer 2 (stated problem): low engagement on routine touchpoints.
  • Layer 3 (operational impact): rising member-service call volume, missed care-gap outreach deadlines.
  • Layer 4 (economic impact): unmeasured but material impact on STAR-rating scorecards and on medical-claims drift in unmanaged cohorts. No one had connected the messaging platform to the claims economics.
  • Layer 5 (root constraint, unconsidered need): during active wildfire season, the plan had no centralized mechanism to maintain continuity of care for displaced members. Messaging platform was being bought to solve a cost problem. The real unconsidered need was a continuity-of-care platform that could pivot the entire plan's communications in 24 hours when a wildfire displaced a ZIP code.

I reframed the RFP from messaging platform to continuity-of-care platform. The wildfire scenario made the case visible to the CMO and Chief Actuary. Messaging was a feature. Continuity of care was the system. The RFP committee had to go back to executive leadership to re-scope.

The result: $200K commodity RFP became $1.4M ARR strategic platform. 7x expansion. The plan used the deal internally as the template for how they scope vendor procurement going forward.

That is what the Omada Payer and Employer Economic Impact Framework will do at every qualified deal. The industry is the same. The muscle is the same. The outcome is a repeatable reframe from commodity point-solution to platform.

Weeks 9-12: Deploy and enable. Train every AE. Use the framework in every new deal starting week 9. Measure win rate and ACV before and after. Iterate.

Success metrics (90 days):

  • 12 to 15 EBRs completed with named executive sponsors
  • Framework drafted and tested in 8+ live deals
  • Quantified dollar impact added to 3+ existing case studies
  • 2+ net-new reference customers cultivated

13. Integration with AI in the GTM Model

The CRO job description explicitly calls for AI integration into Omada's commercial engine. My approach is disciplined, not performative.

13.1 Where AI compounds revenue

  • Trigger-event detection. Continuous monitoring of CMS disclosures, SEC filings, LinkedIn job changes, benefits consultant RFP feeds, press releases. Surface named trigger events to BDRs within 24 hours.
  • Pre-call research synthesis. Every AE gets a one-page research brief on each target account, auto-generated from public sources, validated by the AE. Saves 90 minutes per call.
  • Plan Letter drafting. AE provides the call summary; AI drafts the Plan Letter in the buyer's language using the standard structure. AE validates, edits, sends within 24 hours.
  • Claims-data analysis. Payer and MA deals require analysis of the buyer's claims data. AI accelerates the analysis, not the conclusion.
  • Forecast-integrity checking. Pattern-match open deals against closed-won and closed-lost patterns. Flag deals at risk of slipping early enough to intervene.

13.2 Where AI does not belong

  • Final Plan Letter approval. The seller owns the relationship; the seller owns the letter.
  • Pricing approval. Discount ceilings are human decisions with seller and CCO accountability.
  • Clinical outcomes interpretation. Omada's clinical team owns this.
  • Direct customer interaction (outbound email at scale with no human review).

Guiding principle: AI is speed. Humans are accountability. The CRO owns where that line sits, and it sits in a specific place that can be defended in a board meeting.

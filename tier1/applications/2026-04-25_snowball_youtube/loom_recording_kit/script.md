## 0:30 to 3:30 - California Health Plan, the wildfire reframe ($200K to $1.4M ARR)

**[Lean in slightly. This is the warm-up story. Pace deliberate, not rushed.]**

"Story one. A large regional health plan, roughly 600,000 members, in a region that gets hit hard by wildfires every season.

They put out an RFP. The framing was simple. We need to send messages to members during disaster events. Price it per transaction. Competitive bids were coming in around six cents per message. The expected deal size under that framing was about 200,000 dollars."

**[Pause.]**

"Now. Most sellers will price six cents and try to win on volume. I did not.

What I saw in the RFP was a procurement framing that completely missed the actual problem. The actual problem was not message volume. It was continuity of care under regulatory constraint. Members on insulin lose refrigeration during a power outage. Call centers melt down. And here is the part the RFP never mentioned. Every outbound message a health plan sends has to be pre-approved at the state and federal level, and that approval cycle takes months. So during a wildfire, the plan does not have time to procure new messaging. The capacity has to already be staged and pre-approved before the fire starts."

**[Beat. This is the reframe. Let it land.]**

"So I rejected the RFP framing. I shifted the conversation away from procurement, and I routed it to the Chief Medical Officer, General Counsel, and Government Affairs. Different stakeholders, different budget, different mandate.

The conversation stopped being about cost per message. It became about standing capability that protects the plan from regulatory penalty, clinical liability, and Star Rating exposure during an event."

**[Beat.]**

"I rebuilt the offer. Not per transaction. A platform-based annual subscription with pre-approved continuity-of-care workflows already staged, effectively unlimited messaging during an event, multi-year term. I documented the entire reframe in a Plan Letter so the buyer and I had one shared source of truth, and procurement could not pull us back to unit price.

We closed at 1.4 million ARR, multi-year. Same product, same company, same buyer. Different sales approach. Seven times the RFP-implied size."

**[Pause. Now translate.]**

"Here is the Snowball version of that exact move. A brand CMO comes to you and says, give me a quote per video. Compare to my ad agency on CPM. That is the six cents per message conversation. And if you let them frame it that way, you are competing with every long-form YouTube shop on production cost.

The reframe is the same one. Snowball is not selling videos. Snowball is selling owned-media infrastructure. A brand without a YouTube channel during the next platform shift is in the same posture as a health plan without pre-approved messaging during a wildfire. The CFO will price standing capability. Procurement will not.

That is the move. Same discipline."

## 3:30 to 7:00 - US Radiology, the OCR to revenue infrastructure reframe ($30K to $1.6M ARR)

**[Slight shift. New story, slightly more energy. This is the longest beat.]**

"Story two. A large venture-backed national radiology organization. They were consolidating imaging centers across the US, running on a centers-of-excellence model. 5.5 million faxed physician orders per year, processed manually by humans reading handwritten faxes.

Their initial ask was OCR. Optical character recognition for the faxes. The pricing they wanted was around half a cent per page. The deal as scoped was about 30,000 dollars annually.

A 30K commodity tech buy."

**[Beat. Let the smallness of that sit.]**

"Now, again. Most sellers will sell OCR and call it a fast win. I did not.

What I saw was a venture-backed organization where every single MRI machine and CT scanner is essentially a venture-funded asset whose unit economics depend entirely on utilization. Idle machines lose money every minute they are not running. And the problem with the manual fax process was not OCR accuracy. It was that physician orders were going to multiple centers simultaneously, the centers were competing internally to be first to call the patient, and the slow center kept losing patients to the fast center, or losing them entirely to no-shows.

So OCR was the surface symptom. The actual root constraint was that this organization had no integrated intake-to-engagement system. They had a revenue optimization problem dressed up as a data entry problem."

**[Pause. Set up the move.]**

"I walked the discovery up five levels. From OCR accuracy, to operational delay, to lost first-touch with the patient, to idle-machine revenue loss, to a board-level utilization thesis the VC backers were watching every quarter. Each level I validated with the buyer. Plan Letter after every meeting. Roughly 40 meetings, around 12 of them on-site, six to eight months.

By the time I was done, I was not selling OCR anymore. I was selling a closed-loop revenue optimization system from physician order all the way through patient confirmation. Intake, validation, routing, engagement, all of it.

There was an incumbent. A patient engagement vendor with deep board relationships. I did not fight them inside their scope. I expanded above and below them. I anchored the deal to their renewal date as the forcing function, and I redirected the spend they were already approved for into a broader integrated platform."

**[Beat.]**

"Closed at 1.6 million ARR. Roughly 7 million in TCV across the multi-year. Incumbent displaced. Full intake-to-engagement workflow under our system."

**[Smile here. Brief. The Disney moment.]**

"I will tell you the moment I knew this one was real. We were on-site, and I had just finished walking the COO and the CFO through the utilization math. The COO looked at the CFO, and then he turned to me and gave me a fist bump across the table. Like we were at a Disney park and his kid had just gotten the autograph. Just a guy who finally felt like somebody actually understood his business.

That is what reframe sells. People do not fist-bump you for a 30K OCR quote. They fist-bump you when you finally name the problem they have been trying to articulate for two years."

**[Beat. Translate.]**

"Snowball version. A brand walks in and says, we want one YouTube video. We have a budget for one. That is the OCR ask. The 30K version of the deal.

The reframe is, you do not have a video problem. You have an owned-media revenue infrastructure problem, and the video is one symptom of it. The CFO budget for that is not the marketing line. It is the brand-equity-as-a-balance-sheet-asset budget, which is a completely different number. And if there is an incumbent ad agency on retainer, you do not fight them in their lane. You expand above and below, and you tie the new conversation to their renewal date.

Same discipline."

## 7:00 to 10:30 - CalOptima, threshold entry to enterprise expansion ($249K land to ~$10M TCV)

**[Pace down slightly. This is the most procurement-heavy story. Operator voice, not theatrical.]**

"Story three. The hardest one. Largest deal in my company's history.

A large regional Medicaid health plan, also around 600 to 700,000 members. End of the COVID public health emergency. Members had to be requalified for Medicaid coverage in a bounded window or they will lose their insurance.

The buyer's initial ask was Medicaid redetermination outreach. Help us reach members to requalify. The expected deal size at the surface framing was about 360,000 dollars.

Here is the friction. The buyer had a non-RFP procurement threshold of 250,000. Anything above that triggered a formal multi-month RFP. The redetermination window did not give them months. They needed to start now."

**[Pause.]**

"So the first move was a threshold-constrained entry. I priced the initial scope at 249,000. Just under the formal RFP gate. Not a discount. A sequencing decision. We landed inside the account, started executing, and bought ourselves the operational presence we needed for the larger conversation.

While we were inside delivering, I ran active discovery. I met with stakeholders outside Member Experience. Clinical, Quality, Government Affairs, Compliance. I documented every meeting in a Plan Letter, with the Mutual Sequence of Events embedded inside it as the buying committee's roadmap.

What I uncovered at level five was that redetermination was the visible tip. Underneath was a Star Ratings decline that was costing the plan hundreds of millions in CMS reimbursement exposure. They had no integrated member intelligence layer, no predictive analytics, fragmentation across five departments. The enterprise problem was tens of times bigger than the redetermination ask."

**[Beat.]**

"When the formal RFP came, the budget was about 3.8 million per year. I needed scope I did not have alone. So I called the predictive analytics firm that was about to bid against us, and I offered them subcontractor status under our prime. I added Rex Wallace Consulting as the operational execution partner. Three firms, one prime, one bid.

Then came the internal pressure. My own CRO, my own CEO, my own leadership all pushed me to narrow the bid back down to messaging only, around a million ceiling. The instinct was understandable. Smaller deals are easier to win. I refused. I argued the asymmetric risk. Narrow scope competes against many vendors at low margin. Enterprise scope competes alone at high margin. I owned the architecture and I held the line.

Then came the silent period. During the formal RFP, leadership pressured me to send side-channel notes to the champions. Just check in. Send a thoughtful piece of content. I refused that too. Government procurement has rules. Silence is compliance, not disengagement. Violating that gets you disqualified, and it puts your champion in legal jeopardy."

**[Pause. Bring it home.]**

"We won. Around 3 million ARR, just under 10 million in total contract value. Largest deal in company history. The whole thing was carried by Plan Letter discipline across every meeting, with the Mutual Sequence of Events sitting inside each Plan Letter as the buying committee's shared roadmap."

**[Translate.]**

"Snowball version. A Fortune 500 brand comes in with one YouTube test buy. Maybe a 50K to 150K initial engagement. That is your threshold-constrained entry. You execute. Inside the account, you run discovery on the parts of their content operation that are actually broken. Twelve months later, when their agency-of-record contract is up for renewal, you are not bidding cold. You are the incumbent with delivery proof, and the conversation is no longer about one video. It is about whether the brand becomes a studio.

That is the same move. Land, expand, hold discipline through procurement, and let the Plan Letter carry the deal across the long cycle."

## Ad-lib markers and pause cues

- **[Pause]** = full one-second silence. Resist filling it.
- **[Beat]** = half-second. Breathe.
- **[Smile here]** = genuine, not held.
- **[Lean in slightly]** = forward in chair, not theatrical.
- **[Direct to camera]** = drop any screen reference, full eye contact.

If you fumble a number, do not restart. Self-correct in real time. "Roughly 1.4 million ARR. Sorry, just over 1.4." That kind of correction reads as authentic. Take 2 fixes; restart loses the operator energy.

## Beat 2 — The US Radiology story (1:00 to 6:30)

**Visual:** Jeff on camera the entire beat. No screen share yet. This is a story told eye to lens.

**Tone:** Operator narrating an operator memory. Not pitching. The Disney moment is the only place voice modulation is allowed, and only by a hair.

### 2A. Setup (1:00 to 1:45)

> The customer was a national radiology organization. Venture-backed. Consolidating imaging centers across the country. Five and a half million faxed physician orders a year. Manual processing.
>
> They came to us with a technical ask. OCR for fax ingestion. Commodity unit pricing. The deal on the table was about twenty-five thousand dollars annually. An IT line item.

**Pause cue:** Half-beat after "An IT line item." Sets up the conflict.

### 2B. Conflict (1:45 to 2:45)

> Most sellers in that conversation close a twenty-five-thousand-dollar OCR deal in a quarter and move on. The buyer framed it as scanning. The procurement team treated it as fungible utility. Every OCR vendor was bidding the same accuracy and throughput numbers.
>
> If I accepted that frame, the ceiling was twenty-five thousand. The stakeholder was IT. The competitive position was price.
>
> I did not accept that frame.

**Pause cue:** Full beat after "I did not accept that frame." This is the reframe gate.

### 2C. Reframe (2:45 to 4:15)

> What I saw was a revenue problem the customer was describing as a scanning problem.
>
> Their imaging centers operated on a centers-of-excellence model. Faxed orders hit multiple centers at once. Whichever center reached the patient first won the procedure. Manual intake delayed outreach. Delays meant lost patients. Lost patients meant idle MRI and CT machines. Those machines were venture-funded. Their entire growth thesis was utilization economics.
>
> So I stopped talking about scanning. I started talking about machine utilization revenue lost downstream of intake delay. That moves the conversation out of IT and into operations and finance. It moves the budget from a thirty-thousand-dollar IT line to a seven-figure revenue-optimization line.
>
> Same company. Different stakeholder. Different decision threshold. Different deal.

**Ad-lib marker:** If Jeff stumbles, the recovery line is "same company, different conversation, different deal." Use that as a reset.

### 2D. Plan Letter discipline (4:15 to 5:15)

> Forty meetings. Twelve on-site. Six to eight months. Four C-level stakeholders, plus operations leadership.
>
> The way I held control across that cycle was a Plan Letter after every meaningful meeting. Each Plan Letter validated the framing in that stakeholder's language. The CIO Plan Letter spoke integration and security. The COO Plan Letter spoke workflow and throughput. The CFO Plan Letter spoke utilization and revenue.
>
> Inside each Plan Letter is the Mutual Sequence of Events. That is how I knew the close date weeks before it landed. The sequence was buyer-validated, in writing, every step of the way. By the time the contract draft hit, we were not negotiating. We were executing a pre-validated sequence.

**Pause cue:** Half-beat before "That is how I knew the close date."

**Note on tone:** "Plan Letter is the container, MSoE lives inside it" is encoded here through the phrase "Inside each Plan Letter is the Mutual Sequence of Events." Do not list them as parallel items. Do not say "Plan Letters and Mutual Sequence of Events." That flattens the structure.

### 2E. The Disney moment (5:15 to 6:00)

**Tone shift:** Slightly warmer. This is the only emotional beat in the entire video. Do not oversell it. Tell it the way you would tell a friend at a dinner table.

> I had booked a Disney trip with my family months earlier. Mid-flight, somewhere over the country, I leaned over and fist-bumped my wife. She had her headphones on the whole time. She did not take them off. She knew.
>
> The deal closed that week. One point six million ARR. A twenty-five-thousand-dollar OCR ask broadened into an enterprise revenue operations platform. The incumbent patient engagement vendor was displaced.

**Pause cue:** Two full beats after "displaced." This is the strongest beat in the entire video. Let the moment land.

### 2F. Story close (6:00 to 6:30)

> That is the kind of story I tell. That is the kind of move I make. The pattern is consistent across the rest of my book at Ushur. Twenty-four million in TCV in under two years as an individual contributor.
>
> Now I want to show you what the first ninety days at Snowball look like.

**Transition cue:** Jeff initiates screen share here. The camera bubble shrinks but stays on Jeff. Do not turn the camera off.

## Beat 3 — GTM plan teaser, screen share (6:30 to 10:30)

**Visual:** Screen share of the deck Agent S5 produced. Day 30, Day 60, Day 90 milestone slides. Jeff narrates over them. Camera bubble visible.

**Tone:** Operator walking another operator through a build. Not selling. Showing the shape and the math. The methodology stays hidden. This is the plan-as-reserve-asset rule operationalized in a Loom.

### 3A. Frame the teaser (6:30 to 7:00)

> What you are looking at is the ninety-day plan I built for the founding Head of Sales seat at Snowball. I want to be clear about what I am showing you and what I am not.
>
> I am showing you the milestones. Day thirty looks like X. Day sixty looks like Y. Day ninety looks like Z. You will see the shape, you will see the math, you will see whether the destination is worth the conversation.
>
> What I am not showing you is the methodology behind each milestone. That is the conversation we have if you want to schedule a call.

**Pause cue:** Half-beat. Let the framing register before the first slide.

### 3B. Day 30 milestone walk (7:00 to 7:45)

**Visual:** Day 30 slide.

> Day thirty. The first thirty days are not selling. The first thirty days are qualification rigor.
>
> You told the world publicly that roughly thirty inbound brands a month come through your LinkedIn and X. That is not a sales problem. That is a qualification problem, a reframe problem, and a Plan Letter discipline problem. The first thirty days are about installing the filter.
>
> By the end of day thirty, the inbound triage is running, the ICP is sharper than it was, and the first reframe conversations are scheduled. The shape of the funnel is visible.

**Ad-lib marker:** If Jeff wants to add color, he can name "B2B SaaS-led ICP, with creator-personality as a separate lane." But keep the methodology hidden.

### 3C. Day 60 milestone walk (7:45 to 8:30)

**Visual:** Day 60 slide.

> Day sixty. By day sixty, the first five enterprise reframe conversations are inside the Plan Letter discipline. Not five closed deals. Five conversations under deal control, with Mutual Sequence of Events agreed in writing inside each Plan Letter, and economic alignment with the buyer's budget authority.
>
> You will see on this slide what the pipeline looks like at day sixty. You will see the named segments. You will see the qualification rate against the inbound flow.
>
> What you will not see on this slide is how I run the reframe. That is the conversation.

**Pause cue:** A short pause after "That is the conversation." Reinforces the reserve-asset posture without lecturing.

### 3D. Day 90 milestone walk (8:30 to 9:30)

**Visual:** Day 90 slide.

> Day ninety. By day ninety, the function is operating, not just performing. That means three things.
>
> One. The first closed enterprise relationships at the top of the Snowball ICP. The shift you are building is from thirty inbound brands a month into roughly five closed enterprise relationships per quarter. Different math, different revenue mix, different capacity plan.
>
> Two. The AE hiring profile is drafted. You hire Creative Directors who can tell stories. The same instinct applies to AEs. I am not hiring career SaaS reps. I am hiring operators who can hold a reframe in front of a CMO.
>
> Three. The AE coaching cadence is running. Plan Letter review on a recurring rhythm. Discovery debrief structure. The same discipline you use to teach a Creative Director the YouTube mechanics, applied to the sales floor.

**Note on tone:** This is the densest beat. Slow the pace. Do not hurry through three points.

### 3E. Snowball-specific signals (9:30 to 10:00)

**Visual:** Hold on the Day 90 slide or transition to a summary slide.

> The thing I want you to hear in this is that I am not pitching a generic enterprise sales playbook against your business. The constraint at Snowball is not lead generation. The constraint is qualifying, sequencing, and pricing inbound demand into the right client mix and the right capacity plan.
>
> That is a discipline problem, not a top-of-funnel problem. The discipline I bring is exactly that.

### 3F. Hand back to camera (10:00 to 10:30)

**Visual:** Jeff stops screen share. Back to full camera.

## Practice notes for Jeff

### What to practice most

1. **The Disney moment (5:15 to 6:00).** This is the strongest beat in the video. Practice it three or four times until it feels like dinner-table register, not pitch register. The risk is overselling the emotion. Under-deliver it.
2. **The reframe gate (2:45 transition).** "I did not accept that frame" is a fulcrum line. The video pivots on it. Practice the pause after it.
3. **The close (Beat 4).** The line "that is just how I roll" must land warm and flat both at the open and the close. If it sounds like a tagline either time, the whole frame collapses.

### What is most likely to go wrong

1. **Beat 3 dragging.** The screen share is the danger zone. Four minutes is generous. If Jeff over-explains a milestone, he will leak methodology and hit fourteen minutes. Discipline: state the milestone, name the visible math, refuse the methodology, move on.
2. **The Plan Letter / MSoE structure flattening.** If Jeff says "Plan Letters and Mutual Sequence of Events" anywhere, the structure breaks. The phrase is "Inside each Plan Letter is the Mutual Sequence of Events." Container and contents.
3. **Voice escalating in Beat 2.** The story is 5 minutes 30 seconds long. There is a temptation to build energy across that span. Resist it. Keep the register flat. The Disney moment is the only allowed lift.

### What to avoid

- "We don't follow YouTube best practices, we create them" or any variant. Hashemi's tagline. Will read as un-read.
- "10x peer outperformance." Choogy. Banned.
- Personal earnings figures. Banned.
- Em dashes in any improvised line. Banned.
- "Hey Ryan." Use "Hello, Ryan."
- "Would" anywhere. "Will."

## 3. The System I Run

The operating discipline I bring is the same system that produced $24M TCV in under two years at Ushur as an individual contributor and 5.5 years of 150%+ quota performance at Kronos. It is the system I have taught enterprise sellers as a CustomerCentric Selling Coach for 12 years.

**Five-Level Discovery.** Surface symptom, stated problem, operational impact, economic impact, root constraint and unconsidered need. Most sellers stop at Level 2. Enterprise deals live at Level 5. The progression is taught with a single repeatable question, "and then what happens," walked from the buyer's first complaint to the revenue floor where the economic buyer lives.

**Power Messaging.** Why This Solution and Why Now, both grounded in evidence the buyer themselves disclosed in discovery. Constraint match, differentiated capability, supporting evidence on the Why This side. Visible cost of inaction, active financial loss occurring, delay increasing risk on the Why Now side. Not marketing assertion. Logical conclusion the buyer cannot avoid.

**Two Gates Buying Cycle Alignment.** Gate One: is the buyer in an active evaluation or change cycle. Gate Two: is problem severity quantified to justify change. When either gate fails, the discipline is widen, not walk. I surface a larger use case, follow the economic pain downstream, and anchor the solution to an existing forcing function inside the account, contract renewal, regulatory deadline, board-level number. Most "failed" deals are deals the seller did not widen.

**Plan Letter discipline.** The Plan Letter is the container artifact for every substantive enterprise meeting. It contains, inside it, the named stakeholder map, the buyer's economic case, the Mutual Sequence of Events laying out commitments and milestones from both sides through close, and the success criteria the buyer holds the seller accountable to. Mutual Sequence of Events is one of the elements inside the Plan Letter, not a peer concept beside it. If a seller cannot produce a Plan Letter after a meeting, the seller did not have a real meeting.

**MEDDPICC.** Lives inside this framework, not alongside it. I have run MEDDPICC at every single Ushur and UKG account for the better part of a decade. It is the qualification spine that lets a forecast number get committed; it is not the methodology itself.

**Why this matters at Lovable.** A PLG product entering the enterprise tier has a unique discovery problem. The buyer (a CTO, CIO, VP Engineering, or Head of Innovation) has often never used Lovable themselves. The user (a developer, a product manager, a designer) has used Lovable, often for months, and is the one signaling the buy. The discovery motion has to bridge the user's experience to the buyer's economic case, in the buyer's language, without losing the user's enthusiasm. The Five-Level Discovery framework is the mechanism that does this.

## 4. Market Context and Opportunity Sizing

The AI software-creation category is forming in real time. Three forces are driving it:

1. **Generative AI capability has crossed the threshold for production-quality code generation.** The ceiling on what a non-developer can build with AI assistance has moved up an order of magnitude in 18 months. Lovable, Cursor, Replit, v0, Bolt, and Devin are all expressions of this shift.
2. **Enterprise IT backlogs are at all-time highs.** The Fortune 1000 has years of internal-tool requests stacked behind core engineering capacity. Every CIO is looking for a way to clear the backlog without hiring or outsourcing.
3. **Citizen developer demand has matured.** Product managers, business analysts, designers, and operations staff inside enterprise are no longer satisfied with no-code platforms that hit a complexity ceiling. They want production-grade output. AI software creation gives them that.

**Where Lovable sits.** Lovable's product is positioned as letting anyone build software in any language at velocity. This places Lovable above the developer-tool category and inside the enterprise software-creation category. That positioning is correct, and it is where the durable category leadership lives. Cursor, Copilot, and Codeium will fight for the developer wallet. Lovable's enterprise wedge is the non-developer wallet plus the developer wallet, which is a fundamentally larger TAM.

**TAM triangulation (hypothesis, validates Week 1).**

- Enterprise software dev tools market: ~$30B globally, growing at 15-20% CAGR.
- Internal application development tools (low-code, no-code, citizen dev): ~$45B globally, growing 25%+ CAGR.
- Enterprise services backlog (consulting, custom dev, IT modernization): ~$500B+ annually, with 10-20% addressable by AI software creation over the next five years.
- Lovable's addressable enterprise tier within these intersections: I estimate $5-15B near-term, with significant upside as the category formalizes. This is a hypothesis. I will refine against Lovable's internal data Week 1.

**What enterprise buyers are confused about.** This is the load-bearing market observation:

- They do not yet have a budget category for "AI software creation platform." It does not appear in standard analyst taxonomies.
- They confuse Lovable with developer copilots (smaller deals, IT budget) or with low-code platforms (commoditized, lower price points).
- They do not yet have a procurement playbook for a tool used by both engineers and non-engineers. Standard procurement assumes a tool serves one persona.
- They are unsure whether to evaluate Lovable for security and compliance as a developer tool (lower bar) or as an enterprise application platform (higher bar). The wrong evaluation track kills six-figure deals.

The Head of Global Sales installs the conversation that resolves this confusion in the buyer's favor. That is the GTM unlock.

## 5. Competitive Landscape

I treat the competitive landscape honestly. Some of these players are genuine threats. Some are misread as threats and are actually category-validating tailwinds. I will refine against Lovable's actual win/loss data Week 1.

**Cursor.** Developer-first AI code editor. Excellent product, well-loved by professional engineers. Competing for developer mindshare and individual-developer subscription dollars. Where Cursor wins: serious software engineers writing production code in mature codebases. Where Lovable wins: anyone who is not a senior engineer trying to ship working software. The categories overlap by ~20%; the buyer is different.

**Replit.** Browser-based dev environment with AI assistance. Strong on collaboration and education. Competing for hobbyist and team-coding mindshare. Where Replit wins: collaborative coding, education, hackathons. Where Lovable wins: the buyer who wants the output (a working app), not the experience of coding it.

**GitHub Copilot.** Microsoft-owned, distributed through GitHub and integrated with VS Code. The default for any company already in the Microsoft developer ecosystem. Where Copilot wins: enterprises that already license GitHub Enterprise and are buying Copilot as a line extension. Where Lovable wins: the buyer who is not solving the "make my engineers 20% faster" problem but the "let my non-engineers build the app" problem. Different buyer, different outcome.

**v0 (Vercel).** AI UI generation tied to the Vercel deployment ecosystem. Strong for frontend prototyping. Where v0 wins: design-to-frontend-code translation for teams already on Vercel. Where Lovable wins: end-to-end app creation, backend included, not just UI.

**Bolt (StackBlitz).** Closest direct competitor in positioning. Browser-based AI app builder, similar PLG motion. Honest read: Bolt and Lovable are racing for the same category leadership in non-developer-builds-real-software. The winner is the one that lands the enterprise tier faster and converts the user signal into procurement-grade revenue.

**Codeium.** Enterprise-focused AI code completion. Competing for the Fortune 1000 dev tool budget against Copilot and Cursor. Where Codeium wins: enterprises that want a Copilot alternative with stronger enterprise governance. Where Lovable wins: same as above, the non-developer use case Codeium does not serve.

**Tabnine.** Mature AI code completion with strong on-prem and air-gapped deployment options. Competing for regulated industries (financial services, healthcare, public sector) that cannot send code to cloud. Lovable's enterprise tier needs to address this same procurement bar. Hypothesis-pending: does Lovable have an on-prem or VPC deployment option for regulated buyers? I will validate Week 1.

**Devin (Cognition).** AI software engineer agent. Different category, autonomous coding agent versus interactive AI builder. Where Devin wins: companies trying to replace developer headcount with autonomous agents. Where Lovable wins: companies trying to enable non-developers, not replace developers. Adjacent, not direct.

**The headline competitive insight.** Most of the named competitors are competing for developer wallet. Lovable's enterprise category is software-creation, which is a larger and structurally different market. The risk is allowing the AE team to position Lovable as a Cursor alternative or a Copilot challenger, which collapses the deal size to a per-seat developer-tool comp. The discipline is positioning Lovable as a category that does not yet have a Magic Quadrant and pricing accordingly. This is exactly the reframe motion I ran on the radiology OCR deal: the buyer asked for a tool inside a known category at a known price; the seller delivered a system inside a category that did not yet exist at a price the buyer's CFO understood.

## 7. ICP Deep Dive and Trigger Events

The first 50 enterprise customers will define the category Lovable plays in. They cannot be opportunistic. They have to be a deliberate ICP refinement.

**Hypothesis-driven ICP, refines Week 1 against Lovable's actual closed-won data.**

**Tier 1 ICP, mid-market to lower enterprise ($1B-$10B revenue):**

- Industries: financial services (insurance, regional banks, asset managers), healthcare (provider organizations, regional health plans, healthtech), retail (mid-market omnichannel), manufacturing (industrial equipment, building products), public sector (state and local government, education systems).
- Persona: VP Engineering, CTO of business unit, Head of Innovation, Chief Digital Officer.
- Trigger events: digital transformation budget cycle, post-merger systems consolidation, IT services budget cap, departure of a senior engineering leader, board-level mandate to ship a customer-facing product faster.
- Why this tier first: faster procurement cycles, less complex security review, executive accessibility, willingness to pilot.

**Tier 2 ICP, enterprise ($10B+ revenue):**

- Industries: financial services (top-tier banks, insurers), healthcare (national health plans, large IDNs), retail (national chains), manufacturing (Global 2000), public sector (federal civilian, state-level).
- Persona: CIO, Chief Architect, Chief Digital Officer, Head of Engineering Productivity.
- Trigger events: vendor consolidation initiative, AI center-of-excellence formation, services contract renewal (the radiology pattern), competitive AI strategy from a peer company, regulatory mandate triggering custom software build.
- Why this tier second: longer procurement cycles, harder security review, larger contracts, requires reference customers from Tier 1.

**Numbered trigger events that signal procurement readiness:**

1. A senior engineering leader has been hired in the last six months with a mandate to "modernize" or "speed up" software delivery.
2. The company has announced an AI strategy in an earnings call or investor update.
3. A services or consulting contract for custom software development is up for renewal in the next 12 months.
4. A competitor in the company's industry has publicly announced AI-native product capability that the company does not have.
5. Multiple individual users from the same company have signed up for Lovable in the last 90 days.
6. A user from the company has graduated from free to paid in the last 90 days.
7. The company has a published RFP or RFI for development tools, low-code, or AI productivity in the last 90 days.
8. The company has had a publicly disclosed security or compliance incident requiring rapid software change.

The AE team is coached to prioritize accounts where two or more of these triggers are present concurrently.

**What I will not do.** I will not let the AE team chase any account in any industry that has a few users. That is the leakage motion. The discipline is concentrating effort on a defined named-account list where the trigger events justify the AE's time. This is the difference between a $24M TCV book and a $5M TCV book.

## 10. The 9-Step Sales Sequence at Lovable

The Mutual Sequence of Events lives inside the Plan Letter. It is not a separate document. It is the operational spine that takes a buyer from validated problem severity to signed contract and implementation plan. Adapted to Lovable's PLG-with-enterprise-tier reality.

**Evaluation Phase:**

1. **Validation of Problem Severity.** The user inside the account has signaled. The AE walks discovery to the level where the buyer (CIO, VP Eng, CDO) confirms the problem severity in writing. The PLG user is the inside source; the buyer is the procurement source. Both have to be in the Plan Letter.
2. **Agreement on Success Criteria.** Buyer and seller jointly define what success looks like 12 months post-contract. Specific, measurable, owned by the buyer.
3. **Technical Demo Aligned to Use Case.** Demo is scoped to the buyer's validated use case, not a standard product tour. For Lovable this is critical: the buyer has likely already seen the product through the PLG user. The demo's job is to show the enterprise tier capability, governance, security, scale, that the user has not yet engaged.
4. **Stakeholder Expansion.** The user-champion introduces additional stakeholders relevant to the decision. For Lovable enterprise, this almost always includes a security or compliance reviewer, a procurement contact, a finance signer, and a senior technical owner.
5. **Security and Compliance Review.** Initiated early enough not to become a gate at the end. For an enterprise tier in financial services, healthcare, or public sector, this is the most common deal-killer. Run it in parallel with discovery, not after pricing.

**Execution Phase:**

6. **Commercial Structuring.** Pricing aligned to the platform value, not per-seat. Multi-year terms where the buyer has the budget appetite. Aligned to the buyer's fiscal calendar.
7. **Executive Approval.** The economic buyer confirms commitment in writing. For Lovable enterprise, this is typically a CIO, CDO, or CFO depending on where the budget sits.
8. **Contract Execution.** Legal and procurement close. With the security review pre-completed and the Plan Letter validated, this is mechanical.
9. **Implementation Plan.** Already agreed and scheduled before contract signature. The first 30 days post-signature are committed in writing, with named owners on both sides.

**Why this sequence works for PLG-to-enterprise.** The standard PLG motion ends at step 1 or 2, where the user pays and the company closes the small contract. The enterprise tier requires steps 3 through 9 to be run with discipline. The risk is that the AE team treats every enterprise opportunity as an oversized PLG sale. They do not. They are enterprise deals with a PLG-validated entry. The sequence protects against the deal collapsing back into per-seat pricing in late-stage procurement.

**Coaching point I will hammer with every AE.** A deal in forecast must have a buyer-validated Sequence of Events in writing. No exceptions. A deal without one is not in forecast; it is in pipeline. This is the discipline that separates committed numbers from happy-ear numbers, and it is the same discipline I have run at every Ushur and UKG account for the better part of a decade.

- The economic buyer never engages (deal stalls in mid-funnel).
- The buyer has a low-code or RPA platform contract running with significant remaining term (no forcing function).
- Internal champion leaves the company mid-cycle (deal evaporates).

**Mitigations I install:**
- The sales motion never positions Lovable as a developer copilot alternative. AEs are coached to disqualify or reframe at the first sign of category miscategorization.
- Security review is scoped at step 3 of the Sequence, not step 8.
- Every forecast deal requires a named economic buyer engaged in the Plan Letter.
- The discipline of widen-not-walk: when a deal stalls because the original ask does not clear the Two Gates, AEs are coached to surface a larger use case and tie the solution to a forcing function inside the account, contract renewal, regulatory deadline, board-level number.

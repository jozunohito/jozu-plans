1. The System I Run

Every plan I build for every client and every employer runs on one integrated method.

Deep Discovery (5 layers). Surface Symptoms become Stated Problems become Operational Impact become Economic Impact become Root Constraint and Unconsidered Need. You do not earn the right to claim a Power Message until you reach Layer 4. Most sellers stop at Layer 2 and wonder why their forecasts miss. In lending infrastructure, Layer 4 is where the buyer's CFO and Chief Risk Officer live: cost per serviced loan, compliance exception rate, migration capital expenditure, ledger rebuild hours in a regulatory audit, capital-efficiency impact of operational drag on the lending P&L.

Power Messaging. A Power Message links a unique capability to a buyer-specific problem defined in clear economic terms. It emerges from discovery, never from marketing. "Peach is API-first" is not a Power Message. "Your $400M personal loan portfolio runs on a vendor that charges you 14 months and seven figures to add a line-of-credit product. You have already lost two quarters of origination to a competitor who is shipping that product on modern infrastructure. Peach Adaptive Core spins up a line-of-credit variant in weeks against your existing ledger, which puts approximately $18M of at-risk originations back on your P&L this fiscal year" is a Power Message.

Two gates before I sell. Gate 1 is Active Evaluation. The buyer is actually in motion, with a stakeholder, a timeline, and a procurement path. Gate 2 is Quantified Severity. They can name the dollar cost of doing nothing. If either gate fails, I exit with discipline. I keep the relationship warm and move on. Most deals die because sellers refuse to accept that a buyer is not actually buying.

Mutual Sequence of Events. Co-created with the buyer. Includes procurement, legal, vendor-risk-management (critical in banks and credit unions), security review, SOC 1 and SOC 2 artifacts, PCI DSS attestation, data migration plan, go-live plan, and first-90-day hypercare. The sequence ends beyond signature at first portfolio fully migrated and first compliance audit successfully served, because that is when the buyer actually sees the outcome they bought. That is how we never get caught selling.

The Plan Letter. Sent within 24 hours of every substantive conversation. It restates value in the buyer's economic language, delivers every commitment made in the meeting, lays out the complete timeline to go-live, and asks the buyer to confirm in writing. If they write back yes, the deal belongs in the forecast. If they rewrite the sequence, that is better data and the deal belongs in the forecast with updated milestones. If they go silent, they are not in the forecast.

The forecast rule. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in the forecast. I learned this at UKG, where the culture enforced it without exception. My manager's line was "great job on the forecast. Show me the Plan Letter." That rule made forecasts defensible by construction, not by hope. It is what kept my number real year after year and what produced top-5-percent-globally ranking for nearly six years.

The forcing function. If I cannot write a good Plan Letter after a call, I ran a bad meeting. Period. So, run better meetings. That discipline is how I coach sellers without sitting in every one of their meetings.

Every initiative below exists to make Plan Letters writable and forecasts accurate. That is the whole point.

2.2 The Peach addressable slice

Digital lending is the umbrella. Peach's addressable slice is specifically non-mortgage loan management and servicing for fintechs, specialty lenders, banks, and credit unions, across BNPL, cards, personal loans, business loans, retail installment contracts, and emerging private credit structures.

  • US fintech lenders. Approximately 450 active fintech lenders originating at meaningful volume in the US per CB Insights and PitchBook fintech trackers. The total population expands further when embedded lending and vertical SaaS players with credit products are included.
  • US community banks and credit unions. Approximately 4,000 community banks and 4,700 credit unions per FDIC and NCUA data. Approximately 30 to 40 percent are actively modernizing lending infrastructure in a 36-month window, per Everest Group's Great Core Banking Shakeup analysis.
  • US specialty finance firms and private credit GPs. Over 1,500 private credit funds globally with specialty finance seeing $37B of fundraising in 2025 alone, per With Intelligence. Many of these funds run asset-based lending, equipment finance, consumer receivables, and direct origination programs that need modern servicing.
  • BNPL and cards issuers. Roughly 30 to 50 US-based BNPL and embedded-cards issuers of meaningful scale, plus the long tail of vertical-SaaS embedded credit. The BNPL market is maturing rather than growing explosively, which is actually good for Peach: maturing programs need industrial-grade servicing, not greenfield innovation theatre.
  • Embedded lenders, vertical SaaS. Shopify, Square, Toast, ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, and hundreds of vertical-SaaS players have credit products live or in scoping. The embedded lending layer is the fastest-expanding Peach adjacency.

Peach's addressable account base (my math):

  • Approximately 6,000 US addressable institutions across fintech lenders, community banks, credit unions, specialty finance firms, and vertical SaaS with credit products.
  • Realistic penetration ceiling at a premium modern servicing platform: 8 to 12 percent over seven to ten years. Call it 500 to 700 accounts at maturity.
  • Blended ACV target by account, based on published LoanPro and Mambu benchmarks plus inferences from Peach's product packaging: $250K to $2.5M depending on asset class, portfolio size, and number of product SKUs on the platform.
  • A 500-account book at a $500K average ACV is $250M ARR. A 700-account book at $1M average ACV is $700M ARR. The envelope well exceeds the stated $100M ARR mandate.

Implication for the sell. $100M ARR is not a market-sizing stretch. It is a motion and execution problem. The work is building a disciplined named-account program across three well-defined buyer segments, with a commercial motion that closes at double-digit win rates rather than volume-driven low-win-rate chaos.

2.3 Regulatory tailwinds that create forcing functions

Three regulatory signals converge in the next 18 months and force buying cycles into the window.

  • CFPB Personal Financial Data Rights Rule. Open banking compliance required by April 1, 2026 for the largest institutions, with phased compliance extending to 2030 for smaller institutions. Every consumer lender has to evaluate whether their loan management system can support compliant data sharing at scale. Compliance Guard plus Peach's API architecture answers the requirement out of the box.
  • OCC Community Bank Minimum BSA/AML Examination Procedures. Effective February 1, 2026. Community banks face a standardized exam protocol. Banks still running Premier, Precision, Core, or older loan modules inside a legacy core face elevated audit risk on the loan book. Peach plus Compliance Guard is a procurement-ready answer.
  • Enforcement tone. Block's $80M BSA/AML penalty in January 2025 plus 28 CFPB enforcement actions against nonbanks in 2024 reset the enforcement bar. Boards are now directing compliance teams to budget for modernization in 2026. The buying signal is "our legal and risk committee told us to fix this by year end."

Implication for the sell. Compliance is the fastest-moving wedge in the lending infrastructure market in 2026. A compliance-led sale closes faster than a modernization-led sale, because the CFO stops asking "why now" once the regulator asks "why not yet." Initiative 1 attacks this head-on.

2.4 Private credit and specialty finance as platform expansion

  • US private credit market size: $1.3T AUM today, from $500B five years ago (Morgan Stanley).
  • Specialty finance fundraising in 2025: $37B, second only to direct lending ($79B) among private credit strategies (With Intelligence).
  • Asset-based finance projected to rival or exceed direct lending over the next decade as banks de-risk balance sheets (Morgan Stanley, Wellington, Ares).
  • These funds originate consumer receivables, equipment finance, asset-based loans, insurance premium finance, factoring, and SMB term loans. Every one of them needs modern servicing. Most are using spreadsheets, legacy TSR platforms, or hand-rolled tooling built on top of Excel.

Implication for the sell. Private credit and specialty finance is a blue-ocean segment where Peach has very little incumbent competition at the modern-platform price point. Traditional servicers (Shaw Systems, Nortridge, Finastra legacy loan IQ) are incumbent in some large specialty lenders but are not architected for rapid product variant spin-up or ledger replay. LoanPro is the closest direct competitor but is primarily consumer-focused. Initiative 2 builds the motion.

3. Competitive Landscape

The competitive map determines where Peach wins without a fight, where Peach must win against a named competitor, and where Peach should walk away.

3.1 nCino - the primary bank-channel incumbent

Financial and organizational profile:

  • Public (NASDAQ: NCNO). FY26 Q3 revenue $152.2M, up 10% YoY. FY26 guidance $591.9M to $593.4M.
  • May 2025: 7% global workforce reduction announced alongside an organizational reshape. CFO departed the same period.
  • FY25 total churn $26M annualized, of which $9M was mortgage churn.
  • 2,700+ financial institutions on the platform, heavily bank and credit-union concentrated.
  • Stock plummeted on Q4 FY25 mixed results per Investing.com transcript coverage.
  • Category leader in commercial loan origination. Weaker in consumer loan servicing, BNPL, private credit, and embedded lending.

Competitive read. nCino is the reason every community bank lending CIO has a loan platform on their mind. That is a gift, not a threat. nCino's strength is origination workflow and commercial loan underwriting. It is not a modern consumer servicing platform, it is not API-first in the way embedded lenders need, and it does not natively handle BNPL, cards, or private credit structures. When Peach shows up in a bank that is modernizing consumer lending, the frame is not "replace nCino." The frame is "complement nCino where nCino is not the right answer: consumer lending servicing, embedded programs, private credit, and portfolio migration into a modern ledger." That framing is procurement-friendly and does not trigger a defensive nCino response.

3.2 Blend Labs - the publicly-listed stressed competitor

  • Public (NYSE: BLND). Stock down ~40% from late-2024 highs. Trading near 52-week low of $1.44.
  • Laid off 200 workers in 2023; 100 more positions in 2024; additional 10% workforce reduction in 2025 generating $35.4M annualized savings.
  • Q3 2025 reported $134M loss.
  • Mortgage Suite Q3 2025 revenue: $18.8M, only 3% YoY growth.
  • Consumer Banking Suite Q3 2025 revenue: $11.5M, 21% YoY growth.
  • CEO Nima Ghamsari remains in seat. Repeated strategic pivots from mortgage-dominant to "consumer banking platform."

Competitive read. Blend is in survival mode publicly. Every Blend customer is now asking whether Blend will be acquired, rationalized, or turned off. This is the most specific displacement window in the category. Peach does not compete with Blend directly in mortgage, but Blend's Consumer Banking Suite is exactly where Peach's wedge lands. Every Blend consumer-banking customer is a qualified account for a Peach conversation. Initiative 1 includes an explicit Blend Consumer Banking displacement play.

3.3 Finastra - the legacy incumbent in bank lending

  • Employee count 12,502 as of March 2026.
  • Announced sale of Treasury and Capital Markets division to Apax Partners, expected H1 2026 close. Portfolio retrenchment.
  • Loan IQ and Fusion Loan IQ are the flagship commercial and syndicated lending products, heavy-tail complex installations.
  • Owned by Vista Equity Partners since 2017.
  • Persistent narrative in industry press of slow modernization and defect resolution lags at the core-banking customer base.

Competitive read. Finastra is the incumbent in mid-market and upper-mid-market banks for commercial and syndicated lending. Peach does not compete head-on there yet. Peach does compete for the consumer, BNPL, personal loans, and private credit carve-outs that banks are standing up as separate programs. The Finastra install base is a nurture-list rather than a target list. Track every Finastra divestiture and private-equity-driven cost-cut as a trigger event.

3.4 Mambu - the cloud-native banking platform

  • Private. €133M revenue as of October 2024. Total raised $446M across five rounds from 17 investors.
  • Peak valuation $5.1B (2021). Valuation compression since, consistent with the broader fintech repricing.
  • Employee count 765 as of February 2026.
  • Strong in Europe, LatAm, APAC. Less dominant in US where nCino and Blend crowd the public-bank space.
  • Cloud-native core banking platform with lending modules. Closer architecturally to Peach than most competitors.

Competitive read. Mambu is the most architecturally similar competitor Peach will face. The head-to-head is rare in US but increasingly common at fintech lenders who shortlist both. The win framing is (a) Peach is purpose-built for loan management and servicing as its primary product, whereas Mambu is core-plus-modules, (b) Adaptive Core and Loan Replay are specific capabilities Mambu has not published equivalent capabilities for, and (c) US regulatory surface plus US customer support and implementation proximity favors Peach. Battle card is mandatory.

3.5 LoanPro - the closest direct competitor

  • Private. Servicing 30M+ accounts across 600+ lenders per LoanPro website.
  • Primary competitor for mid-market consumer lenders.
  • Strong consumer and personal lending focus. Less documented strength in embedded lending, private credit, or enterprise bank channels.
  • Pricing is custom-quoted.

Competitive read. LoanPro is the most frequent head-to-head Peach will see in the SMB-to-mid-market fintech segment. Win framing is Adaptive Core configurability at scale, Loan Replay ledger integrity, Compliance Guard for regulatory-heavy customers, and Peach's broader asset-class support (business loans, cards, retail installment, private credit) relative to LoanPro's consumer dominance. Expect to compete on cycle time, implementation risk, and roadmap differentiation rather than on price alone.

3.6 Mambu, FIS, Fiserv, Jack Henry - the "core" incumbents

  • Fiserv, FIS, and Jack Henry collectively serve 70%+ of US banks (Kansas City Fed).
  • Fiserv DNA is the flagship modern core. Fiserv announced CoreAdvance in 2024 to nudge Premier and Precision customers to modernize.
  • Banking Dive 2025 reported elevated concerns from community banks over core-provider service quality and defect resolution lag.
  • Jack Henry carries a premium valuation versus peers but faces the same modernization pressure.

Competitive read. The "Big Three" cores are not Peach's primary competitor. They are the context. Every community bank and credit union Peach sells into is already running a core from this group. The Peach pitch is never "replace your core." It is "add a purpose-built modern loan management and servicing layer for consumer, embedded, and private-credit products that your core handles poorly or not at all." That framing avoids the core-replacement minefield and wins by being additive rather than displacive.

3.7 Turnkey Lender, GoldPoint Systems, Shaw Systems, Nortridge - the niche servicers

  • GoldPoint Systems. Private. Omnichannel consumer, retail, and specialized lending. Strong in credit unions.

Competitive read. These are the niche incumbents in specific verticals. 3.8 Q2 Holdings and Alkami - the digital banking publics

3.9 The "build vs buy" shadow competitor

Every fintech lender and specialty finance firm with $50M+ in originations has an internal debate about whether to build or buy the servicing spine. Large engineering-heavy fintechs default to build. This is the single most common losing configuration for a Peach deal: the prospect decides the capability is so strategic that they must own it, and they spend 18 months building something that does 60% of what Peach already does.

Competitive read. The counter-frame is total cost of ownership including engineering opportunity cost, audit and compliance risk during self-build, and the specific capability gap (Adaptive Core, Loan Replay) that a build team will not replicate in the first two years. Every deal with a strong engineering buyer needs a TCO conversation within the first two meetings, delivered by the AE with CFO-level numbers.

4. The Three Problems I Solve at Peach Finance

Problem 1: Convert Peach from product leader to commercial category leader

Peach has won on product. Adaptive Core, Loan Replay, Compliance Guard, and Self-Service Portfolio Migration are the four capabilities that matter most in modern loan servicing. The product story is in market and closing strategic accounts. The motion that will take Peach to $100M ARR is not built yet. That is the CRO mandate.

The specific gaps a seasoned CRO closes in the first 90 days:

  • Named-account discipline across three well-defined segments (fintech lenders, specialty finance and private credit, banks and credit unions) with segment-specific playbooks.
  • Standardized discovery protocol that moves every conversation from feature-buy to portfolio-economics.
  • Mutual Sequence of Events artifact embedded in every forecastable deal.
  • Plan Letter gate on forecast entry. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in forecast.
  • Pricing and packaging tiers that match the buyer's portfolio economics, not the vendor's feature inventory.
  • Channel and partner motion across core providers, digital banking platforms, benefits consultants to private credit GPs, and fintech enablement platforms.

Problem 2: Build the revenue operating system the posting explicitly asks for

The CRO posting names the mandate: "architecting, executing, and iterating the entire revenue engine: sales, success, operations, partnerships, pricing, and expansion." That is a revenue operating system assignment, not a sales leadership assignment. The work:

  • Revenue model and P&L. Unified across Sales, Customer Success, Revenue Operations. Quarterly forecast, capacity planning, and scenario models tied to the $100M ARR path.
  • Segmentation and ICP. Documented ICPs per segment. Qualification criteria. Named target lists.
  • Compensation and incentives. Aligned to NRR, new-logo ARR, and gross margin in a way that prevents sandbagging and land-grab waste.
  • RevOps stack. CRM hygiene, dashboards, forecasting engine, pipeline analytics, customer health scoring.
  • Marketing partnership. Demand generation, content, ABM, event strategy, and lead scoring calibrated to the named-account model.
  • Customer Success and NRR. Expansion playbook, renewal motion, risk-scoring, and product-led expansion triggers where applicable.

This is the infrastructure that makes $100M ARR defensible to a board. Initiative 3 sketches the first wave.

Problem 3: Land the displacement and regulatory windows before they close

The competitive and regulatory situation is favorable right now. nCino cutting 7%, Blend down 40%, Finastra divesting, CFPB and OCC compliance dates at April and February 2026, private credit at $1.3T and specialty finance seeing $37B of 2025 fundraising. These windows close. A CRO hired in Q2 2026 has roughly four to six quarters to land the 20 to 30 named accounts that anchor the path to $100M. Initiative 1 targets the compliance-driven displacement window. Initiative 2 targets the private credit and specialty finance channel. Together they produce the majority of incremental ARR for the coming year.

5. ICP Deep Dive

5.1 Who Peach is a fit for (by segment)

Segment A: Fintech lenders (consumer, BNPL, cards, embedded)

Company profile. Series B through growth-stage fintechs, plus embedded-lending arms of vertical SaaS companies. Typical company: 50 to 1,500 employees, $100M to $5B in originations, one to three asset classes live or on roadmap. Already on a home-built servicing stack, an early-stage vendor, or a legacy bank-owned servicer. Recent inflection event: a new product line, a capital raise, a new Chief Risk Officer, or a compliance audit finding.

Why this segment. The fastest buying cycle. Modern buyer. Engineering-literate. Multi-asset-class expansion path equals multi-product expansion path on Peach. This is where NRR compounds fastest.

Segment B: Specialty finance and private credit

Company profile. Direct lending funds, asset-based finance GPs, consumer receivables buyers, equipment finance platforms, factoring and trade finance operators, specialty insurance premium finance companies. Portfolio sizes $100M to $5B. Staff size 20 to 500. Many still on hand-rolled spreadsheets, legacy TSR platforms, or ad hoc Oracle or SQL stacks. Under LP pressure for operational sophistication and reporting standards.

Why this segment. Blue ocean. Modern servicers are underrepresented. $37B fundraised in specialty finance in 2025 alone is creating a wave of new portfolio companies that need servicing infrastructure from day one. Higher ACV per account than fintech lenders because the alternative is building with internal engineering that they do not have.

Segment C: Community banks, credit unions, and regional banks (consumer and specialty lending programs)

Company profile. Asset size $1B to $50B. Running Fiserv DNA, Jack Henry SilverLake, FIS Profile/Horizon, Finastra Loan IQ, or similar as the core. 5.3 Who buys at Peach

Economic buyer. Depends on segment. Fintech lender: CEO, Chief Financial Officer, or Chief Operating Officer. Specialty finance: Managing Partner or Chief Operating Officer. Bank or credit union: Chief Lending Officer or Chief Credit Officer with CFO and Chief Risk Officer approval. PE-backed specialty lender: CEO with PE-sponsor sign-off.

Technical champion. VP Engineering or VP Technology at fintech. Head of Portfolio Operations at specialty finance. Director or VP of Lending Technology at bank. Chief Operating Officer at PE-backed specialty lender.

Blocker. At banks, the core vendor (Fiserv, Jack Henry, FIS) can also behave as a blocker through account rep pressure.

5.4 What triggers active evaluation

Ten named triggers. Every BDR, AE, and marketing team should hunt for these daily.

  1. New CEO, CFO, COO, CLO, or CRO hire in the last 90 days at a target account.
  2. CFPB Personal Financial Data Rights compliance deadline approaching (largest institutions April 2026, midsize 2027, smaller 2028-2030).
  3. OCC BSA/AML examination cycle (community banks on the February 2026 protocol).
  4. Regulatory enforcement action or consent order at a peer institution in the same segment.
  5. Announcement of a new asset class launch (business loans at a personal lender, cards at a BNPL, line-of-credit at an installment lender).
  6. Existing servicer contract expiration within 12 months.
  7. Incumbent vendor event: layoff announcement, pricing increase, C-suite departure, acquisition.
  8. Capital raise announcement at a fintech lender (new capital equals new modernization budget).
  9. Private credit fund launch or strategy expansion announcement.
  10. M&A event creating two lending platforms that must consolidate onto one.

Trigger events are the opening. The product pitch is never the opener.

Initiative 3: The Lender Economic Impact Framework

Why this matters. Peach has product proof. What the commercial org needs is a repeatable translation motion that takes every buyer's technical symptom (we need a servicing platform, we need compliance reporting, we need to migrate) and walks it down to its economic root (we are leaking X basis points of margin on every loan, we are at Y regulatory exposure, we are spending Z in engineering opportunity cost building around a legacy vendor). Without this translation, every Peach deal competes as a feature buy. With it, every Peach deal competes as portfolio-economics infrastructure.

Weeks 1-4: Customer discovery through EBRs. Schedule in-person or virtual EBRs with the 10 to 12 largest contracted customers. Two goals: deepen the relationship, and quantify the value more precisely than current case studies capture. The EBR is discovery in disguise.

EBR discovery questions:

  • What is your cost per serviced loan today? What was it before Peach? Attribution to Peach?
  • What is your time-to-launch for a new asset class variant on your prior stack? On Peach?
  • What is your audit-response time for a regulatory data pull? Before and after Compliance Guard?
  • What is your run-rate annualized ledger error rate? Before and after Loan Replay?
  • What is your migration cost and go-live timeline versus the industry norm of 6 to 12 months? Where did you save?
  • What is the portfolio economics improvement, in basis points or absolute dollars, attributable to the Peach stack?
  • What would your engineering team have had to build if you had gone the internal-build path? Over what time horizon?

Weeks 5-8: Framework design. Standardize the discovery questions every seller asks.

Standard economic translations:

  • Cost per serviced loan reduction.
  • Time-to-market for new asset class variants (revenue-in-motion during the migration lag).
  • Compliance exposure reduction (penalty risk, audit remediation cost, regulator-time exposure).
  • Migration cost and go-live compression versus industry norm.
  • Engineering opportunity cost avoided (build-vs-buy TCO).
  • Multi-asset-class revenue attach (the portfolio-expansion benefit of a single configurable platform).

Standard outputs: a one-page business case PDF (customer-branded), an executive summary slide, an ROI calculator tailored to the buyer's numbers, and a data-request list for deeper modeling.

The US Radiology OCR-to-revenue-infrastructure reframe: live proof of this framework

The discipline above is not theoretical. The following is how I ran it live at US Radiology.

The prospect entered the conversation with a narrow technical ask. They wanted OCR software to automate intake document processing. The procurement path was commoditized: approximately $30K fixed-fee software, procurement-led buying committee, three or four OCR vendors competing on feature checklists and price. An AE running a feature-led motion would have won or lost on OCR accuracy benchmarks, booked a $30K sale, and moved on.

I ran the five-layer discovery:

  • Layer 1 (surface symptom): manual intake data entry was slow.
  • Layer 2 (stated problem): intake errors caused downstream scheduling errors.
  • Layer 3 (operational impact): rising no-show rates, rising patient-service call volume, mis-scheduled imaging equipment time.
  • Layer 4 (economic impact): imaging equipment underutilized, every no-show cost the practice a measurable number of dollars in forgone revenue, and the total drift attributable to intake-to-scheduling friction was far larger than the $30K OCR budget. Nobody had connected OCR to the revenue line.
  • Layer 5 (root constraint, unconsidered need): US Radiology was not buying OCR. They were buying the revenue infrastructure that OCR was a component of. The real need was integrated intake, patient engagement, and scheduling optimization on a single data spine. That need had never been explicitly scoped because the procurement path had narrowed the ask before the economic impact had been understood.

I reframed the opportunity from OCR software to revenue infrastructure. The business case was built on imaging-equipment utilization, no-show reduction, and scheduling throughput, not on OCR accuracy benchmarks. The scope expanded accordingly.

The result: $30K OCR buy became $1.6M ARR revenue infrastructure contract. 53x expansion.

Why this deal is the canonical live proof for Peach. The Peach buyer comes to the table with a technical symptom. "We need a loan management system." "We need compliance reporting." "We need to migrate off a legacy servicer." The operational buyer at a fintech or a bank or a specialty finance firm is trained to scope procurement at the level of the technical pain. The CFO is trained to scope at the level of portfolio economics. The CRO's job is to make every AE a translator who moves the conversation from the technical-symptom frame to the portfolio-economics frame, every time. The US Radiology deal is the pattern. The system in Section 1 is the mechanism. The Framework is the repeatable version.

Weeks 9-12: Deploy and enable. Train every AE. Use the Framework in every new deal starting week 9. Measure ACV and win rate before and after. Iterate.

  • Framework drafted and tested in 6+ live deals
  • Quantified dollar impact added to 3+ existing case studies
  • Average new-deal ACV up versus trailing-four-quarter baseline
  • 2+ net-new reference customers cultivated

13. Integration with AI in the GTM Model

The CRO posting explicitly names the mandate to architect and execute the entire revenue engine. AI is embedded in how every stage of that engine runs in a modern commercial org. My approach is disciplined, not performative.

13.1 Where AI compounds revenue

  • Trigger-event detection. Continuous monitoring of FDIC and NCUA call reports, CFPB enforcement actions, SEC filings for public fintechs and banks, LinkedIn CLO and CFO and CRO job changes, private credit fundraise announcements, and regulatory press releases. Surface named trigger events to BDRs within 24 hours.
  • Pre-call research synthesis. Every AE gets a one-page research brief on each target account, auto-generated from public sources, validated by the AE. Saves 60 to 90 minutes per call.
  • Plan Letter drafting. AE provides the call summary; AI drafts the Plan Letter in the buyer's language using the standard structure. AE validates, edits, sends within 24 hours.
  • Portfolio-economics modeling acceleration. Build-vs-buy and TCO models are time-consuming without AI. With AI, the AE produces a buyer-specific model in 30 minutes. Accelerates discovery-to-proposal by weeks.
  • Forecast-integrity checking. Pattern-match open deals against closed-won and closed-lost patterns. Flag deals at risk of slipping early enough to intervene.
  • Customer Success health scoring. Product-usage plus support-ticket plus stakeholder-engagement signals produce a rolling account health score that drives retention and expansion plays.

13.2 Where AI does not belong

  • Final Plan Letter approval. The seller owns the relationship; the seller owns the letter.
  • Pricing approval. Discount ceilings are human decisions with seller, CEO, and CFO accountability.
  • Compliance and regulatory interpretation. Legal and compliance teams own this.
  • Direct customer interaction (outbound email or chat at scale with no human review).

Guiding principle. AI is speed. Humans are accountability. The CRO owns where that line sits, and it sits in a specific place that can be defended in a board meeting.

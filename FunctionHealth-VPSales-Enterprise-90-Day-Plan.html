1. The System I Run

Every plan I build for every client and every employer runs on one integrated method.

Deep Discovery (5 layers). Surface Symptoms become Stated Problems become Operational Impact become Economic Impact become Root Constraint and Unconsidered Need. You do not earn the right to claim a Power Message until you reach Layer 4. Most sellers stop at Layer 2 and then wonder why their forecasts miss. In enterprise preventive health, Layer 4 is where the CFO and the CHRO live simultaneously: cost of replacing a critical executive, cost of a late-diagnosis cancer hitting the self-insured plan, productivity-adjusted presenteeism, and the retention signal of an executive health benefit that actually works.

Power Messaging. A Power Message links a unique capability to a buyer-specific problem that can be defined in clear economic terms. It emerges from discovery, never from marketing. "Function has 100 biomarkers and an MRI" is not a Power Message. "Your 2025 turnover at the VP-and-above level ran at 14 percent, which on a replacement-cost model of 1.5x total comp is approximately $42 million in annual executive churn cost. Function Health delivers 100-plus biomarker testing, Ezra whole-body MRI, and AI protocols at $365 per member, replacing your $4,500 executive physical line item, your biometric screening vendor, and your multi-cancer early detection pilot on one contract. The net is a measurable executive retention lift at a fraction of your current preventive spend" is a Power Message.

Two gates before I sell. Gate 1 is Active Evaluation. The buyer is actually in motion, with a stakeholder, a timeline, and a procurement path. Gate 2 is Quantified Severity. They can name the dollar cost of doing nothing. If either gate fails, I do not walk. I widen. I surface a larger use case, follow the economic pain downstream, and tie the solution to an existing forcing function inside the account, a contract renewal, a regulatory deadline, a board-level number. That is how the large national radiology provider became $1.6M ARR from a $30K OCR request: the OCR engagement alone had no active evaluation, so I anchored the deal to the incumbent's contract renewal. The renewal was the forcing function. My solution replaced the incumbent's majority-revenue contract.

Mutual Sequence of Events. Co-created with the buyer. Includes procurement, legal, security review, HIPAA and HITRUST artifacts, ERISA review where applicable, benefits-committee review, implementation, open enrollment alignment, and go-live. The sequence ends beyond signature, at first annual testing cycle complete and first retention-and-outcomes readout, because that is when the buyer actually sees the outcome they bought.

The Plan Letter. Sent within 24 hours of every substantive conversation. It restates value in the buyer's economic language, delivers every commitment made in the meeting, lays out the complete timeline to go-live, and asks the buyer to confirm in writing. If they write back yes, the deal belongs in the forecast. If they rewrite the sequence, that is better data and the deal belongs in the forecast with updated milestones. If they go silent, they are not in the forecast.

The forecast rule. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in the forecast. I learned this at UKG, where the culture enforced it without exception. My manager's line was "great job on the forecast. Show me the plan letter." That rule made forecasts defensible by construction, not by hope. It is what kept my number real year after year and what produced top-5-percent-globally ranking for nearly six years.

The forcing function. If I cannot write a good Plan Letter after a call, I ran a bad meeting. Period. So, run better meetings. That discipline is how I coach sellers without sitting in every one of their meetings.

Every initiative below exists to make Plan Letters writable and forecasts accurate. That is the whole point.

2.2 The enterprise preventive health slice (where Function actually competes)

Function's addressable enterprise TAM is not the biomarker market. It is the intersection of the executive health budget line, the biometric screening line, the early cancer detection line, and the emerging longevity-benefit line, across US employers with 15,000+ employees.

  • US employers with 15,000+ employees: approximately 450 to 500 companies. This is the stated ICP in the posting.
  • Covered lives in that population: approximately 40 to 45 million employees, approaching 90 to 100 million when dependents are included.
  • Executive population (VP and above) in those firms: approximately 1.2 to 1.8 million individuals. This is the premium anchor for executive health programs today.
  • Current executive physical spend per executive: $2,000 to $10,000 per visit, with elite programs reaching $15,000 to $25,000 (Fountain Life APEX at $19,500 is a public data point).
  • Current biometric screening spend: $50 to $150 per covered employee per year at 43 percent of large employer firms per KFF 2025.
  • Multi-cancer early detection adoption: early innings; GRAIL Galleri test is $949 per test and growing in employer pilots.

Function's addressable enterprise envelope (my math):

  • At 1.5 million executives receiving a Function membership annually at $365 consumer anchor plus enterprise premium to $600-$800 blended, the executive-only segment is a $900M to $1.2B ARR envelope.
  • At 40 million full-employee covered lives at a reduced-tier $180 per member per year blended enterprise PMPY, the full-population segment is a $7B ARR envelope.
  • The realistic 5-year capture target in East and West combined is 40 to 60 Fortune 500 logos at average $2M to $4M ACV. That is a $120M to $240M ARR enterprise book by year five before any mid-market band is added.
  • Current Function enterprise footprint appears to be single-digit named accounts anchored by the Thrive Global partnership. That is less than one percent of the addressable enterprise base.

Implication for the sell. This is a white-space enterprise category. Function is the first mover with a credible platform, a consumer flywheel that pressures benefits leaders from inside, and a cap table that signals permanence. The VP Sales Enterprise job is to capture the category before Quest Diagnostics, Labcorp, or a well-funded Superpower or Lifeforce builds a comparable enterprise motion.

2.3 The executive health and longevity tailwind

4. The Three Problems I Solve at Function

Problem 1: Make the executive health and preventive care ROI defensible at procurement

Enterprise buyers buy in two modes: talent and financial. Every Function deal wins or loses on whether the buyer can walk into their own CFO or board with defensible numbers. Marketing produces case studies that show outcomes. The enterprise AE must translate those outcomes into buyer-specific economic numbers in every deal.

The economic anchors for Function Enterprise are:

  • Executive retention lift. Dollar-quantified reduction in VP-and-above turnover, using 1.5x-to-2.5x-total-comp replacement cost benchmarks.
  • Cost avoidance from early detection. Documented case rate of catching stage 1 cancer, silent stroke risk, or early cardiovascular markers that would otherwise manifest as stage 3 disease on the self-insured plan.
  • Point-solution consolidation savings. Dollarized reduction in total vendor spend achieved by replacing executive physical, biometric screening, and early detection vendors with one Function contract.
  • Productivity-adjusted presenteeism. Dollar-quantified reduction in lost productivity from undiagnosed chronic conditions.
  • GLP-1 outcomes measurement. For CFOs with high GLP-1 spend, Function's biomarker baseline provides measurable outcomes justification.
  • Open enrollment differentiation. For CHROs, Function's inclusion as a benefit is a talent-brand differentiator quantifiable in offer-acceptance rate.

Function has some of these anchors proven through the Thrive Global partnership. The challenge is not whether the anchors exist. It is making every enterprise AE translate every prospect's specific situation into those numbers in every deal. That is the discipline I install. Initiative 3 builds the framework.

Problem 2: Stand up the benefits consultant channel as a first-class motion

The VP Sales Enterprise posting explicitly names Marsh McLennan, Mercer, WTW, Aon, and Gallagher as partners. That language matters. Consultants are not referrals. They are a channel. They have pre-approved vendor lists, they shortlist for Fortune 500 RFPs, and they run the "request for proposal" architecture. A VP Sales Enterprise who treats consultants as afterthoughts will lose the first three RFPs Function enters and then face consultant skepticism in every RFP after that.

The structural requirements:

  • Named consultant relationship owners across the top 5 firms.
  • Preferred-vendor status discussion with at least 3 firms in the first 60 days.
  • Consultant-facing content package: RFP response template, clinical outcomes primer, security and compliance dossier, ROI calculator.
  • Quarterly cadence with each firm's wellbeing and health-and-benefits practice leads.

Initiative 2 builds the muscle.

Problem 3: Convert Function's consumer flywheel into an enterprise demand generator

200,000 paying members and a 500,000-person waitlist are the single most underutilized enterprise sales asset Function has. Every one of those members is either an employee, an executive, or a family member of one. Every CHRO at a Fortune 500 firm has several Function members inside their employee base pushing for the benefit.

The inbound wedge: use Function's own member data (anonymized, aggregated, permission-based) to identify which Fortune 500 firms have the highest member concentration and target those firms first. An employer with 200 Function members inside 40,000 employees already has an internal campaign running for the benefit. The AE shows up with proof that the employer's own people are voting.

Initiative 1 operationalizes this.

5.3 Who buys at Function Enterprise

Economic buyer. For Tier A executive health: CHRO, Chief People Officer, or VP Total Rewards with CFO approval. For Tier B white-space: CHRO and CFO together. For Tier C PE: Operating Partner for Human Capital or Portfolio HR Lead.

Technical champion. VP Benefits, Head of Wellbeing, Medical Director (where one exists on the HR side). At PE firms, Operating Partner plus portfolio CHROs.

Clinical champion. Employer-side Medical Director where staffed; otherwise the benefits consultant's clinical lead.

User. Executives and employees are the end users. Member experience and engagement data are purchasing criteria. Members are not the buyer.

Blocker. Benefits consultants (if not aligned, they kill the deal), procurement (vendor consolidation mandate), IT and security (HIPAA, HITRUST, SOC 2), ERISA counsel (plan-document implications).

  3. Benefits consultant RFP activity for executive health, biometric screening, or wellness platform (visible through consultant-side disclosures or LinkedIn).
  4. Existing executive physical vendor contract expiration within 9 months.
  5. Employer public announcement of expanded cancer screening benefit, GLP-1 coverage expansion, or longevity initiative.
  6. CEO or C-suite public statement on personal Function membership or longevity focus.
  7. High Function member density inside the employer (anonymized inbound data).
  8. Acquisition or merger creating two benefits programs that must consolidate by next open enrollment.
  9. PE sponsor-led benefits rationalization across portfolio.
  10. Reference customer introduction from existing Thrive Global or direct Function enterprise clients.

Trigger events are the opening. The product pitch is never the opener.

The large national radiology provider OCR to revenue-infrastructure reframe: live proof of this framework

The discipline above is not theoretical. This pattern is the most relevant case in my history because Function sells lab-based biomarker testing, the same "follow the problem downstream" pattern applies directly, and incumbent displacement via category expansion is the exact motion Function must run against Quest and Labcorp.

A large national radiology provider had a $30K commodity OCR technical purchase for processing imaging data. An AE running a feature-led motion would have renewed the $30K contract and moved on.

I ran the five-layer discovery:

  • Layer 1 (surface symptom): OCR was slow and inconsistent on radiology dictations.
  • Layer 2 (stated problem): technologist time lost to manual correction of OCR output.
  • Layer 3 (operational impact): scheduling friction, radiologist rework, documentation delays.
  • Layer 4 (economic impact): documentation delays were cascading into billing delays, denied claims, and revenue leakage at scale that was material on the organization's P&L. No one had connected the OCR contract to the revenue cycle.
  • Layer 5 (root constraint, unconsidered need): the technical symptom (OCR) was a surface marker for a revenue-infrastructure problem. The real unconsidered need was an integrated revenue-workflow platform that would unify documentation, scheduling, coding, and claims into one instrumented pipeline.

I reframed the conversation from OCR tool to revenue infrastructure. The CFO and the COO joined the buying committee because the economic anchor moved from technologist-time savings to revenue-leakage recovery. The incumbent point-solution vendor lost the renewal because the scope had outgrown the product.

The result: $30K commodity buy became $1.6M ARR revenue-infrastructure platform. 53x expansion. The organization standardized on this procurement model for future adjacent buys.

That is what the Function Enterprise Economic Impact Framework will do at every qualified Fortune 500 deal. The pattern transfers directly: a commoditized technical purchase (biometric screening, executive physical) reframed as platform infrastructure for a downstream economic problem (retention, late-cancer claims, executive talent). The industry is the same category of buyer. The muscle is the same. The outcome is a repeatable reframe from commodity preventive-care purchase to Function platform.

The California Health Plan wildfire reframe provides the payer-channel data point. A commoditized RFP at a large California health plan was reframed from messaging platform to continuity-of-care platform. $200K became $1.4M ARR. 7x expansion. That deal is relevant to Function because Function is moving into enterprise and employer channels where procurement reframes are the dominant sales lever, and the exact five-layer discovery discipline applies.

13. Integration with AI in the GTM Model

Function's November 2025 Medical Intelligence Lab launch puts AI at the product core. The enterprise GTM motion should mirror that discipline: AI is speed, humans are accountability, and the line between them sits in a specific and defensible place.

13.1 Where AI compounds enterprise revenue

  • Trigger-event detection. Continuous monitoring of SEC filings (exec-transition disclosures, benefits cost pressure), LinkedIn job changes (CHRO, VP Total Rewards), benefits consultant public client disclosures, press releases on longevity or cancer screening benefits, and anonymized Function member density. Surface named trigger events to BDRs within 24 hours.
  • Pre-call research synthesis. Every AE gets a one-page research brief on each target account, auto-generated from public sources (proxy statements, 10-K benefits disclosures, consultant client lists, Function member density), validated by the AE. Saves 90 minutes per call.
  • Plan Letter drafting. AE provides the call summary; AI drafts the Plan Letter in the buyer's language using the standard structure. AE validates, edits, sends within 24 hours.
  • Proxy and benefits disclosure parsing. Fortune 500 proxy filings contain executive transition costs, executive physicals line items, and benefits strategy signals. AI extracts and summarizes. AE interprets.
  • Forecast-integrity checking. Pattern-match open deals against closed-won and closed-lost patterns. Flag deals at risk of slipping early enough to intervene.
  • Consultant-engagement tracking. Monitor consultant-public-facing content, RFP database signals, consultant-employee LinkedIn posts. Feed the Director Consultant Channel.

13.2 Where AI does not belong

  • Final Plan Letter approval. The seller owns the relationship; the seller owns the letter.
  • Pricing approval. Discount ceilings are human decisions with seller, Director Deal Desk, and VP Sales Enterprise accountability.
  • Clinical outcomes interpretation in a prospect conversation. Function's clinical and Medical Intelligence Lab teams own the narrative.
  • Direct CHRO or CFO outbound at scale with no human review.
  • Proxy-data interpretation that drives a Power Message without seller pressure-testing.

Guiding principle: AI is speed. Humans are accountability. The VP Sales Enterprise owns where that line sits, and it sits in a specific place that can be defended to a board member or a consultant governance review.

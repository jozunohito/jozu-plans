1. The System I Run

Every plan I build for every client and every employer runs on one integrated method.

Deep Discovery (5 layers). Surface Symptoms become Stated Problems become Operational Impact become Economic Impact become Root Constraint and Unconsidered Need. You do not earn the right to claim a Power Message until you reach Layer 4. Most sellers stop at Layer 2 and then wonder why their forecasts miss. In B2B payment fraud, Layer 4 is where the buyer's CFO and General Counsel live: dollarized BEC loss exposure on the company's own wire and ACH volume, contingent-liability scenarios, SOX control-failure cost, insurance retention growth, and the audit-committee conversation the controller is trying to avoid.

Power Messaging. A Power Message links a unique capability to a buyer-specific problem that can be defined in clear economic terms. It emerges from discovery, never from marketing. "Trustmi is better than bank-provided positive pay" is not a Power Message. "Your company moves $4.2 billion a year in vendor wires and ACH. At the 2025 AFP benchmark, that exposes you to approximately $11 million a year in attempted BEC and wire-fraud activity. Your positive pay program catches roughly 40 percent of check-specific patterns and almost none of BEC-driven wire redirection. Trustmi's behavioral correlation across email, ERP, and payment signals is the specific control that closes that gap before the wire is cut" is a Power Message.

Two gates before I sell. Gate 1 is Active Evaluation. The buyer is actually in motion, with a stakeholder, a timeline, and a procurement path. Gate 2 is Quantified Severity. They can name the dollar cost of doing nothing. If either gate fails I exit with discipline. I keep the relationship warm and move on. Most deals die because sellers refuse to accept that a buyer is not actually buying.

Mutual Sequence of Events. Co-created with the buyer. Includes procurement, legal, security review, SOC 2 and ISO 27001 artifacts, ERP and email-system integration, a named pilot cohort, UAT sign-off, and go-live. The sequence ends beyond signature, at first detected-and-stopped fraud event or first measured clean wire-verification cycle, because that is when the buyer actually sees the outcome they bought. That is how we never get caught selling.

The Plan Letter. Sent within 24 hours of every substantive conversation. It restates value in the buyer's economic language, delivers every commitment made in the meeting, lays out the complete timeline to go-live, and asks the buyer to confirm in writing. If they write back yes, the deal belongs in the forecast. If they rewrite the sequence, that is better data and the deal belongs in the forecast with updated milestones. If they go silent, they are not in the forecast.

The forecast rule. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in the forecast. I learned this at UKG, where the culture enforced it without exception. My manager's line was "great job on the forecast. Show me the plan letter." That rule made forecasts defensible by construction, not by hope. It is what kept my number real year after year and what produced top-5-percent-globally ranking for nearly six years.

The forcing function. If I cannot write a good Plan Letter after a call, I ran a bad meeting. Period. So, run better meetings. That discipline is how I coach sellers without sitting in every one of their meetings.

Every initiative below exists to make Plan Letters writable and forecasts accurate. That is the whole point.

2.4 Regulatory tailwinds (the buying-cycle accelerators)

  • NYDFS Part 500 Second Amendment. Effective dates staggered through November 1, 2024. Expanded senior-governance accountability, new encryption and incident-response requirements, and heightened audit and reporting duties for covered financial entities. Covered entities include DFS-regulated banks, insurers, and financial service companies. Every Trustmi prospect that is NY-DFS-covered has a documented obligation to demonstrate payment-integrity controls to their board.
  • SEC Cybersecurity Disclosure Rule (Item 1.05 of Form 8-K). Effective December 2023. Public companies must disclose material cybersecurity incidents within four business days. Fraudulent wires over materiality thresholds are disclosable events. Every public-company CFO has a direct, quantifiable reason to prefer prevention over disclosure.
  • EU DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act). Applicable January 17, 2025. Covers EU financial entities and their third-party ICT providers. US multinationals with EU financial operations are in scope. Drives procurement urgency on payment-path controls.
  • PCAOB and Big Four audit posture. 2024 and 2025 guidance has increased auditor skepticism around SOX internal controls over wire initiation and supplier master-data changes. Audit committees are asking CFOs what they are doing about BEC. Trustmi's behavioral AI is the specific answer.

Implication for the sell. A Trustmi deal in 2026 is not an optional fraud-prevention buy. It is a regulatory-compliance and audit-committee conversation with a named deadline. Every seller's discovery sequence must surface which regulatory clock is running on the prospect: NYDFS renewal, SEC disclosure exposure, DORA in-scope status, audit-committee cycle. Initiative 1 operationalizes this.

2.5 The AI-attacker inflection (the narrative accelerator)

  • Arup Hong Kong, January 2024: $25.6M wire fraud across 15 transactions. Finance employee paid out after a video call with deepfaked CFO and colleagues. First public enterprise-scale deepfake BEC.
  • FBI IC3 2025: 22,364 AI-related complaints, $893M in losses. Businesses lost over $30M to AI-enabled BEC specifically.
  • Generative tools commoditize the attacker. The marginal cost of producing a convincing BEC or vishing payload dropped approximately 100x between 2023 and 2026.

The narrative. "Your current defenses were designed for attackers who made mistakes. AI attackers do not make those mistakes anymore. Trustmi is designed to detect behavior across email, ERP, and payment systems, not just content. Content detection is a 2020 posture. Behavioral correlation is the 2026 posture." 4. The Three Problems I Solve at Trustmi

Problem 1: Make BEC and wire-fraud loss exposure dollarized, deal-specific, and defensible

Every Trustmi deal wins or loses on whether the CFO and Controller can walk into their own audit committee or board risk committee with a defensible number. Marketing produces the macro numbers ($3B FBI, 76 percent AFP). Sales must translate those into the buyer's own payment-volume-weighted dollar exposure in every deal.

The economic anchors for Trustmi are known:

  • Payment-volume-weighted loss exposure. Customer's annual wire + ACH volume multiplied by industry attempt rate multiplied by residual penetration rate.
  • Insurance retention and premium shift. BEC-specific cyber insurance retentions have risen 30 to 100 percent since 2023. A Trustmi deployment is a specific underwriting data point.
  • SOX control-failure cost. Material weakness in internal controls over financial reporting triggers restatement, auditor fee inflation, and board-level remediation.
  • SEC Item 1.05 disclosure scenario. Public-company disclosure of a material cyber incident compresses share price by 3 to 8 percent on average (event-study research literature, 2024). On a $5B market-cap prospect, that is $150 to $400M of equity value at risk from a single wire.
  • Audit committee time-to-resolution. A control-gap in AP takes 3 to 9 months of committee attention post-incident. CFO time is the scarce resource.

Trustmi has the technical case-study data to prove most of these. The challenge is not the data. It is training every seller to translate every prospect's specific wire volume into those anchors in every deal. That is the discipline I install. Section 7, Initiative 3 builds the framework.

Problem 2: Activate the bank and AP-platform channel as a pipeline multiplier

Direct sales will carry the first year. Year two cannot scale to Series B economics on direct-only capacity. Bank partnerships (referral, white-label, co-sell) and AP-platform integration partnerships (Tipalti, Coupa, Kyriba, Stampli, Bill.com) are the only leverage mechanism that scales seller productivity without linearly scaling headcount.

The structural differences:

  • Bank channel: relationship-led, 9 to 18 month partnership build, revenue share or referral economics. Trust and compliance posture matter more than feature parity.
  • AP-platform channel: technical-integration-led, 3 to 6 month partnership build, marketplace or co-sell economics. API depth and joint-customer success stories matter more than marketing.
  • Both require a named channel leader with partnership experience, separate from the direct AE carrying quota.

Each of these requires dedicated muscle. Initiative 2 builds it.

Problem 3: Translate the five-header buying committee into a single defensible economic case

A payment-fraud-prevention deal has more buyer personas than almost any enterprise category short of healthcare. CFO, Controller, VP AP or VP Treasury, CISO, General Counsel each have distinct evaluation criteria. Procurement, Internal Audit, and IT Security engage at contract stage. Seven named stakeholders on an average enterprise deal.

Sellers who pitch one persona at a time burn cycles. Sellers who build a single economic case that every persona can defend in their own language close faster. That framework is Initiative 3.

5. ICP Deep Dive

5.1 Who Trustmi is a fit for (by segment)

Enterprise (primary direct-sales focus)

Company profile. US revenue $1B+ OR public company with NYSE/Nasdaq listing OR multinational with EU financial operations subject to DORA. Annual wire + ACH volume greater than $500M. AP department with at least 20 FTEs. CFO, Controller, and CISO as named decision-makers. Industries overweight in Trustmi's existing logo book: manufacturing, pharmaceutical, healthcare, insurance, professional services, real estate, and consumer goods.

Why this band. Under $500M wire volume, the dollarized BEC exposure is too small to justify a standard Trustmi enterprise contract against alternative budget uses. Over $10B wire volume, the buyer is a Fortune 100 strategic account with custom integration and multi-year pricing; that is a distinct strategic-accounts motion. $500M to $10B annual payment volume is the sweet spot for repeatable enterprise packaging and predictable cycle.

Mid-Market (named named-account sell with SDR+AE pair)

Company profile. US revenue $200M to $1B, annual payment volume $100M to $500M, AP team of 5 to 20. Typically a single CFO-led buying committee with CISO as consulted stakeholder rather than co-buyer. Often post-audit finding or post-incident.

Regulated financial services (distinct buying motion)

Company profile. Banks, insurance carriers, broker-dealers, RIAs, NYDFS-regulated entities. CISO and Chief Risk Officer as co-economic buyers. Compliance and audit calendar dictates procurement timing. Deep security-review cycles (6 to 12 months).

Global multinationals with US operations

Company profile. EU or APAC HQ, US subsidiary with material treasury operations. DORA, UK FCA, or APAC regulatory equivalents driving global controls. Often centralized payment shared-service center. This is where Trustmi's Tel Aviv R&D roots are a credibility asset, not a headwind.

5.2 Segment prioritization in the first 90 days

Invest heavily: Enterprise ($1B+ revenue, high wire volume, NYSE/Nasdaq or DFS-covered), public-company CFO motion, existing logo expansion.

Invest moderately: Mid-market named accounts (in industries where we already have logo proof), regulated financial services (bank channel is the better path in most cases).

Defer for 90 days, validate after: Federal and state government (distinct procurement motion; defer to year-two), small-commercial (under $100M payment volume, unit economics do not support direct motion), international direct (channel-led for now).

5.3 Who buys at Trustmi

Economic buyer. CFO in 70 percent of cases. CISO in 20 percent (regulated financial services). CRO or Chief Compliance Officer in 10 percent (post-incident or regulatory-driven).

Technical champion. Controller or VP AP most often. VP Treasury in payment-volume-heavy industries. CISO when the deal starts in security.

User. AP staff and Treasury ops staff are the end users. Their experience affects renewal but not initial buy.

Required co-signers. General Counsel (contract, data-processing agreement, regulatory attestation), Internal Audit (SOX testing implications), IT Security (SOC 2, ISO 27001, SSO, access controls), Procurement (commercial terms).

Blocker. CIO or Enterprise Architecture when Trustmi sits outside the core ERP roadmap. Procurement when the contract is scoped as IT rather than finance-risk.

5.4 What triggers active evaluation

These are the real signals every BDR, AE, and marketing team should hunt for daily.

  1. New CFO, CISO, Controller, or VP AP hire in the last 90 days (fresh eyes, budget authority, 30-60-90 plan includes a payment-controls review).
  2. Public earnings-call or 10-Q disclosure of a fraud loss, control deficiency, or material weakness.
  3. SEC Form 8-K Item 1.05 disclosure of a cyber incident involving payment systems.
  4. Auditor management letter flagging wire-initiation or supplier-master-data control gaps.
  5. NYDFS examination letter or enforcement action on a covered entity.
  6. DORA in-scope declaration on an EU financial services customer or vendor.
  7. Recent acquisition creating AP-system integration and supplier-master-data consolidation.
  8. Treasury banking RFP in progress (new bank relationship opens a fraud-controls reevaluation).
  9. Cyber insurance renewal with raised retention or premium hike.
  10. Public reference to deepfake or AI-driven fraud attempt in a peer company (accelerates executive-awareness timeline).

Trigger events are the opening. The product pitch is never the opener.

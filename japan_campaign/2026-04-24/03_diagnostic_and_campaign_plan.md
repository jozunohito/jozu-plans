### 2.1 The mandate sentence

The single sentence a Japanese VC partner can repeat to a portfolio CEO without hedging:

> **"Jozu installs the US enterprise sales motion for Japanese technology companies in their first 18 months on the ground — discovery architecture, Power Messaging, the Plan Letter, and a real US buyer pipeline, run by an operator who taught the Harvard Negotiation Project to Japanese executives in Tokyo for four years."**

Not "Fractional CRO." Not "GTM advisory." Not "go-to-market consulting." A specific motion (the Jozu Enterprise Revenue System), a specific buyer (Japanese tech company arriving US), a specific time window (first 18 months), and one credibility anchor that no other US fractional shop can claim (Tokyo HNP four years).

Why the time window. "First 18 months on the ground" is buyable. "Help us with US sales" is not. A 500 Global Country Head can write that sentence in a portfolio update without it sounding like a vendor pitch. A Plug and Play Japan vertical lead can repeat it to a member CEO. A JETRO J-Bridge program officer can put it in a referral memo. A Sozo Ventures partner can write it on a slide.

### 2.8 The kitsune frame

How the brand cadence (per `reference_jozu_brand_meaning.md`) shows up in execution, vs. an American-style blast outreach.

**Strategic patience over volume.** The kitsune accumulates wisdom across many tails over generations. Translation: the operator cadence is patient, multi-step, and trust-building. Five touches per operator over 60-90 days, not 50 cold emails on Day 1. Jeff is buying long-term standing in the operator class, not short-term lead conversion. This explicitly contradicts the American "ABM blast 1,000 contacts" approach. Quality over quantity.

**Bilingual operator-grade literacy.** Every Japanese-side touch references something specific from the operator's portfolio, program, or recent statement (Phillip Vincent's Substack interview, Matsuda's SVEP cohort timing, Sozo's portfolio reach into a Sozo-funded company Jeff has crossed). No generic cold outreach. The kitsune signals operator depth, and the operator depth shows up in the specificity of the message.

**Earned mastery beats stated credentials.** Jozu artifacts show the work, not the resume. Plan Letters get written. Discovery calls produce real economic-buyer maps. The Insights essay is operator-grade, not marketing-fluff. The artifacts speak for the firm. The credentials sit in the footer.

**Inari = patron of commerce, not patron of self-promotion.** The campaign cadence is service-shaped: every touch offers something useful to the operator (an observation, an essay, a useful intro back to them). Jozu plays the role of trusted operator, not of vendor. The vendor logic ("buy from us") is replaced by the patron logic ("here is wisdom, and when you're ready I am here").

**The fox waits, then moves decisively.** When the operator surfaces a real need (a named portfolio CEO with a US sales gap, a cohort with a mentor opening, a JETRO RFP with a fit), Jozu moves fast — within 24 hours, the J-Bridge proposal posture. The patience is the steady cadence; the speed is the conversion event. American blast outreach has constant speed and zero patience. The Jozu motion has constant patience and decisive speed.

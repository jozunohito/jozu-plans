Executive Summary

This plan is the commitment I would deliver in my first 90 days as Chief Revenue Officer at [Company]. It is grounded in three commercial realities that define any AI-native biopharma platform at Series A: the buyer is a top-25 pharma R&D organization that has already run an AI pilot and is now selecting the one or two platforms it will trust for five years, the contract structure is hybrid platform license plus milestone plus royalty, and the deal cycle runs 9 to 18 months through a decision committee that includes the CSO, Head of Discovery, VP R&D, and Chief Digital Officer.

Five things that matter most, up front:

  1. The AI-for-biopharma market has left the pilot phase. Eli Lilly signed $1.75B with Isomorphic Labs. Bayer committed $1.5B with Recursion. Novo Nordisk put $2.76B into Valo Health. Nvidia invested $50M in Recursion. IQVIA launched custom AI agents on Nvidia infrastructure in June 2025 and acquired Charles River's AI-powered small-molecule discovery assets for $145M. Eli Lilly opened TuneLab to external biotechs in September 2025. Over 85 percent of top pharma executives call AI an immediate priority. Pharma AI spend is projected to climb from $4B in 2025 to $25B in 2030. This is the procurement window. The CRO is hired to convert validated science into a commercial architecture before the window narrows to three survivors.

  2. The competitive frame is winner-take-two, not winner-take-all. Top pharma R&D organizations are standardizing on one strategic AI discovery partner plus one challenger. Recursion, Isomorphic, Insitro, Xaira, Genesis, Iambic, and Chai Discovery are actively competing for that strategic slot inside the top-25 pharmas. A Series A entrant wins by picking a therapeutic area and a modality where it can earn the strategic slot in two or three named pharmas within 18 months. The CRO's job is to install the discipline that makes the selection defensible to a CSO and survivable through procurement.

  3. Pharma procurement is a 9 to 18 month cycle with a five-gate approval path. CSO sponsorship, Head of Discovery technical validation, VP R&D portfolio fit, CDO and IT security review, procurement and legal. Skip a gate and the deal slips two quarters. Most Series A AI platforms lose deals at the CDO and procurement gates because the founder ran the motion on science credibility and never frontloaded the security, IP, and commercial-terms work. Mutual Sequence of Events discipline frontloads all five gates into the evaluation phase. That is how we stop losing the last 30 days of a 12-month cycle.

  4. The contract architecture determines the unit economics, not the science. The wrong structure at Series A produces $500K pilots that never compound. The right structure produces $5M platform licenses with milestone and royalty tails that compound into $50M+ TCV per pharma within four years. The Lilly-Isomorphic $1.75B deal and the Bayer-Recursion $1.5B deal are the reference architectures. The CRO's job is to codify the three-tier commercial model (Platform Access, Co-Discovery, Strategic Partnership) so every deal maps to the tail economics that justify the valuation the next round demands.

  5. Plan Letter discipline is the forecast gate. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in the forecast. This is the rule I learned at UKG competing against ADP and Workday for nearly six years in one of enterprise tech's tightest forecast cultures, and it is the rule that produced 10x peer outperformance during my IC year at Series A Ushur. In a Series A AI-biopharma platform with a 24-month runway and a Series B to raise against commercial traction, forecast integrity is not a nice-to-have. It is the artifact that closes the next round.

The three load-bearing initiatives for the first 90 days are Top-10 Pharma Strategic Account Penetration, Platform Licensing and Commercial Architecture, and the Regulated-Procurement Reference Framework. Each is sized, specified, and measured below. Every one of them is anchored in a methodology I have run for 25 years, most recently through $8M ARR and $24M TCV in under two years as the senior IC at Series A Ushur, and through the CalOptima Health (Orange County Health Authority) Medicaid redetermination deal that grew a $249K land into approximately $10M TCV by running CMS-regulated procurement discipline end-to-end.

1. The System I Run

Every plan I build for every client and every employer runs on one integrated method.

Deep Discovery (5 layers). Surface Symptoms become Stated Problems become Operational Impact become Economic Impact become Root Constraint and Unconsidered Need. You do not earn the right to claim a Power Message until you reach Layer 4. Most sellers stop at Layer 2 and then wonder why their forecasts miss. In AI for biopharma, Layer 4 is where the CSO and Chief Digital Officer live: total R&D spend per therapeutic area, attributable cycle-time reduction in hit-to-lead or lead optimization, probability-of-success lift at candidate nomination, FTE redeployment math, cost per IND filing.

Power Messaging. A Power Message links a unique capability to a buyer-specific problem that can be defined in clear economic terms. It emerges from discovery, never from marketing. "[Company]'s foundation model is the best in class" is not a Power Message. "Your oncology portfolio has two programs stalled at the lead-optimization gate with combined sunk cost above $40 million. [Company]'s generative-chemistry capability, paired with our structural-biology validation pipeline, has produced documented lead-optimization cycles of 6 to 9 months versus your current 18-month internal baseline. For these two programs that translates to $22 million in redeployed capital and 9 months of patent-life preservation" is a Power Message.

Two gates before I sell. Gate 1 is Active Evaluation. The pharma is actually in motion, with a named CSO sponsor, a budgeted program, and a procurement path. Gate 2 is Quantified Severity. They can name the dollar cost of the stalled program, the missed IND window, or the portfolio-level trial failure rate. If either gate fails, I do not walk. I widen. I surface a larger use case, follow the economic pain downstream, and tie the solution to an existing forcing function inside the account, a contract renewal, a regulatory deadline, a board-level number. That is how the large national radiology provider became $1.6M ARR from a $30K OCR request: the OCR engagement alone had no active evaluation, so I anchored the deal to the incumbent's contract renewal. The renewal was the forcing function. My solution replaced the incumbent's majority-revenue contract.

Mutual Sequence of Events. Co-created with the buyer. For AI-biopharma this sequence runs across CSO sponsorship, Head of Discovery technical validation, VP R&D portfolio fit review, Chief Digital Officer and IT security review, legal and IP terms, procurement, and executive steering committee approval. The sequence ends beyond signature, at first productive molecule delivery or first joint publication milestone, because that is when the buyer actually sees the outcome they bought. That is how we never get caught selling.

The Plan Letter. Sent within 24 hours of every substantive conversation. It restates value in the buyer's economic language, delivers every commitment made in the meeting, lays out the complete timeline to first deliverable, and asks the buyer to confirm in writing. If they write back yes, the deal belongs in the forecast. If they rewrite the sequence, that is better data and the deal belongs in the forecast with updated milestones. If they go silent, they are not in the forecast.

The forecast rule. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in the forecast. I learned this at UKG, where the culture enforced it without exception. My manager's line was "great job on the forecast. Show me the plan letter." That rule made forecasts defensible by construction, not by hope. It is what kept my number real year after year and what produced top-5-percent-globally ranking for nearly six years.

The forcing function. If I cannot write a good Plan Letter after a call, I ran a bad meeting. Period. So, run better meetings. That discipline is how I coach sellers without sitting in every one of their meetings.

Every initiative below exists to make Plan Letters writable and forecasts accurate. That is the whole point.

4. The Three Problems I Solve at [Company]

Problem 1: Make the CSO-to-Procurement buying journey defensible

Pharma enterprise AI deals die at the gates, not at the demo. A CSO can sponsor [Company] all day. If the Chief Digital Officer has not seen the security and IP architecture, if VP R&D has not confirmed portfolio fit, if procurement has not been given a commercial-terms template it can defend internally, the deal stalls the last 30 days of a 12-month cycle. Every [Company] opportunity must have all five gates mapped, frontloaded, and buyer-validated in writing before week 8 of any pharma engagement. Initiative 3 codifies the playbook.

Problem 2: Close the platform licensing and commercial architecture gap

A Series A AI-biopharma platform without a codified three-tier commercial architecture prices every deal off the last deal. That is how $500K pilots become the ceiling instead of the floor. The reference architectures are Isomorphic-Lilly $1.75B and Recursion-Bayer $1.5B. Neither was negotiated from zero. Each was built against a commercial template with a milestone tree and a royalty schedule the CRO had pre-cleared with legal and finance. [Company] must have its own three-tier template (Platform Access, Co-Discovery, Strategic Partnership) by day 60. Initiative 2 builds it.

Problem 3: Convert scientific validation into procurement-grade references

[Company]'s scientific credibility lives in published papers and conference posters. Pharma procurement does not buy papers. It buys reference customers, contract templates, security artifacts, and IP clearance. The CRO's job is to translate scientific validation into a reference framework procurement can defend. This includes named-pharma reference calls, documented cycle-time and POS economics per engagement, SOC 2 Type II plus pharma-grade security artifacts, and IP assignment structures that survive pharma legal review. Initiative 1 uses real account work to build these artifacts deal by deal.

5.3 Who buys at [Company]

Economic buyer. Depends on deal size. For $5M+ annualized commitments: Chief Scientific Officer with Board-level awareness, CFO sign-off, sometimes CEO signature. For smaller $500K to $2M engagements: Head of Discovery or VP R&D with CSO approval.

Technical champion. Head of Discovery, VP R&D for the target therapeutic area, Head of AI/ML within R&D, sometimes an SVP leading a specific therapeutic franchise.

User. Medicinal chemists, computational biologists, structural biologists, and data scientists inside the pharma R&D organization. They are not the buyer but their adoption criteria shape the technical validation.

Blocker. Chief Digital Officer and IT security (data residency, IP leakage, model provenance), Chief Legal Officer and IP counsel (IP assignment, background IP rights), procurement (commercial terms, supplier diversification mandate), internal AI/ML leadership (build-vs-buy veto).

5.4 Trigger events that open active evaluation

These are the real signals every BDR, AE, and marketing team should hunt for daily.

  1. New CSO, Chief Digital Officer, Head of AI/ML in R&D, or Head of Discovery hire in the last 180 days.
  2. Public earnings-call reference to AI in R&D or to pipeline productivity pressure.
  3. Disclosed strategic AI partnership with a competitor (signal that pharma is in the mode and likely to add a second).
  4. Pipeline setback or phase-3 failure with visible portfolio impact (accelerates AI adoption).
  5. Patent-cliff exposure within 24 to 36 months (LOE pressure drives discovery acceleration budgets).
  6. M&A event creating duplicate R&D platforms (consolidation procurement window).
  7. Board-level AI strategy refresh announced (follows most new-CEO transitions).
  8. JP Morgan Healthcare Conference statement on AI R&D priorities.
  9. New CDO hire specifically into R&D (separate from enterprise IT CDO).
  10. CRO/CDMO announcement of AI-forward partnership (opens channel conversation).

Trigger events are the opening. The product pitch is never the opener.

The CalOptima Health Medicaid redetermination deal: live proof of regulated-procurement discipline

The discipline above is not theoretical. The following is how I ran regulated-procurement discipline end-to-end at CalOptima Health during Medicaid redetermination, and the pattern maps one-for-one to pharma enterprise procurement.

CalOptima Health is a CMS-regulated Medicaid managed-care plan covering more than 900,000 members in Orange County. Medicaid redetermination (the post-COVID continuous-enrollment unwind) created a compliance-urgent procurement window in 2023-2024 with a multi-vendor tech stack, a strict CMS-aligned timeline, and a procurement committee reporting to both the CEO and the Board.

I ran the five-layer discovery.

  • Layer 1 (surface symptom): member-outreach volume overload during redetermination.
  • Layer 2 (stated problem): existing IVR and call-center capacity could not scale to redetermination-window traffic.
  • Layer 3 (operational impact): redetermination deadlines risked member-eligibility gaps, which would produce CMS-level compliance exposure and member churn.
  • Layer 4 (economic impact): unmanaged redetermination churn translated directly to lost capitated revenue, CMS compliance findings, and STAR-rating impact on the Medicaid Medicare dual-eligible book. Quantified at multi-million-dollar annualized exposure.
  • Layer 5 (root constraint, unconsidered need): the real constraint was not outreach capacity. It was the absence of an end-to-end orchestrated member-engagement platform that could sequence outreach, capture consent, route to eligibility case workers, and produce CMS-auditable records at scale. Redetermination was the trigger. The capability gap would persist long after redetermination ended.

I reframed the procurement from "redetermination outreach vendor" to "member engagement platform with CMS-auditable orchestration." The buying committee had to expand: CMO, Chief Compliance Officer, and Chief Operating Officer joined the procurement leader at the table. Mutual Sequence of Events was co-authored across all five gates (clinical, compliance, security, IT, procurement) and frontloaded into the evaluation phase. Partner orchestration (IVR, translation, case-worker routing, CRM integration) was engineered as a single commercial wrapper so procurement had one contract to defend.

The result: $249K initial threshold-entry land became approximately $10M TCV over the contract horizon. The land size passed the procurement-threshold gate without triggering a full competitive RFP. The expansion landed through the orchestrated-partner structure on a timeline the buyer had already validated in writing.

Why this maps to pharma. Same pattern:

  • CMS-regulated procurement maps to FDA-regulated discovery and IND governance.
  • Multi-year capitated contract structure maps to multi-year milestone-plus-royalty platform partnership.
  • Partner orchestration (IVR + case-worker + CRM) maps to CRO/CDMO orchestration (IQVIA or Parexel integration inside the [Company] deal).
  • Five-gate approval (CEO, Board, CMO, Compliance, Procurement) maps to five-gate pharma approval (CSO, VP R&D, CDO, CLO, Procurement).
  • Threshold-entry land sized below the full-RFP trigger maps to Platform Access tier below the Co-Discovery or Strategic Partnership threshold.

That is what the [Company] Regulated-Procurement Reference Framework will do at every qualified Tier-1 deal. The industry is different. The muscle is the same. The outcome is a repeatable compression of a 12-to-18-month pharma procurement cycle into a buyer-validated Mutual Sequence of Events with frontloaded gate clearance.

Weeks 9-12: Deploy and enable. Train every AE on the framework. Use it in every new Tier-1 engagement starting week 9. Measure cycle time, gate-clearance velocity, and first-year ACV before and after. Iterate.

13. Integration with AI in the GTM Model

A CRO at an AI-for-biopharma platform must use AI in the commercial engine with discipline. Performative AI-in-sales erodes credibility with the same CSOs you are trying to sell. My approach is specific and accountable.

13.1 Where AI compounds revenue

  • Trigger-event detection. Continuous monitoring of pharma 10-K and 10-Q disclosures, investor-day transcripts, earnings-call language, patent-cliff data, CDO and CSO LinkedIn transitions, conference abstracts, and AI-partnership press releases. Surface named trigger events to BDRs within 24 hours.
  • Pre-call research synthesis. Every AE gets a one-page research brief on each Tier-1 account, auto-generated from public sources, validated by the Scientific Liaison. Saves 2 to 3 hours per Tier-1 call preparation.
  • Plan Letter drafting. AE or Scientific Liaison provides the call summary; AI drafts the Plan Letter in the buyer's scientific and economic language using the standard structure. AE validates, edits, sends within 24 hours.
  • Pipeline and cycle-time pattern recognition. Pattern-match open Tier-1 deals against closed-won and closed-lost patterns by gate. Flag deals at risk of slipping at the CDO or procurement gate early enough to intervene.
  • Competitive intelligence synthesis. Continuous monitoring of Recursion, Isomorphic, Insitro, Xaira, Iambic, Genesis disclosed deals and SEC filings. Deal-value and structure signals feed Initiative 2 pricing refinement quarterly.

13.2 Where AI does not belong

  • Final Plan Letter approval. The seller owns the relationship; the seller owns the letter.
  • Pricing and milestone approval. Discount ceilings and structure are CFO, CEO, and General Counsel decisions.
  • Scientific interpretation. [Company]'s scientific leadership owns this.
  • Direct customer interaction (outbound email at scale with no human review). A CSO who gets a templated pitch from an AI-for-biopharma company remembers it for years.

Guiding principle. AI is speed. Humans are accountability. The CRO owns where that line sits, and it sits in a specific place that can be defended in a board meeting and in a Tier-1 pharma reference call.

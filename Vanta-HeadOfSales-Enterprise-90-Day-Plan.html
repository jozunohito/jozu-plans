1. The System I Run

Every plan I build for every client and every employer runs on one integrated method.

Deep Discovery (5 levels). Surface Symptoms become Stated Problems become Operational Impact become Economic Impact become Root Constraint and Unconsidered Need. You do not earn the right to claim a Power Message until you reach Level 4. Most sellers stop at Level 2 and then wonder why their forecasts miss. In enterprise compliance, Level 4 is where the CFO and general counsel live: DORA penalty exposure up to 2 percent of global turnover, SEC cyber disclosure liability, NY DFS Part 500 Second Amendment certification risk, IPO audit delay cost per quarter, TPRM audit cycle time, FTE load on evidence collection, and the dollar cost of a failed SOC 2 Type II that blocks a renewal.

Power Messaging. A Power Message links a unique capability to a buyer-specific problem defined in clear economic terms. It emerges from discovery, never from marketing. "Vanta automates compliance" is not a Power Message. "Your TPRM program runs 600 vendor reviews a year with a 14-day median cycle. Vanta's TPRM Agent compresses median cycle time by up to 81 percent, which on your volume produces approximately 35 FTE-weeks of reclaimed engineering and security time per quarter, and closes the audit-finding window on vendor tiering that your CFO flagged in the last SEC cyber disclosure review" is a Power Message.

Two gates before I sell. Gate 1 is Active Evaluation. The buyer is in motion, with a stakeholder, a timeline, and a procurement path. Gate 2 is Quantified Severity. They can name the dollar cost of doing nothing. If either gate fails, I do not walk. I widen. I surface a larger use case, follow the economic pain downstream, and tie the solution to an existing forcing function inside the account, a contract renewal, a regulatory deadline, a board-level number. That is how the large national radiology provider became $1.6M ARR from a $30K OCR request: the OCR engagement alone had no active evaluation, so I anchored the deal to the incumbent's contract renewal. The renewal was the forcing function. My solution replaced the incumbent's majority-revenue contract.

Mutual Sequence of Events (nine steps). Co-created with the buyer. Evaluation phase: problem severity validation, success criteria, demo aligned to use case, stakeholder expansion, security and compliance review. Execution phase: commercial structuring, executive approval, contract execution, implementation plan. Every step is validated in writing before the next one runs. This is the sequence that closes procurement, legal, and infosec-committee-governed deals.

The Plan Letter. Sent within 24 hours of every substantive conversation. It restates value in the buyer's economic language, delivers every commitment made in the meeting, lays out the complete timeline to go-live, and asks the buyer to confirm in writing. If they write back yes, the deal belongs in the forecast. If they rewrite the sequence, that is better data and the deal belongs in the forecast with updated milestones. If they go silent, they are not in the forecast.

The forecast rule. No buyer-confirmed Plan Letter, no deal in the forecast. I learned this at UKG, where the culture enforced it without exception. That rule made forecasts defensible by construction, not by hope. It is what produced my top 5 percent globally ranking for 5.5 years and a senior IC run at Series A Ushur delivering $8M ARR and $24M TCV in under two years.

The forcing function. If I cannot write a good Plan Letter after a call, I ran a bad meeting. That discipline is how I coach sellers without sitting in every one of their meetings.

Every initiative below exists to make Plan Letters writable and forecasts accurate. That is the whole point.

2.3 Regulatory forcing functions (the timing is right now)

Five regulatory vectors converge in the next 12 to 24 months. Each is a discrete procurement-motion trigger.

  • NY DFS Part 500 Second Amendment. Expanded governance, annual certification by senior officers, CISO board reporting.

Implication for the sell. Every one of these is a "Why Now" trigger that a disciplined enterprise seller can convert into a Plan Letter within one meeting. 4. The Three Problems I Solve at Vanta

Problem 1: Convert SOC 2 leadership into enterprise multi-framework dominance

Vanta owns SOC 2 automation. The enterprise buyer does not buy SOC 2. The enterprise buyer buys a multi-framework, multi-entity, board-reportable trust program that covers SOC 2, ISO 27001, ISO 42001, HIPAA, PCI, GDPR, NIST CSF 2.0, DORA, NIS 2, EU AI Act, SEC 106, and NY DFS. Every Enterprise seller must carry a repeatable multi-framework Power Message in every deal. The posted JD is explicit: "lead multi-product selling as the platform expands into security, compliance, and privacy." That is this problem.

The economic anchors for enterprise trust programs are known:

  • DORA penalty exposure. Up to 2 percent of global turnover per finding. Dollarize against the prospect's own revenue.
  • SEC cyber disclosure cost. 8-K incident disclosure cycle time, 10-K program posture documentation load, audit committee reporting cadence.
  • Audit cycle compression. Evidence collection FTE load reduction, audit duration reduction, control failure reduction.
  • TPRM cycle time. 81 percent faster vendor review on Vanta's TPRM Agent, translated into FTE-weeks and audit finding closure time.
  • Certification-attached revenue. Deals in the prospect's own pipeline that stall or lose when the prospect cannot show current SOC 2, ISO, or HITRUST.
  • M&A and IPO readiness premium. The direct link between documented trust program maturity and valuation.

You already have these numbers in your customer base. The challenge is not knowing the anchors exist. It is making every enterprise seller translate every prospect's situation into those numbers in every deal. That is the discipline I install in Initiative 1.

Problem 2: Build the enterprise segment as a dedicated go-to-market, not an extension of mid-market

The posted JD calls for "long-term enterprise strategy including account segmentation and territory design" and "scalable and leveraged go-to-market motions." The structural differences between mid-market and enterprise at Vanta's scale:

  • Procurement cycle: 120 to 180 days in mid-market, versus 240 to 540 days in enterprise.
  • Buyers: CISO, CIO, general counsel, DPO, chief compliance officer, procurement, audit committee. Plural stakeholders in parallel, not a single decision maker.
  • Contract structure: multi-year, multi-entity, multi-framework, with subsidiary-level rollout, custom DPA terms, and complex renewal mechanics.
  • Competitive surface: Archer, ServiceNow GRC, OneTrust, MetricStream, Drata all show up. Never a solo shortlist.
  • Support posture: named customer success, named security engineer, and audit-liaison coverage, not shared pods.

Each of these requires dedicated muscle. Initiative 2 builds it.

Problem 3: Turn the partner and GSI ecosystem into the primary enterprise leverage vector

The JD specifies "leveraged go-to-market motions incorporating channel partners and global system integrators." Enterprise compliance sales are won on the back of auditors, Big Four advisory, regional audit firms, hyperscaler marketplaces (AWS, Azure, GCP), and the named systems-integrator set (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG, Accenture, IBM, Wipro, TCS, Infosys). Every enterprise compliance procurement involves one or more of these parties. Vanta either arrives with them or against them. Arriving with them compresses cycles by 30 to 50 percent and expands average deal size. Initiative 3 is the operationalization.

Economic buyer. CISO with CFO approval in most cases. In regulated industries, often chief compliance officer or general counsel with CFO. In IPO-track privates, CFO directly.

User. Compliance analysts, security engineers, audit-liaison staff, procurement analysts running vendor reviews.

Blocker. Procurement (vendor consolidation mandates, standard terms), legal (DPA, SCC, contractual indemnities), infosec committee (competitive incumbent preference), IT (ServiceNow consolidation mandate).

5.4 What triggers active evaluation

These are the real signals every enterprise BDR, AE, and marketing team should hunt for daily.

  1. New CISO, CIO, chief compliance officer, or general counsel hire in the last 90 days.
  2. Public 10-K disclosure referencing material weakness in cyber controls, or SEC 8-K cyber incident filing.
  3. Audit finding from Big Four or regional audit partner referencing TPRM, evidence lifecycle, or control posture gaps.
  4. DORA critical ICT third-party designation (November 2025 onward).
  5. EU AI Act high-risk system inventory started (directly observable via job postings for "AI governance" or "responsible AI").
  6. IPO filing or amended S-1 reference to cyber or compliance posture.
  7. Public layoffs or restructuring impacting GRC or security FTE headcount (automation is the answer).
  8. Competitor disclosure (Archer, OneTrust, MetricStream) of renewal cycle or re-RFP activity at the prospect.
  9. Acquisition or carve-out creating multi-entity compliance consolidation need.
  10. Board cybersecurity committee charter update or first-ever appointment of a board cyber expert.
  11. ISO 27001:2022 transition deadline miss (October 31, 2025 passed) creating re-certification urgency.
  12. Public penetration test disclosure or breach notification creating board pressure.

Trigger events are the opening. The product pitch is never the opener.

8. Pricing and Packaging Thesis

Vanta's enterprise pricing needs to do four things: reward multi-framework attach, reward multi-entity expansion, protect the core SOC 2 business from feature commoditization, and align the agent products to value realization rather than seat or asset count.

8.1 Multi-framework attach pricing

Most mid-market compliance automation pricing is per-framework. That model breaks at enterprise because the enterprise buyer has 6 to 12 active frameworks, often simultaneously. The enterprise package should lead with a framework-bundle price (e.g., SOC 2 + ISO 27001 + HIPAA + PCI + GDPR + NIST CSF 2.0) and price additional frameworks (DORA, NIS 2, EU AI Act, ISO 42001, FedRAMP) as delta uplifts, not standalone SKUs. This matches how the buyer actually constructs the trust program.

8.2 Multi-entity pricing

Enterprise customers have parent plus subsidiaries plus acquired entities. Current per-entity pricing at most compliance vendors penalizes exactly the multi-entity rollout that Vanta should be reinforcing. The enterprise package should include three to five entities in the base and price additional entities at a declining marginal cost, encouraging rollout rather than slowing it.

8.3 TPRM Agent and Compliance Agent pricing

The Agentic Trust Platform products are value-realization products, not seat products. Pricing should be anchored to the unit of value: vendor-reviews-per-year (TPRM Agent), evidence-items-automated (Compliance Agent), assessment-volume (privacy automation). Not per-user. Per-user pricing commoditizes the agent story and invites Drata to undercut.

8.4 Enterprise commercial construct

  • Three-year term default. Year one baseline, year two uplift (5 to 7 percent), year three uplift (additional 5 to 7 percent). Multi-year accelerator for the AE.
  • Opt-in agent attach. TPRM Agent or Compliance Agent priced as an add-on with a documented ROI calculator at signature. Attach converts at renewal.
  • Strategic-account custom construct. Global 2000 accounts get a custom enterprise license agreement with multi-entity, multi-framework, and agent-inclusive pricing under a negotiated ceiling.
  • Default discount ceilings. 10 percent single-year, 15 percent multi-year. Anything above requires CRO or CEO sign-off.

8.5 What this is not

  • Not a pricing reset. The mid-market engine is working and should not be disturbed.
  • Not a seat-based model. Seats commoditize agent value.
  • Not a free trial for enterprise. Free trials are a mid-market motion. Enterprise trials run through Proof-of-Value engagements with mutual success criteria in writing.

13. Integration with AI in the GTM Model

The posted JD calls for "openness to leveraging AI tools responsibly." Vanta runs the Agentic Trust Platform externally. The enterprise GTM motion should run internally on the same philosophy.

13.1 Where I use AI in the enterprise GTM model

  • Account intelligence. AI-assisted enrichment on the 300 named accounts. 10-K cyber disclosure extraction, CISO and general counsel mapping, LinkedIn posture monitoring, regulatory-filing trigger detection.
  • Power Message generation. AI-assisted drafting of account-specific Power Messages using the battle-card templates, validated by the AE before it reaches a human buyer.
  • Plan Letter drafting. AE drafts Plan Letter after the call; AI validates against the buyer's stated severity math and flags gaps before the Plan Letter is sent.
  • Forecast anomaly detection. AI flags deals in the forecast without a Plan Letter, without a Mutual Sequence of Events, or without a quantified-severity number. Inspection focus follows the flags.
  • Competitive intelligence. AI-assisted monitoring of Drata, Secureframe, Thoropass, Archer, OneTrust, MetricStream disclosures, pricing leaks, and win-loss signals.
  • Deal reviews. AI-assisted synthesis of call recordings, transcripts, and emails into a deal-level summary before the weekly review.

13.2 Where I do not use AI

  • First meeting with a named economic buyer. Human discovery, no exceptions.
  • Plan Letter sign-off. Human judgment on the final version before sending.
  • Mutual Sequence of Events co-authoring. Human-to-human.
  • Executive sponsorship conversations. Senior human contact.

13.3 The discipline

AI is a force multiplier on a disciplined human system. It is not a substitute for one. Every AI use above accelerates a human activity that is already part of the system. None of them replace the human judgment at the buyer-facing moment. That philosophy is also how Vanta sells the Agentic Trust Platform externally, which means the enterprise segment should model it internally.
